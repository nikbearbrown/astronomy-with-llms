# The Sun: A Garden-Variety Star

## Three possible titles

1. **The Sun: A Garden-Variety Star**
2. **What the Sun Actually Is—and Why That Changes Everything**
3. **The Sun in Close: A Star's Interior, Its Fury, and Its Reach**

---

## TL;DR

The Sun is not the stable, unchanging thing ancient astronomers imagined. It is a roiling ball of ionized hydrogen and helium held together by gravity, inside which hydrogen fuses to helium at 15 million Kelvin. That fusion radiates outward so slowly through the dense interior that a photon takes a hundred thousand years to reach the surface. Once it does, we see the photosphere—a thin, convective layer where the light finally escapes. Above that sits an atmosphere that does not cool as you move outward, as one would expect; instead, the temperature climbs from 6,000 K to a million. The Sun's magnetic field drives everything dramatic that happens above the surface: sunspots, flares, coronal mass ejections. That field is generated by churning plasma in the convection zone, and it flips every 11 years or so. Particles streaming from the Sun's corona—the solar wind—have traveled silently through space for 400 million years, and when they hit Earth, they write aurora in the sky and sometimes black out power grids. The Sun is a machine of sublime elegance and brutal indifference.

## 1. Opening: The Carrington Event, September 1859

Richard Carrington was observing sunspots on September 1, 1859, when he saw something that would occupy him for the rest of his life. Two bright points of light erupted on the solar surface—there one moment, transforming the chromosphere, then fading. He had witnessed a solar flare, though the term did not yet exist and he could not know what it meant.

A day or so later, something arrived at Earth. It was not visible. It made no sound. It carried no weight that a scale could measure. But it was there, moving at nearly the speed of light, riding the solar wind: a cascade of charged particles magnetized by the Sun's own disrupted field.

At telegraph offices across North America, operators watched their wires—the new technology that bound the young nation together—begin to spark. Some wires burned their hands. Others watched arcs of flame leap between the machines. In London and across Europe, the same chaos unfolded. Telegraph systems that had worked reliably for years simply stopped. The signals could not get through.

And in the night sky, something else happened that people could see. The aurora, which normally glowed only near the poles, burned visible in Hawaii. In the Caribbean, people woke at 2 a.m. thinking it was dawn. The entire night sky was alight with curtains of red and green. For the first time, scientists could ask: what happens on the Sun that reaches here?

This is the question the chapter asks. Not "How does the Sun work?"—that is the machinery. But "What does the Sun do to us?" That is where the question leads.

## 2. What the Sun Actually Is: Specifying the Concept

For two thousand years, the Sun was thought to be immutable. Unchanging. Perfect. Aristotle said so. Ancient astronomers said so. The Sun was not made of the stuff of Earth—not earth, water, air, or fire in the way we understood them. It was something different, something eternal.

In 1925, a woman named Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin examined the light coming from the Sun through a spectroscope. She measured the dark lines in that spectrum—the wavelengths the Sun's atoms had absorbed. Every element has a signature in light. Hydrogen, helium, carbon, nitrogen, iron. She did the calculation three times. Each time, the same answer: the Sun is 73% hydrogen by mass and 25% helium. Everything else—carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, the iron in blood, the calcium in bone—all of it makes up 2%.

This answer was so unexpected that she doubted herself. She wrote: "The enormous abundance derived for these elements in the stellar atmosphere is almost certainly not real." Even she did not believe her own data. But she was right. The Sun is not like Earth. It is not mostly rock and metal and trace gases. It is almost entirely the simplest elements there are.

This matters because it tells us what the Sun *is*. It is not a ball of burning coal, as nineteenth-century scientists had guessed. It is a ball of ionized gas—plasma—held together by its own gravity. Hydrogen atoms in the Sun are so hot that their electrons have been stripped away, leaving behind positively charged nuclei surrounded by a sea of free electrons. That is what plasma is: a soup of charged particles, electrically conducting, responsive to magnetic fields. Most of the universe is plasma. We live in the exception—a cool corner of space where matter is mostly neutral.

The Sun, at its visible surface, has a temperature of about 5,800 Kelvin. That is where the light escapes. But beneath that surface, it grows hotter as you descend. At the core, where hydrogen fuses to helium, the temperature reaches 15 million Kelvin. To specify "the Sun's temperature" alone is imprecise. The Sun has no single temperature. It is a gradient—hot in, cool out.

## 3. The Sun's Interior: Three Layers and How Light Gets Out

Imagine a photon born in the core. It has just been released by the nuclear reaction that fused two hydrogen nuclei into one helium nucleus. The photon is energetic, carrying that reaction's released energy. Now it must travel 696,000 kilometers outward to the surface.

But the core is so dense—so packed with atoms—that the photon cannot go far before it collides with an electron or nucleus. The collision sends the photon off in a random direction. It loses a little energy. Then it travels again, collides again, bounces again. This is the radiative zone: a layer so dense that light bounces around randomly, like a person working their way through a crowd, taking two steps forward, getting redirected, taking a step sideways, bumping into someone, starting over. A photon that "should" reach the surface in eight minutes instead takes about 100,000 years.

This is the first trade-off: the Sun's core releases energy rapidly, but gravity's weight has compressed the interior so thoroughly that the energy cannot escape quickly. The Sun burns slowly partly because it is *inside* the Sun—trapped in density.

At about 70% of the way from the core to the surface, the density drops enough that photons can no longer carry energy efficiently. Here, a different mechanism takes over: convection. Hot plasma rises (the hot stuff is less dense), cools as it radiates energy away, becomes denser, and sinks back down. This convective zone is a churning mass, like a pot of boiling oatmeal, but 200,000 kilometers deep. The rising and sinking motions carry heat outward far more efficiently than radiation can.

This convection creates the second trade-off: it is violent and chaotic, mixing the plasma, shearing it, stirring magnetic fields into twisted knots. The Sun's magnetic field is generated here, in the convection zone. Electricity flows. Magnetic loops form. Some of those loops will rise through the photosphere and create the active regions—sunspots, flares, eruptions—that we will return to.

At the surface itself, the photosphere: a relatively thin layer (just over 400 kilometers) where the gas transitions from opaque to transparent. Below this line, photons bounce around and cannot escape. Above it, photons travel freely into space. What we see when we look at the Sun is this photosphere. It looks sharp, but it is not a boundary—there is no surface, no hard edge. It is a gradation, like the edge of a cloud as you fall through it while skydiving. But where the cloud is cool and soft, the photosphere is unimaginably hot.

At the photosphere's surface, the temperature is about 5,800 K. Even cooler sunspots—the dark regions mentioned in the opening—are still 3,800 K. A piece of sunspot material transported to Earth would shine brilliantly. It appears dark only in contrast to the surrounding brilliance.

## 4. The Sun's Atmosphere: A Temperature Puzzle

Above the photosphere sits something that defied explanation for centuries: the Sun's atmosphere gets *hotter*, not cooler, as you move away from the heat source.

Just above the photosphere is the chromosphere. Its name comes from its color—chromos is Greek for "color." During total solar eclipses, when the bright photosphere is blocked by the Moon, the chromosphere appears as a thin red line. That red is hydrogen-alpha light, the emission line of hydrogen at a specific wavelength (656 nanometers). The chromosphere is about 2,000–3,000 kilometers thick and has a temperature of roughly 10,000 K.

But the real surprise sits above that: the corona. Its name comes from the crown it appears to wear around the Sun during eclipses. The corona is extremely hot—between one and three million Kelvin. This should be impossible. You are moving *away* from the source of heat, and the temperature is climbing one hundred-fold.

Between the chromosphere and the corona sits a razor-thin transition region—just a few tens of kilometers thick—where the temperature rises from 10,000 K to a million. The physics of how and why this happens is still debated. The solar magnetic field is certainly involved. Waves propagating upward from the convection zone probably deposit energy. But the full mechanism remains mysterious—one of the remaining unsolved problems in solar physics.

Here is the trade-off that matters: we cannot simply explain the Sun's atmosphere by scaling principles from the photosphere. The Sun is not a simple machine where energy flows outward and temperature steadily falls. The atmosphere has its own physics, driven by magnetic fields and waves and processes we are still working to understand.

What we do know is that the corona is extremely tenuous—the lowest densities we can routinely achieve in laboratory vacuums. The corona has only about a billion atoms per cubic centimeter, compared to the billion-billion molecules in Earth's air at sea level. The corona is held to the Sun by magnetic force, not by gravity. The magnetic field acts like a cage, containing and shaping the hot gas.

## 5. The Sun's Magnetic Field and the Sunspot Cycle: How Magnetism Drives the Drama

The sunspot cycle is the heartbeat of the Sun, and it is driven by magnetism.

Sunspots appear as dark spots on the photosphere—typically 3,800 K compared to 5,800 K around them. They are large (often bigger than Earth), they come and go, and they occur in groups. Galileo, observing them through one of the first telescopes in 1612, realized they proved the Sun rotates. By watching their apparent motion across the solar disk, he calculated the rotation period: about 25 days at the equator.

The Sun does not rotate like a solid body. Different latitudes rotate at different speeds—the equator completes a turn in 25 days, but the poles take 36 days. This *differential rotation* is crucial to understanding the sunspot cycle.

In the 1820s, an amateur astronomer and pharmacist named Heinrich Schwabe decided to look for a planet inside Mercury's orbit. He kept meticulous daily records of sunspot numbers, hoping to spot the planet's shadow. He failed to find the planet, but found something more important: the number of visible sunspots follows a cycle. Sometimes more than 100 spots appear. Sometimes none do. The cycle averages 11 years.

Why does this cycle exist? The answer is magnetism.

The Sun's magnetic field can be measured using an effect called the Zeeman effect. In a strong magnetic field, the energy levels of atoms split into multiple closely-spaced levels. This splitting causes spectral lines to split into multiple lines instead of remaining as single sharp lines. By measuring this splitting in light from sunspots, we can measure the magnetic field strength. Sunspots have very strong fields—thousands of times Earth's magnetic field.

Here is how the cycle works: The Sun's magnetic field is generated by the dynamo effect. In the convection zone, churning plasma creates electric currents. Moving electric charges generate magnetic fields. The dynamo operates like this: the convection zone generates a magnetic field. Differential rotation winds that field up like a rubber band being twisted. The field grows stronger and stronger, creating knots and loops. When the field becomes strong enough, it forms loops that rise through the photosphere. Where a loop reaches the surface, it creates a pair of sunspots—one end with north polarity, the other with south polarity.

Sunspots are dark because the magnetic field that creates them resists the convection underneath. Hot gas wants to bubble upward. The magnetic field holds it down. So the region above the sunspot is cooler and darker than the surrounding photosphere. The magnetic field literally suppresses the heat.

As the sunspot field emerges, rises, and eventually breaks apart and decays, the magnetic field re-seeds itself. The field regenerates with the *opposite* polarity. This takes about 11 years on average—the sunspot cycle.

But here is the full story: the magnetic polarity flips from one hemisphere to the other every 11 years. During one cycle, the leading sunspots in the Northern Hemisphere all have north polarity, while leading spots in the Southern Hemisphere have south polarity. In the next cycle, it reverses. So the *true* cycle—when the system returns to its original state—takes 22 years, not 11.

The trade-off: the sunspot cycle is regular enough to predict on long timescales, but irregular enough that we cannot predict individual flares or their timing. We see the cycle. We understand the mechanism in outline. But the Sun still surprises us.

## 6. The Active Sun: Flares, Prominences, and Coronal Mass Ejections

The sunspot cycle is the baseline. It is the background rhythm. But the Sun's real violence happens in the corona.

When the Sun is at sunspot maximum—when the magnetic field is most twisted and concentrated—dramatic events become common. Plages appear (the word is French for "beaches"): bright regions in the chromosphere above sunspots, where gas is hotter and denser. Prominences appear: graceful loops of plasma suspended above the surface by magnetic force, reaching sometimes more than a million kilometers high. Some prominences remain stable for days. Others erupt violently, sending plasma at speeds exceeding 1,300 kilometers per second—faster than almost any natural process on Earth.

And then there are solar flares.

A solar flare is a sudden explosion in the corona, releasing energy equivalent to a million hydrogen bombs. A typical flare lasts 5 to 10 minutes. The largest last hours. The flare heats the surrounding plasma to 10 million Kelvin—hotter than the Sun's core. At that temperature, the plasma emits a flood of X-rays and extreme ultraviolet light that bathes Earth's upper atmosphere and reaches satellites in space.

The cause: magnetic reconnection. Imagine two loops of magnetic field, pointing in opposite directions, rising from the photosphere. They approach each other. The magnetic field resists—opposite poles repel. But the plasma is hot and turbulent. Eventually, the field lines break and reconnect to the opposite loops. When they do, all the energy stored in the magnetic field is released at once—explosively. A stretched rubber band releases energy when it snaps. The Sun's magnetic field releases it as a thermonuclear blast.

Sometimes, a flare is accompanied by a coronal mass ejection (CME). A CME is an enormous bubble of plasma—tens of millions of tons of gas—that erupts from the corona at speeds of 500–1,000 kilometers per second. It travels outward in all directions, carrying the Sun's magnetic field with it. If it is aimed at Earth, it will arrive in two to four days.

These events—flares and CMEs—are not random. They cluster in *active regions*: places on the Sun where sunspots, plages, prominences, and flares all occur together in the same latitude and longitude. Active regions are where the magnetic field is strongest, most twisted, most ready to release energy.

## 7. Space Weather: The Carrington Event and the Science of Solar Wind Impacts

The solar wind is a constant stream of charged particles flowing from the Sun: protons, electrons, alpha particles. The wind moves at about 400 kilometers per second—nearly a million miles per hour. The density is extraordinarily low (about five particles per cubic centimeter by the time the wind reaches Earth), but the Sun has an enormous surface area. The Sun loses about 1–2 million tons of material per second to this wind. Over a billion years, it adds up. But it is negligible compared to the Sun's mass.

The solar wind comes primarily from coronal holes: regions where the Sun's magnetic field opens up and flows away into space without looping back. In the open geometry, plasma can escape unhindered. Where the field is closed and looped back, the plasma is trapped.

When the solar wind reaches Earth, it encounters Earth's magnetic field. The wind compresses the field on the day-side, stretches it on the night-side, creating a cavity called the magnetosphere. The wind particles cannot easily penetrate the field at the equator, but at the north and south magnetic poles, the field lines funnel downward into the atmosphere. Charged particles follow those field lines.

As the particles strike atoms in the upper atmosphere—oxygen and nitrogen—they excite them to higher energy states. When those atoms relax back to their ground state, they emit light. This is the aurora: the northern lights and southern lights. The colors depend on which atom was struck and at what altitude. Oxygen emits green and red. Nitrogen emits blue and purple. The result is curtains of colored light that can be visible from hundreds of kilometers away.

This is the routine solar wind. But when a flare or CME strikes Earth, everything changes.

The Carrington Event of September 1859 is the archetype. The flare released energy equivalent to billions of hydrogen bombs. The CME that followed carried a magnetic field with a particular orientation—aligned to amplify Earth's field rather than dampen it. When it reached Earth, it shocked the magnetosphere. The field lines convulsed. Magnetic energy was converted to kinetic energy. Particles were accelerated to enormous speeds.

The aurora flared visible in Hawaii and the Caribbean—locations that almost never see it. Telegraph wires sparked. The global communication network of the day simply stopped working. Operators reported that the wires became too hot to touch.

If such an event occurred today, the damage would be catastrophic. A 2012 solar storm narrowly missed Earth by about a week. If it had hit, models suggest that power transformers across North America would have failed. Replacing them would take years. The economic cost would exceed a trillion dollars. Hospitals would lose power. Water systems would fail. The event would be comparable to a major war in its consequences.

But there is no way to prevent it. Space weather does not respect borders or plans. We can only prepare and monitor.

## 8. The Sun in the Cosmos: Synthesis and Perspective

The Sun is utterly ordinary. It is a G2V star—a yellow dwarf. Billions of other stars are larger, older, hotter, colder, more massive, less massive. By stellar standards, the Sun is unremarkable.

Yet the Sun is everything to us. Its fusion produces every element heavier than helium that exists in our bodies. The carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, iron—all of it was manufactured in the cores of stars and scattered by supernovae before our solar system formed. We are made of stellar ashes.

The Sun's magnetic field, generated in its convection zone by the turbulent mixing of plasma, drives all the activity we see: sunspots, flares, prominences, the corona. That same field reaches Earth as the solar wind. The wind shapes Earth's magnetosphere, produces the aurora, and sometimes damages our technology. The Sun's cycles—the 11-year sunspot cycle, the 22-year magnetic cycle—are rooted in hydrodynamic processes that we understand in outline but not completely. The corona's heating mechanism remains mysterious. We are still learning.

What the Carrington Event taught us is that the Sun's quiet periods are aberrations. The Sun is fundamentally violent and variable. That we have enjoyed 164 years of relative quiet since Carrington is luck, not design. The solar wind is not an anomaly. The magnetic field's chaos is not exceptional. The Sun's fury is the rule.

And yet, for all its violence and indifference, the Sun is what allows us to exist. Without its fusion, there would be no light, no heat, no chemistry complex enough to build life. Without the solar wind shaping Earth's magnetosphere, unfiltered cosmic rays would sterilize the surface. The Sun simultaneously threatens us and sustains us.

To know the Sun is to know our place in the universe: at the mercy of a machine of sublime physical beauty, whose mechanics we can measure and sometimes understand, whose moods we can learn to predict, but whose force we cannot control.

## What would change my mind

Evidence that the Sun's magnetic field is generated by a mechanism wholly different from the dynamo effect—or that the corona is heated by a process completely independent of magnetic waves—would require rewriting our understanding of solar dynamics.

## Still puzzling

The heating mechanism of the solar corona remains one of the major unsolved problems in solar physics. We know the corona is hot. We know the magnetic field is involved. But how magnetic energy is converted to heat efficiently enough to reach a million Kelvin is not fully understood.
