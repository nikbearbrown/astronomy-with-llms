## Venus

### Figure 1: Magellan Radar Map of Venus Surface

**Source:** NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory / Magellan Mission (1990–1994)

**Resolution:** 100 meters at best; composited from imaging radar reflections

**Shows:**
- Ishtar highland region (north), with Maxwell Mountains rising 11 km above plains
- Aphrodite highland region (east-west across equator), size of Africa
- Lowland lava plains covering 75% of surface
- Corona structures (circular/oval features from subsurface volcanism)
- Ridge and crack patterns (tectonic stress)
- Fresh impact craters with little degradation

**Pedagogical purpose:** Demonstrates that Venus has an active geological history, despite hostile surface conditions. Shows scale (Earth-sized planet) and topographic variation. Illustrates that without erosion (no water, weak winds), crater morphology is preserved, allowing age estimates through crater counts.

**Referenced in:** Section III (Venus geological features)

---

### Figure 2: Venus Atmospheric Structure (Cross-Section)

**Source:** Composite from Venera lander measurements, Pioneer Venus, and Venus Express data

**Shows:**
- Dense CO2 atmosphere extending to ~96 km altitude
- Sulfuric acid cloud layers at 30–60 km altitude (bright white)
- Temperature inversion in troposphere
- Diffuse light reaching surface (red-tinted, equivalent to Earth's overcast)
- Pressure and temperature profile from surface (730 K, 92 bar) to exosphere

**Pedagogical purpose:** Visualizes the scale of Venus's atmosphere (nearly 100 times Earth's mass in a column). Shows why landers fail — extreme pressure and heat. Illustrates how clouds form from sulfuric acid, a byproduct of SO2 + H2O reaction in a dry, hot atmosphere.

**Referenced in:** Section III (runaway greenhouse mechanism)

## Mars

### Figure 3: Mars Global Topographic Map (Laser Ranging Data)

**Source:** Mars Global Surveyor Laser Altimeter (MOLA); NASA/JPL

**Resolution:** Meter-scale precision in elevation; planet-wide coverage

**Shows:**
- Southern highlands (heavily cratered, ancient)
- Northern lowlands (younger, volcanic plains)
- Tharsis bulge: 5 km uplift, the size of North America
- Olympus Mons: 500 km diameter, 20 km tall (three times Earth's tallest mountain)
- Valles Marineris: canyon system extending 5,000 km (quarter way around planet)
- Difference in elevation from highest peak to deepest basin: 31 km

**Pedagogical purpose:** Establishes Mars's geological complexity. The contrast between ancient highlands and young volcanic plains mirrors Earth's continental vs. oceanic crust, but lacks the active plate tectonics. Shows why Mars's geology is interesting despite being currently inactive.

**Referenced in:** Section IV (Mars geological features and history)

---

### Figure 4: Valles Marineris Canyon System (Orbital Mosaic)

**Source:** Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter High Resolution Imaging Science Experiment (HiRISE)

**Resolution:** ~0.3 meters per pixel (enough to see individual boulders)

**Shows:**
- Main canyon: 7 km deep, 100 km wide, 5,000 km long
- Side canyons and branching tributaries
- Layered deposits on canyon floors (evidence of ancient water activity)
- Landslide scars on walls (evidence of erosion by water, then subsidence)
- Dust and sand dunes in canyon bottom

**Pedagogical purpose:** Demonstrates scale and specificity. The Grand Canyon of Earth would fit in one side canyon. Not cut by rivers (Mars is now too dry), but widened by water seeping from springs and undercutting cliffs, leading to collapses. Shows Mars's geological complexity in a single, comprehensible feature.

**Referenced in:** Section IV (Mars canyons and tectonics)

---

### Figure 5: Martian Gullies (High-Resolution Detail)

**Source:** Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, HiRISE camera

**Resolution:** 0.25–0.5 meters per pixel

**Shows:**
- Dark streaks on crater walls and steep slopes (high-latitude regions)
- Seasonal appearance/disappearance of streaks
- Branching pattern consistent with flowing water
- No impact craters superimposed (indicating geological youth)
- Hydrated salts detected spectroscopically along streaks (2015)

**Pedagogical purpose:** Provides evidence that Mars is not entirely static. Water (probably salty) flows on the surface seasonally, under conditions not yet fully understood. Challenges simple "Mars is dead and frozen" narratives.

**Referenced in:** Section IV (current water activity on Mars)

---

### Figure 6: Olympus Mons (3D Topographic Visualization)

**Source:** MOLA data, rendered with vertical exaggeration for clarity

**Shows:**
- Shield volcano with extremely low slopes (typical of low-viscosity basalt)
- Summit caldera (volcanic crater) 40 km across
- Lava flows extending 500 km from summit
- Absence of impact craters (surface age <100 million years, possibly much younger)
- Size comparison: base larger than the state of Missouri

**Pedagogical purpose:** Illustrates that Mars, though currently cold, still possesses young geological features. Olympus Mons may be intermittently active today. Shows that planetary geology is driven by interior heat, which persists longer on larger bodies (Mars's heat will persist for billions of years more).

**Referenced in:** Section IV (Mars volcanoes)

---

### Figure 7: Mars Polar Caps (North Pole, Orbital View)

**Source:** Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, HiRISE

**Shows:**
- North polar cap: water ice, ~1,000 km diameter, ~3 km thick
- Layered deposits surrounding the cap (dust + ice, alternating bands)
- Each band 10–100 meters thick, recording climate cycles
- Low-elevation basin structure (would have been a sea, if oceans existed)

**Pedagogical purpose:** Demonstrates that Mars retains significant water in frozen form. The layered deposits record orbital-driven climate cycles (similar to Earth's ice ages but on shorter timescales, 10–100 kyr). Shows that Mars's climate is dynamic, not static.

**Referenced in:** Section IV (polar caps and climate cycles)

---

### Figure 8: Runoff and Outflow Channels (Orbital Mosaic)

**Source:** Mars Global Surveyor, Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter

**Resolution:** 5–50 meters per pixel (depending on source)

**Shows:**
- Runoff channels: narrow, sinuous, 10–20 km long (small-scale features, hard to see)
- Outflow channels: massive, 10+ km wide, hundreds of kilometers long
- Diverging pattern suggesting catastrophic flooding from permafrost release
- Crater counts indicating age: runoff channels ~4 billion years old, outflow channels ~3 billion years old

**Pedagogical purpose:** Evidence that Mars had substantially different climate in the past. Runoff channels suggest rainfall. Outflow channels suggest catastrophic subsurface water release. Together, they reconstruct a history of water on Mars.

**Referenced in:** Section IV (evidence for ancient water)

---

### Figure 9: Mineral Evidence: Hematite Spheres ("Blueberries")

**Source:** Opportunity Rover microscopic imager; Meridiani Planum

**Size:** ~3 mm diameter

**Shows:**
- Small spheres of hematite (iron oxide mineral)
- Hematite forms only in the presence of water
- Found in ancient lakebed deposits
- Abundance suggests shallow salty lake that evaporated repeatedly

**Pedagogical purpose:** Demonstrates how mineralogy provides a "fingerprint" of past environmental conditions. Even without organic fossils, rocks tell a chemical story. Hematite = water.

**Referenced in:** Section IV (mineralogical evidence for water)

## Comparative Diagrams

### Figure 10: Three Planets Compared (Schematic Cross-Sections)

**Shows:**
- Venus: thick atmosphere, dense core, lost magnetic field
- Earth: moderate atmosphere, active core, strong magnetic field, oceans
- Mars: thin atmosphere, cooled core, lost magnetic field, polar ice caps

**Size:** Relative diameters to scale

**Pedagogical purpose:** One-figure synthesis of the three planets' physical and atmospheric states. Encodes the major differences (escape velocity, magnetic field, atmosphere) in a single visual.

**Referenced in:** Sections II and VII (comparative planetary evolution)

---

### Figure 11: Escape Velocity and Atmospheric Retention (Graph)

**Axes:**
- X: Escape velocity (km/s)
- Y: Fraction of atmospheric gas retained over 4.5 billion years (log scale)

**Data points:**
- Mercury: 4.3 km/s, ~0% retained
- Moon: 2.4 km/s, ~0% retained
- Mars: 5.0 km/s, ~0% (with solar wind effect)
- Venus: 10.4 km/s, ~99% (without runaway greenhouse)
- Earth: 11.2 km/s, ~99%

**Shows:** Exponential relationship. Small differences in escape velocity produce huge differences in atmospheric retention.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Quantifies the relationship between planetary mass (which sets escape velocity) and atmospheric fate. A threshold effect: above ~10 km/s, most atmospheres are retained; below ~5 km/s, escape is inevitable.

**Referenced in:** Section II (escape velocity mechanism)

---

### Figure 12: Runaway Greenhouse Feedback Loop (Diagram)

**Shows:**
1. Extra heat → Increased evaporation
2. More water vapor → Stronger greenhouse effect
3. Higher temperature → Even more evaporation
4. Positive feedback loop (not self-limiting)
5. Eventually: UV photolysis of H2O → H escape → CO2 accumulation
6. Result: Runaway state (high temperature, no water, thick CO2)

**Pedagogical purpose:** Makes explicit the concept of a tipping point. The process does not stabilize; it cascades. Contrasts with Earth's carbonate-silicate cycle, which is self-regulating.

**Referenced in:** Section III (runaway greenhouse mechanism)

## Historical Figures

### Figure 13: Lowell's Mars Drawings (1905–1916)

**Source:** Lowell Observatory archives

**Shows:**
- Hand-drawn maps of Mars's surface as Lowell and his assistants perceived them
- Canals and markings rendered as straight lines
- Global coverage suggesting an engineered network

**Pedagogical purpose:** Historical context. Shows how observational bias and human pattern-recognition can mislead. The "canals" were optical illusions, but they captured the public imagination for decades. Demonstrates the importance of high-resolution, objective data (which came only with spacecraft).

**Referenced in:** Section I (historical context)

---

### Figure 14: Mariner 4 Image of Mars (1965)

**Source:** NASA, Mariner 4 mission

**Shows:**
- First close-up image of Mars's surface
- Impact craters clearly visible
- Gray, crater-pocked terrain
- Resolution: ~1 km per pixel

**Pedagogical purpose:** Landmark image. Resolved the canal controversy once and for all. Made it clear that Mars was a complex, geologically diverse world, not a smooth, engineered sphere.

**Referenced in:** Section I (opening puzzle)
