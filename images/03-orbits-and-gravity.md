# Image Specs: Orbits and Gravity

## Required Figures

### Figure 1: Ellipse with Foci Marked
- **Location:** Concept 1, "The mechanism" section (Kepler's first law)
- **Purpose:** Show geometric definition of ellipse and position of Sun at one focus
- **Elements:**
  - Ellipse (not a circle)
  - Two points marked inside as "Focus 1" and "Focus 2"
  - Sun symbol at one focus
  - Empty space marked at other focus
  - Several points on the ellipse with distance measurements to both foci (to show sum is constant)
  - Labels: major axis, minor axis, semimajor axis, eccentricity
- **Caption:** "An ellipse is defined by two special points (foci) inside it. The sum of distances from any point on the ellipse to the two foci is always the same. In planetary orbits, the Sun sits at one focus and the other focus is empty space. This is why planets are sometimes closer and sometimes farther from the Sun."
- **Type:** Diagram (vector)

### Figure 2: Kepler's Second Law — Equal Areas in Equal Times
- **Location:** Concept 1, "The mechanism" section
- **Purpose:** Visualize that a planet moving faster at perihelion covers the same area in the same time as at aphelion
- **Elements:**
  - Elliptical orbit viewed from above
  - Sun at focus
  - Planet shown at two locations: close to Sun (perihelion) and far from Sun (aphelion)
  - Two wedge-shaped areas highlighted (one near perihelion, one near aphelion)
  - Shading to show equal areas
  - Velocity vectors on planet at both locations (longer at perihelion, shorter at aphelion)
  - Time intervals marked as equal (same duration between positions)
- **Caption:** "Kepler's second law: as a planet orbits, the line connecting planet to Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal time. At perihelion (closer to Sun), the planet moves faster and covers a large arc. At aphelion (farther), the planet moves slower and covers a smaller arc. The areas swept are equal."
- **Type:** Diagram with shading (raster)

### Figure 3: Newton's Mountain
- **Location:** Concept 2, "The cold open" section
- **Purpose:** Illustrate how orbital velocity increases with launching faster; shows path becomes circular orbit
- **Elements:**
  - Very tall mountain on spherical Earth
  - Cannon on mountain top
  - Four trajectories shown (progressively flatter):
    1. Low velocity: projectile falls to Earth nearby
    2. Higher velocity: projectile travels farther before impact
    3. Even higher velocity: trajectory becomes curved, still hits Earth
    4. High enough velocity: trajectory curves at same rate as Earth; projectile orbits
  - Curved surface of Earth shown throughout
  - Labels for each trajectory
  - Labels for "gravity pulls down," "velocity carries forward," and "orbit achieved"
- **Caption:** "Newton's insight: if a projectile is fired horizontally fast enough, Earth's curvature drops away as quickly as gravity pulls the projectile down. The projectile falls around Earth, tracing an orbit. This unifies falling objects and orbiting objects under the same force."
- **Type:** Diagram (vector with curved lines)

### Figure 4: Gravitational Force vs. Distance (Inverse-Square Law)
- **Location:** Concept 2, "The mechanism" section
- **Purpose:** Show how gravitational force decreases with the inverse square of distance
- **Elements:**
  - Two massive objects (represented as spheres or schematic masses)
  - Force arrows showing direction and relative magnitude at different distances
  - Distance labels (1 unit, 2 units, 3 units, etc.)
  - Force strength labels (F, F/4, F/9, etc.)
  - Graph below: vertical axis = force magnitude, horizontal axis = distance
    - Hyperbolic curve showing 1/r^2 relationship
- **Caption:** "Newton's law of universal gravitation: the force between two objects drops off as the inverse square of the distance. Double the distance, and the force becomes one-quarter as strong. This is one of the most important relationships in physics."
- **Type:** Diagram with inset graph (vector)

### Figure 5: Orbital Velocity vs. Altitude (Circular Orbits)
- **Location:** Concept 3, "The mechanism" section
- **Purpose:** Show how orbital velocity decreases with increasing altitude around Earth
- **Elements:**
  - Earth sphere (to scale or schematic)
  - Several circular orbits at different altitudes (near surface, ISS height, geostationary, etc.)
  - Velocity vectors on satellites at each altitude (longer vectors for closer orbits)
  - Labels showing altitude and corresponding orbital velocity
  - Optional: orbital period labels (90 min, 24 hours, etc.)
  - Escape velocity shown as threshold beyond which orbit becomes parabolic/hyperbolic
- **Caption:** "As satellites orbit at greater altitudes, their orbital velocity decreases because gravitational force weakens with distance. The International Space Station at 400 km altitude orbits at 7.7 km/s with a period of 92 minutes. Geostationary satellites at 36,000 km orbit once per day."
- **Type:** Diagram (vector)

### Figure 6: Elliptical Orbit with Perihelion and Aphelion
- **Location:** Concept 3, "The mechanism" section
- **Purpose:** Show characteristics of elliptical orbits and relationship to circular orbits
- **Elements:**
  - Ellipse representing orbit
  - Sun at focus
  - Planet at perihelion (closest point) with label and velocity vector (longer)
  - Planet at aphelion (farthest point) with label and velocity vector (shorter)
  - Distance labels: r_p and r_a
  - Velocity labels: v_p and v_a
  - Eccentricity value shown
  - Optional: energy diagram showing kinetic and potential energy at different points
- **Caption:** "In an elliptical orbit, a planet moves fastest at perihelion (closest to Sun) and slowest at aphelion (farthest). The product of distance and velocity remains constant, conserving angular momentum."
- **Type:** Diagram (vector)

### Figure 7: Escape Velocity vs. Orbital Velocity
- **Location:** Concept 3, "The trade-off" section
- **Purpose:** Show relationship between orbital velocity and escape velocity; energy perspective
- **Elements:**
  - Earth (schematic or to scale)
  - Three satellite trajectories:
    1. Circular orbit (v = v_orbital)
    2. Elliptical orbit with higher aphelion (v between v_orbital and v_escape)
    3. Parabolic trajectory toward escape (v = v_escape)
    4. Hyperbolic trajectory (v > v_escape)
  - Velocity vectors at launch point for each
  - Energy diagram: kinetic energy vs. potential energy at different velocities
  - Labels showing v_escape = √2 × v_orbital
- **Caption:** "Escape velocity is √2 times the circular orbital velocity at the same altitude. Objects at escape velocity reach infinite distance with zero velocity. Faster than that, and they coast away with leftover speed. Slower, and they fall back."
- **Type:** Diagram with inset energy graph (vector)

### Figure 8: Discovery of Neptune
- **Location:** Concept 4, "The cold open" section
- **Purpose:** Historical illustration of how orbital perturbations led to discovery of unseen planet
- **Elements:**
  - Uranus's orbit (with perturbations exaggerated for visibility)
  - Predicted path from Newton's laws (shown as one line)
  - Actual observed path of Uranus (deviations marked)
  - Diagram showing direction of perturbation force
  - Neptune's location at discovery marked
  - Predicted location marked (with 1-degree accuracy notation)
  - Timeline: 1781 (Herschel discovers Uranus), 1846 (Neptune found)
  - Portraits of Adams and Le Verrier (optional)
- **Caption:** "In 1846, mathematicians Adams and Le Verrier used perturbations in Uranus's orbit to predict the existence and location of Neptune, a planet no one had ever seen. When astronomers looked at the predicted spot, they found Neptune within 1 degree — a triumph of gravitational theory."
- **Type:** Diagram with historical annotation (vector)

### Figure 9: Binary Stars Orbiting Common Center of Mass
- **Location:** Concept 4, "The mechanism" section
- **Purpose:** Show how two comparable-mass objects orbit their mutual center of mass
- **Elements:**
  - Two stars of different sizes (representing different masses)
  - Circular orbits around a central point (center of mass)
  - Smaller star orbiting at larger radius (because it is less massive)
  - Larger star orbiting at smaller radius
  - Both shown at multiple positions in their orbit to show they maintain relative separation
  - Center of mass marked
  - Separation distance labeled
  - Orbital period labeled (same for both)
  - Optional: velocity vectors showing speeds are inversely proportional to masses
- **Caption:** "When two stars have comparable mass, both orbit their common center of mass. The more massive star orbits closer to the center of mass. By observing their orbital motions, astronomers can determine the individual masses using Newton's form of Kepler's third law."
- **Type:** Diagram (vector)

### Figure 10: Sputnik Orbital Decay
- **Location:** Concept 3, "The cold open" section
- **Purpose:** Show how atmospheric drag causes orbit to decay
- **Elements:**
  - Earth (schematic)
  - Initial orbit of Sputnik at 230 km altitude
  - Series of progressively lower orbits (showing decay over time)
  - Atmospheric layers shown (exosphere, thermosphere, mesosphere)
  - Drag forces indicated on satellite at lower altitudes
  - Timeline: Oct 4, 1957 (launch) to Jan 4, 1958 (burnup)
  - Spiral inward shown through overlapping orbits
- **Caption:** "Sputnik orbited at 230 km altitude in the thin upper atmosphere. Atmospheric drag removed energy with each orbit, causing Sputnik to spiral inward. As it fell into denser atmosphere, drag increased, accelerating the decay. Sputnik burned up 92 days after launch, demonstrating that orbits are delicate equilibria."
- **Type:** Diagram (vector with spiral)

## Notes for Artist/Illustrator

1. **Consistency:** Use the same Earth color, Sun color, and object representation across all figures.
2. **Scale:** Some figures are schematic (not to scale) to make the physics clear. Label accordingly.
3. **Labels:** Clear, large labels. Avoid clutter.
4. **Vector preferred:** For clarity and editability, SVG or vector format is preferred over raster.
5. **Accuracy:** Ensure all physics concepts are accurate (e.g., no circular orbits for Mercury).
6. **Pedagogical clarity:** Exaggerate eccentricities and force differences where needed to make the concept visible.
