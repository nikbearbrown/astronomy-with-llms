# Bookmap: Orbits and Gravity (Attenborough × Feynman)

## Overview

This bookmap analyzes the source material for Chapter 03: Orbits and Gravity. The source consists of seven OpenStax Astronomy chapter sections covering Kepler's laws, Newton's laws of motion and gravitation, orbital mechanics, solar system orbits, spacecraft orbits and escape velocity, and perturbations and the discovery of Neptune. The material is dense with historical narrative, mathematical formalism, and observational specifics. The conversion to Attenborough × Feynman style requires selective emphasis: prioritizing the human story (Brahe, Kepler, Newton, the mathematicians who found Neptune), the mechanism-from-first-principles, and the scaling between human experience and cosmic scale.

### Section 2: Kepler's Laws [Source: 02-m59775.md]

**What the source teaches:**

- Brahe's personality: wealthy, vain, paranoid about sharing data; his loss of patronage and move to Prague.

- Kepler's struggle with circular orbits; discovery that Mars's orbit is an ellipse.
- Definition and geometry of ellipse: foci, eccentricity, semimajor axis.
- Kepler's second law: equal areas in equal times (explained as planet speeding up near Sun, slowing far away).
- Kepler's third law: P² ∝ a³; worked examples with Mercury and other planets.

**How the source teaches it:**
- Strong historical narrative: names, places, personalities, conflicts (Brahe's secrecy, Kepler's poverty).
- Teaches the mechanism through visual analogies (string and tacks for drawing ellipses).
- Provides worked examples that show the law's applicability to all planets.
- Uses specific numbers (Mercury's semimajor axis: 228 million km; eccentricity of Mars: 0.1).

**What the Attenborough × Feynman version does:**
- Preserves the historical scene (Brahe and Kepler meeting in Prague, personality clash).
- Cold opens the concept section with the scene of Kepler arriving in Prague in winter, 1600; establishes the problem (data vs. theory).
- Explains ellipse through first principles: geometric definition of foci, eccentricity as elongation measure.
- Emphasizes the revolution: Kepler breaks with tradition (circles are no longer sacred).
- Shows why the shift matters: planets are sometimes much closer to the Sun, sometimes farther (e.g., Mercury's radiation difference).
- Kepler's second law presented as a consequence of geometry + gravity (though Kepler himself did not know the gravity part).
- Third law elevated to full dignity: "The beautiful equation P² = a³" — shows the power of mathematical relationships.
- **Trade:** The source emphasizes empirical discovery and parameter fitting; the conversion emphasizes the intellectual courage of rejecting an ancient assumption and the unity of the laws.

**Scenes worth expanding:**
- Brahe locked away in his observatory for 20 years, measuring obsessively.
- Brahe's jealousy (not giving Kepler all the data at once).
- Brahe dying, Kepler finally getting access to the full dataset.
- Kepler testing circle after circle, failing, trying ellipse, suddenly everything fitting.

**Numerical specifics preserved:**

- Mars's eccentricity: 0.09.
- Mercury's eccentricity: 0.21 (note: more eccentric than other planets, which is why Kepler could detect the difference).

### Section 4: Universal Gravitation [Source: 04-m59777.md]

**What the source teaches:**
- Newton's extension of gravity from Earth to the entire universe.
- The inverse-square law: F ∝ M₁M₂/R².
- Comparison: falling apple vs. Moon's acceleration toward Earth.
- Moon is 60 Earth radii away; acceleration should be 1/3600th; it is, matching observation.
- Weight as gravitational force; distinction between mass and weight.
- Astronauts in free fall (weightlessness not due to absence of gravity, but due to falling).
- Newton's reformulation of Kepler's third law to include both masses: a³ = (M₁ + M₂)P².
- Using orbital motions to deduce masses (inverse problem).
- Worked examples: human weight on Earth; calculating planetary properties.

**How the source teaches it:**
- Opens with the brilliant leap: Earth's gravity extends to the Moon.
- Provides mathematical justification: the inverse-square law predicts the Moon's observed acceleration.
- Distinguishes mass from weight through multiple examples (Moon vs. Earth).
- Explains weightlessness: astronauts fall at the same rate as their spacecraft, so they experience no additional force.
- Shows how Newton's framework explains Kepler's laws (mathematical derivation sketched).
- Includes Newton's revised Kepler's third law, enabling mass measurement.
- Multiple worked examples: weight calculations, exoplanet mass deduction.

**What the Attenborough × Feynman version does:**
- Preserves the leap: gravity is universal.
- Explains the inverse-square law through first principles (how force decays with distance).
- Uses the apple/Moon comparison, but makes it vivid: "The Moon is falling toward Earth, just as the apple falls. The Moon does not hit Earth because it is moving sideways fast enough that Earth's curvature carries it out of harm's way."
- Distinguishes mass (intrinsic property) from weight (gravitational force), using clear examples (Moon vs. Earth).
- Explains free fall and weightlessness as a consequence of gravity acting equally on all objects in the orbit.
- Emphasizes that gravity never becomes zero — it reaches across the entire universe, even if weakly.
- Includes worked example: calculating your weight on Earth; showing how weight changes with gravity.
- Highlights the power: once gravity is understood, you can deduce masses from orbits without direct measurement.
- **Trade:** The source emphasizes the mathematical form of gravity (inverse-square law); the conversion emphasizes the unifying principle and its consequences.

**Equations preserved:**
- F_gravity = G(M₁M₂)/R² (universal law of gravitation)
- a³ = (M₁ + M₂)P² (Newton's form of Kepler's third law)

**Key insight preserved:**
- Gravity is never zero; it reaches infinitely far but falls off inversely with the square of distance.

### Section 6: Orbital Mechanics — Spacecraft and Escape Velocity [Source: 06-m59779.md]

**What the source teaches:**
- Newton's Mountain thought experiment: firing a bullet horizontally faster and faster; eventually it orbits.
- Circular satellite velocity: 8 km/s for Earth orbit.
- Escape velocity: 11.2 km/s for Earth.
- Relationship: escape velocity = √2 × circular orbital velocity.
- Sputnik (October 4, 1957) as first artificial satellite.
- Thousands of satellites since, used for weather, GPS, communications, military purposes.
- ISS at low Earth orbit: ~90-minute period.
- Low Earth orbits: vulnerable to atmospheric drag; requires periodic boost to avoid decay.
- Interplanetary spacecraft: achieve escape velocity, coast to target, use gravity assist for redirection.
- Example: Voyager 2 used gravity assist at Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.
- Orbital insertion: must slow down (fire thrusters) to be captured into elliptical orbit around target.

**How the source teaches it:**
- Newton's Mountain is the central analogy: elegantly unifies orbital motion with projectile motion.
- Numerical specificity: 8 km/s for Earth orbital velocity, 11.2 km/s for escape velocity.
- Historical example: Sputnik as watershed moment.
- Practical examples: ISS period, atmospheric drag effects, Voyager missions.

**What the Attenborough × Feynman version does:**
- Opens the orbital mechanics concept section with Sputnik's orbital decay: a concrete, visceral example of the delicate balance of orbits.
- Explains orbital velocity through the mechanism: centripetal acceleration must equal gravitational acceleration.
- Derives orbital velocity from first principles; shows it depends on central mass and orbital radius.
- Explains escape velocity as a threshold: when kinetic energy is large enough that gravity cannot pull the object back.
- Shows the relationship: escape velocity = √2 × orbital velocity (energy consideration).
- Distinguishes between energy and velocity: orbital objects have less total energy, escape objects have more.
- Uses worked example: ISS at 400 km altitude (7.68 km/s, 92-minute period).
- **Trade:** The source emphasizes practical applications (Sputnik, Voyager, ISS); the conversion emphasizes the physics principles and how they constrain what is possible.

**Preserved details:**
- Newton's Mountain (cannon on tall mountain).
- Sputnik launch date: October 4, 1957.
- Sputnik lifetime: ~92 days (to illustrate orbital decay).
- ISS altitude: 400 km; period: ~92 minutes; velocity: ~7.7 km/s.
- Escape velocity: 11.2 km/s (roughly 25,000 mph).
- Escape velocity = √2 × orbital velocity (algebraic relationship).
- Voyager gravity-assist missions (though not elaborated in detail).

### Section 7: Perturbations and Neptune's Discovery [Source: 07-m59780.md]

**What the source teaches:**
- Problem of three or more bodies: orbits become complicated; requires computers.
- Example: cluster of 1000 stars orbiting common center; must calculate gravitational forces from all on one.
- Within solar system: perturbations are small but computable using elegant mathematical techniques.
- Historical example: discovery of Neptune.
- Uranus discovered (1781) by Herschel, a musician-turned-astronomer.
- By 1790, orbit calculated; but Uranus did not match predicted positions.
- By 1840, discrepancy was ~0.03° (barely visible but larger than error margins).
- 1843: John Couch Adams (young mathematician at Cambridge) analyzes irregularities, hypothesizes unseen planet.
- Adams predicts location and mass of unknown planet; informs George Airy (Astronomer Royal) in October 1845.
- Airy delays following up.
- Meanwhile, Urbain Le Verrier (French mathematician, unaware of Adams) solves same problem independently, publishes June 1846.
- Le Verrier's prediction matches Adams's within 1°.
- James Challis (Cambridge Observatory) begins search but is negligent examining his data (actually saw Neptune but did not recognize it).
- Johann Galle (Berlin Observatory) receives Le Verrier's letter September 23, 1846, finds Neptune that same night, less than 1° from prediction.
- Discovery represents triumph of gravitational theory: prediction without observation.
- Bonus section: "Astronomy and the Poets" — how poets reacted to Copernicus, Newton, new mechanical world view. John Donne, Alexander Pope, Arthur Hugh Clough quoted.

**How the source teaches it:**
- Uses the three-body problem as motivation: why perturbations matter.
- Tells the Neptune story chronologically: observation → prediction → failure to follow up → independent rediscovery → triumph.
- Emphasizes the human drama: Adams vs. Le Verrier, Airy's procrastination, Challis's negligence, Galle's success.
- Includes cultural context: poems reacting to the shift from geocentric to heliocentric cosmos.

**What the Attenborough × Feynman version does:**
- Opens the orbital masses concept with the Neptune discovery scene: mathematicians looking at a planet that no one has ever seen.
- Frames it as the ultimate test of Newton's theory: can we predict existence and location of an invisible object from the gravitational perturbations it causes?
- Condenses the historical details: names (Adams, Le Verrier), dates (1846), result (found within 1°).
- Emphasizes the mechanism: observe perturbations in Uranus, infer the presence and properties of Neptune.
- Explains Newton's reformulated Kepler's third law (with both masses) as the tool that makes this possible.
- Preserves the drama: "No one had ever seen Neptune. It was too faint to notice. No one even knew it existed. Yet mathematicians deduced its existence..."
- **Trade:** The source emphasizes the historical narrative and cultural impact; the conversion emphasizes the logical triumph of applying gravitational theory to an inverse problem (inferring mass from perturbations).

**Preserved details:**
- Adams and Le Verrier both solved the problem independently in 1845-1846.
- Prediction was accurate to within 1° (extraordinary).
- Neptune is so faint it was not discovered until the prediction.
- The discovery vindicated Newton's laws and demonstrated their universality.

## Harvest of Angles, Analogies, Structures, and Gaps

### Cold-Open Candidates (Scene-First Approach)

1. **Sputnik orbiting Earth (1957):** A human-made object in space — wonder, achievement, question of how it stays there.
2. **Newton's plague year (1665-1666):** A young man alone, thinking about apples and moons.
3. **Kepler in Prague (winter 1600):** A poor mathematician arriving to work with a wealthy, cranky observer; obsessed with patterns in Brahe's data.
4. **Neptune's discovery (1846):** Mathematicians looking at an invisible planet, knowing where to find it because it perturbs Uranus.
5. **The cannon on the mountain (Newton's thought experiment):** Firing faster and faster, until the trajectory matches Earth's curvature.

All five are used in the converted chapter.

### Specification Moves (Defining Vague Terms)

- **"Orbit"**: Not a mystical path, but a trajectory where falling and forward motion balance.
- **"Ellipse"**: Not a sloppy circle, but a precisely defined curve with two foci; sum of distances to foci is constant.
- **"Gravity"**: Not just a force pulling downward; a universal attractive force between all masses, proportional to their product and inversely proportional to the square of distance.
- **"Weightlessness"**: Not the absence of gravity, but free fall; everything accelerates together at the same rate.
- **"Escape velocity"**: Not reaching infinity, but having enough kinetic energy that gravity cannot pull you back.

### Mechanism Explanations (First-Principles Deep-Dives)

1. **Kepler's first law:** Ellipse geometry (foci, eccentricity) emerging from gravity + conservation of angular momentum.
2. **Newton's mountain:** Projectile motion becoming orbital when the projectile's trajectory curves at the same rate as Earth.
3. **Inverse-square law:** How gravitational force decays with distance; shown through worked example (your weight on Earth).
4. **Orbital velocity:** Centripetal acceleration (v²/r) equals gravitational acceleration (GM/r²); solve for v.
5. **Escape velocity:** Energy consideration; kinetic energy must exceed gravitational potential energy's magnitude.
6. **Perturbations:** Small deviations in an orbit caused by nearby masses; used to infer the presence and mass of unseen objects.

### Trade-Offs Named

1. **Kepler's empiricism vs. Newton's mechanism:** Laws describe, but do not explain why.
2. **Newton's universality vs. domain limits:** Gravity works everywhere, but breaks at extreme scales (relativity, quantum mechanics).
3. **Simplicity of circular orbits vs. realism of elliptical orbits:** Circular is easy to calculate; elliptical is true.
4. **Precision of predictions vs. complexity of three-body problem:** Two bodies, clean math; three or more, requires computation.
5. **Gravity's infinite reach vs. its practical weakness at distance:** Gravity never zero, but so faint far away it is imperceptible.

### Scale Oscillations (Human ↔ Cosmic)

- You stand on Earth (human scale).
- Gravity pulls you down at 9.8 m/s².
- The Moon is 60 Earth radii away; gravity pulls it down at 1/3600th the rate.
- Yet the relationship (inverse-square) is the same.
- Sputnik orbits at 8 km/s; takes 92 minutes to circle Earth.
- Mars orbits at 24 km/s; takes 687 Earth-days to circle the Sun.
- Neptune orbits at 5 km/s; takes 165 years to circle the Sun.
- The further you are, the slower you move, and the longer the period.
- Yet all follow the same law: P² = a³.

### Moral Weight (Through Accumulation)

- Kepler breaks with tradition (circles are not sacred; ellipses work better).
- Newton unifies (falling and orbiting are the same phenomenon).
- The discovery of Neptune (invisible object deduced from mathematics and gravity).
- Spacecraft orbit Earth, leaving the surface for the first time.
- We measure the masses of distant objects without ever touching them.
- We predict where comets will appear centuries from now.
- The solar system is knowable. Not through magic, but through mechanisms we can understand.

### Misconceptions Addressed in Source (Worth Preserving)

1. Circular orbits as the "natural" perfect shape (broken by Kepler's data).
2. Gravity as local to Earth (Newton shows it is universal).
3. Weight and mass as synonymous (precision: weight depends on location; mass is intrinsic).
4. Orbits requiring continuous force (once in orbit, no thrust needed; gravity provides the continuous "pull").
5. Gravity as binary (present or absent); actually inverse-square (weaker at distance, but never zero).

### Gaps and Limitations in Source (Noted for Future Expansion)

1. **Tides:** Gravity's gradient across an extended body (Moon pulling harder on Earth's near-side than far-side) not covered in this chapter. Foundational for next chapter.
2. **Orbital resonances:** When periods are in simple ratios (2:1, 3:2), resonances can affect stability (e.g., Kirkwood gaps in asteroid belt). Not covered.
3. **Three-body problem:** Mentioned as complicated; not solved. Heuristically understandable, but mathematically intractable except in special cases.
4. **Relativity and quantum limits:** Newton's theory breaks near black holes and at atomic scales. Mentioned in trade-offs, not elaborated.
5. **Angular momentum conservation:** Used to explain Kepler's second law, but not derived from first principles.

## Summary: How to Use This Bookmap

This bookmap shows that the source material is rich in historical narrative, mathematical rigor, and specific examples. The Attenborough × Feynman conversion:

- **Keeps:** Cold opens (Sputnik, Brahe-Kepler, Newton, Neptune), mechanism explanations (Newton's mountain, inverse-square law, orbital velocity), specific numbers (8 km/s, 11.2 km/s), and the unifying insight (gravity explains all orbits).
- **Reorders:** Puts gravity central rather than treating Kepler's laws and Newton's laws as separate topics.
- **Emphasizes:** The human story (people discovering laws, making breakthroughs) and the scales oscillation (human experience to cosmic scale).
- **Omits:** Detailed mathematical proofs, tables of material densities, comprehensive orbital data for all planets, the "Astronomy and the Poets" section (though cultural context could enrich future editions).
- **Expands:** Mechanism sections and worked examples to show how principles are applied.
