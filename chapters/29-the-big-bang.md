# The Universe's First Three Minutes: Evidence for the Beginning

**Suggested titles:**

2. Beyond the Horizon: How We Know the Universe Had a Start

---

**TL;DR:** Three independent observations — galaxies flying apart, a faint microwave glow from when the universe was 380,000 years old, and the precise abundances of the lightest elements — converge on a single story. The universe was once infinitely hot, infinitely dense, and infinitely small. It has been expanding and cooling ever since.

---

## I. The Puzzle: A Universe That Had to Begin

It is 1929. Edwin Hubble, an astronomer at Mount Wilson Observatory in California, publishes a simple observation. He has measured the distances to a small sample of galaxies and their velocities (inferred from redshift). The correlation is stark: the farther away a galaxy is, the faster it is moving away from us.

The result stuns the astronomical community. The universe is not static. It is not a unchanging stage. It is expanding. Galaxies are not drifting randomly; they are receding from each other in an organized, measurable way.

Here is where the puzzle takes shape. If the universe is expanding *now*, and has been expanding for billions of years, run the film backward. At what point did all the matter we see today converge to a single point?

Hubble's law, stated mathematically, is simple:

$$v = H_0 \times d$$

where $v$ is recession velocity, $d$ is distance, and $H_0$ is a constant (the Hubble constant). Rearrange it:

$$T_0 = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{1}{H_0}$$

The age of the universe estimated this way is just the reciprocal of the Hubble constant. If Hubble constant is about 70 kilometers per second per megaparsec, the age comes out to roughly 13.8 billion years.

But this calculation assumes the expansion has been constant. What if it hasn't? What if gravity slowed the expansion in the past, or something else sped it up? How do we know the true age?

The answer requires two additional lines of evidence — evidence so old, so direct, and so independent that together they are nearly impossible to doubt.

## II. Three Pillars: How We Know What We Know

### A. The Light from When the Universe Was 380,000 Years Old

In 1964, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson, working at Bell Laboratories in New Jersey, were trying to build a sensitive radio antenna. They kept finding a persistent noise in their receiver — a faint, isotropic glow coming from all directions. At first, they thought it was interference. They cleaned pigeon droppings from the antenna horn. They repositioned the equipment. The glow remained.

What they had discovered, without meaning to, was the cosmic microwave background (CMB) — the ancient light from when the universe was only 380,000 years old.

Here is what that number means. For the first 380,000 years, the universe was too hot for electrons and protons to combine into atoms. Space was filled with a roiling plasma of free particles and photons. Light could not travel far without hitting a charged particle and scattering. The universe was opaque — like looking into thick fog.

But as the universe expanded, it cooled. At about 380,000 years, the temperature dropped to 3,000 Kelvin. At this temperature, electrons and protons combined into neutral hydrogen atoms in a process called recombination. Suddenly, photons could travel through space without scattering. Light decoupled from matter. The universe became transparent.

The light that was traveling through the plasma at that moment has been traveling ever since, 13.8 billion years unimpeded. It is still arriving at Earth today. Because the universe has been expanding, that light has been stretched — redshifted — to wavelengths in the microwave part of the spectrum. This is the cosmic microwave background.

Here is what makes this observation so powerful: the CMB gives us a direct snapshot of the universe when it was young. We can measure its temperature (2.73 Kelvin today, after cooling for 13.8 billion years of expansion). We can map its tiny variations in temperature from place to place. We can ask: what do these variations tell us about the universe's early conditions?

The temperature variations are small — about one part in 100,000. A variation that looks like 2.73000 Kelvin in one direction looks like 2.73001 Kelvin in another. Yet these tiny fluctuations contain *everything* we see today: they are the seeds of galaxies, the birthplaces of stars, the imprints of the universe's deepest physics.

Color scale represents temperature variations from about 2.72998 K to 2.73002 K. Acoustic peaks visible in the angular power spectrum graph (inset).]

By the early 2000s, satellites — first COBE, then WMAP, and finally Planck — mapped the CMB with extraordinary precision. They found that the temperature variations were not random. Instead, they showed a characteristic *pattern* in their power spectrum — a technical term for how the temperature variations are distributed across different angular scales on the sky.

The pattern revealed peaks at specific angular scales. These peaks are called acoustic peaks, and they are the signature of sound waves in the early universe. Here is why this matters: in the hot, dense plasma of the early universe, pressure waves moved through the matter like sound waves in air. When recombination happened, these waves froze in place. They imprinted themselves on the temperature map of the CMB. By measuring the positions of the peaks, we can infer what the early universe was like — its density, its composition, the geometry of space itself.

The first acoustic peak, for instance, tells us the geometry of the universe. If space is flat (which general relativity suggests), sound waves should reach a maximum amplitude at a particular angular scale. If space is curved — either closed like a sphere or open like a saddle — that scale changes. The Planck measurements confirm that space is flat to better than 0.1% precision. The universe is not curved.

The second and third peaks tell us about the densities of matter and radiation. The heights of the peaks tell us about the baryon density — the density of ordinary matter (protons, neutrons, electrons) as opposed to dark matter. From these measurements, we can infer that ordinary matter makes up about 5% of the universe's total energy density. Dark matter makes up about 27%. The remaining 68% is dark energy.

This is not speculation. This is inference from precision measurements of light that has traveled across the universe for 13.8 billion years.

### B. The Primordial Abundances: Helium, Hydrogen, and Lithium

The second independent line of evidence comes from the simplest atoms: hydrogen, helium, and lithium.

In the early universe, during the first three minutes after the Big Bang, the temperature was still hot enough for nuclear fusion. Protons and neutrons combined to form the nuclei of the lightest elements. As the universe cooled past about 1 billion Kelvin, fusion stopped. The abundances of these light elements were frozen in.

Today, we measure those abundances. By mass, the universe is about 75% hydrogen, 25% helium, and a trace amount (about 0.0001%) of lithium-7. These numbers are strikingly consistent across the universe — in our Galaxy, in other galaxies, in the intergalactic gas clouds between galaxies.

Now here is the key insight: we can calculate, from first principles and quantum mechanics, what the abundances should be if the universe started in a state of extreme heat and density and has been cooling and expanding ever since.

The calculation is called Big Bang nucleosynthesis. It works like this:

**Step one:** Start with a universe filled with radiation and a roughly equal number of protons and neutrons.

**Step two:** As the universe cools below 1 trillion Kelvin, neutrons begin to combine with protons to form deuterium (a nucleus with one proton and one neutron).

**Step three:** Deuterium combines with more nucleons to form helium-4 (two protons, two neutrons).

**Step four:** A small amount of lithium-7 forms, but most reactions are blocked because no stable nucleus with mass number 5 or 8 exists — a "mass gap" that halts the buildup of heavier elements.

**Step five:** Below 1 billion Kelvin, the reaction rates drop too low for further fusion. The abundances freeze.

**Step six:** The universe continues to cool and expand.

The remarkable fact is that the predicted abundances match the observed abundances *exactly*. The ratio of hydrogen to helium, the tiny amount of lithium, the absence of heavier elements — all match what the Big Bang nucleosynthesis calculation predicts.

No other theory explains this pattern. If the universe had a different origin — if it had always existed in a steady state, or if it began cold and warmed up, or if elements were created in stars and then released — the predictions would be wrong. The light element abundances are a fossil record of the early universe's temperature and density.

### C. The Expansion Rate and the Age of Consistency

The third pillar is the age determination itself, but refined by understanding that the expansion rate has not been constant.

In the 1990s, astronomers used Type Ia supernovae as standard candles to measure distances to distant galaxies. Type Ia supernovae occur in binary systems where a white dwarf pulls material from a companion star until the white dwarf's core reaches the Chandrasekhar limit and explodes. At maximum brightness, they outshine their host galaxies.

The initial expectation was that the expansion should be *decelerating* due to gravity. The mutual attraction between galaxies should slow the expansion as time passed. If you measure the current expansion rate and assume it has been slowing down, you overestimate the universe's age.

What the observations revealed in 1998 was shocking: distant supernovae were *fainter* than expected if the expansion were decelerating. In fact, they were fainter than expected even if the expansion had been constant. The only explanation was that the expansion is *accelerating*. The universe is expanding faster now than it was billions of years ago.

This discovery revealed the existence of dark energy — an unknown component that makes up 68% of the universe's total energy density and is causing the expansion to accelerate. The source of this energy remains a profound mystery.

Yet here is the key: when we account for both the early deceleration (due to gravity when galaxies were close) and the late acceleration (due to dark energy), the age calculation comes out to 13.8 billion years.

Moreover, this age is consistent with independent measurements. The oldest stars in our Galaxy, found in globular clusters, have ages of 12 to 13 billion years — younger than the universe itself, as they must be. The oldest known white dwarfs have ages of about 12 billion years. The radioactive decay of long-lived isotopes like uranium-238 in ancient stars gives ages of about 12.5 billion years. All these independent clocks point to a universe age between 12 and 14 billion years. The figure of 13.8 billion years is the modern consensus.

## III. The First Three Minutes: What Physics Tells Us

Now we come to the profound question: what was the universe *like* in the first few minutes?

The story begins even before we can observe directly. Einstein's general relativity predicts that if the universe started from an extremely hot, dense state and has been expanding ever since, there must have been a moment of infinite density and infinite temperature — a singularity. But the equations break down at a singularity. We cannot penetrate to times earlier than about $10^{-43}$ seconds (a number with a decimal point followed by 42 zeros and then a one). At earlier times, quantum gravity effects dominate, and we have no adequate theory.

But we can trace the universe from about $10^{-6}$ seconds onward.

### The Electron-Positron Era

At $10^{-6}$ seconds, the universe is about 1 trillion Kelvin. The density is so high that if you could gather all the atoms in all the stars in the Milky Way and compress them to the size of a sugar cube, you would just begin to approach these densities.

At this temperature and density, pairs of particles and their antimatter counterparts (the positron, which is an electron with positive charge) are constantly being created and annihilated by collisions between high-energy photons. The universe is a seething plasma of electrons, positrons, photons, and the ghostly neutrinos, with traces of other particles.

### The Quark-Gluon Plasma

Go back further, to $10^{-12}$ seconds, and the universe is a trillion trillion Kelvin. At these energies, the very structure of matter breaks down. Protons and neutrons, which are made of quarks held together by gluons, disassemble. The universe becomes a quark-gluon plasma — a state of matter never observed in any laboratory until the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider at Brookhaven National Laboratory began colliding atoms at near light speeds in the 2000s.

### Nucleosynthesis: The First Three Minutes

As the universe cools below 1 trillion Kelvin (which happens at about $10^{-5}$ seconds), quarks combine to form protons and neutrons. These particles then collide and decay in a delicate dance.

Neutrons are slightly heavier than protons. In the hot early universe, when the temperature is high enough, collisions between photons, electrons, and positrons convert protons into neutrons and vice versa. As the temperature drops, the conversion becomes less favorable. Fewer neutrons are created, and the ones that exist begin to decay back into protons (a free neutron decays with a half-life of about 10 minutes at rest, though the process is altered in the early universe due to the high density and temperature).

By about 1 second, the universe has cooled enough that neutrons and protons no longer freely interconvert. The ratio of neutrons to protons becomes fixed at about one neutron for every six protons.

Between 1 and 3 seconds, this is the moment when nucleosynthesis truly begins. Neutrons combine with protons to form deuterium (one proton, one neutron). Deuterium combines with more nucleons to form helium-3 and then helium-4.

The calculation, first worked out by George Gamow and Ralph Alpher in 1948, goes like this:

Starting with 1 neutron and 6 protons (the ratio frozen at 1 second), nearly all the neutrons end up incorporated into helium-4 nuclei. Each helium-4 nucleus contains 2 neutrons and 2 protons. So if we have 1 neutron for every 6 protons:

- The neutrons pair up into 7 neutron-proton pairs (one neutron pairs with 1 proton; we have 5 protons left over).
- These 7 pairs could form 3.5 helium-4 nuclei... but we don't get half a nucleus. The exact calculation accounts for the fact that some deuterium survives, some tritium forms, and some other light nuclei appear. The result: roughly 25% of the mass ends up in helium-4, and the remaining 75% stays as hydrogen (single protons that will later capture electrons to become atoms).

Below 1 billion Kelvin, the reaction rates become too slow for further fusion. Helium and hydrogen abundances freeze. The universe continues to cool, but no new nuclei are forged in this era. We call this "Big Bang nucleosynthesis," and it is one of the best-tested predictions of the Big Bang model.

### Recombination and the Release of Light

For the next 380,000 years, the universe cools gradually. Photons still outnumber massive particles. Light cannot travel far without scattering off free electrons.

Then, at about 380,000 years, the universe reaches 3,000 Kelvin. Electrons and protons combine into neutral hydrogen atoms. This is recombination. Photons can now travel freely. The universe becomes transparent. The light that was bouncing around in the plasma decouples from matter and streams outward. This ancient light is the cosmic microwave background we observe today.

But before this happens, one more crucial process occurs: the superhot plasma leaves its imprint on the distribution of matter. Regions of higher density cool more slowly. Regions of lower density cool faster. These density variations create slight variations in temperature. When recombination happens, these temperature variations are frozen into the radiation field. The pattern we measure today in the CMB is the fossil record of these density perturbations. They are the seeds of galaxies.

## IV. The Major Open Questions

Three decades of observations have given us a framework that accounts for the history of the universe from the first fraction of a second to today. Yet profound mysteries remain.

### What is Dark Energy?

We know dark energy exists because we observe the accelerating expansion. We know it makes up 68% of the universe's energy density. But we do not know what it *is*.

One possibility is the cosmological constant — an energy associated with the vacuum itself. Quantum mechanics predicts that "empty" space is not truly empty; virtual particle-antiparticle pairs flicker in and out of existence constantly. The energy density of this quantum vacuum might be the source of dark energy.

Yet when physicists try to calculate this energy density from first principles, they get a number that is off by a factor of at least $10^{120}$ — that is, a 1 followed by 120 zeros. This is called the cosmological constant problem, and it stands as one of the deepest unsolved problems in physics.

Alternative theories suggest dark energy might be "quintessence" — a dynamical field similar to the Higgs field — or that general relativity itself might be wrong on the largest scales. None of these explanations is yet supported by decisive evidence.

### The Hubble Tension

A second mystery is the tension between two different measurements of the Hubble constant — the expansion rate today.

One measurement comes from the cosmic microwave background. The Planck satellite measured the acoustic peaks with extraordinary precision and inferred what the Hubble constant must be. The value: 67.4 kilometers per second per megaparsec, with an uncertainty of about 0.5%.

A second measurement comes from Type Ia supernovae and Cepheid variables. Local distance measurements using these objects give a Hubble constant of about 73 kilometers per second per megaparsec, with an uncertainty of about 1%.

The two measurements disagree at high statistical significance. Either there is a systematic error in one of the measurements, or the expansion rate has changed in a way that our current models do not account for. The resolution remains unknown.

### Inflation: What Triggered the Hot Big Bang?

There is a deeper mystery: what about the moment before the observable universe began to expand?

The Big Bang model predicts an early period of *inflation*, a theoretical phase where the universe expanded exponentially fast — doubling its size roughly every $10^{-37}$ seconds. This inflation would have smoothed out any primordial irregularities, flattened the geometry of space (which is why space is flat today), and stretched quantum fluctuations to become the seeds of galaxies and clusters.

The model of inflation explains the uniformity of the cosmic microwave background (why it is so nearly the same temperature in all directions), the flatness of space (why the geometry is Euclidean to such precision), and the origin of structure (how tiny quantum fluctuations became the initial perturbations that grew into galaxies).

Yet we do not know what caused inflation, what field drove it, or when it ended and the "hot Big Bang" phase began. Different models of inflation make different predictions about the spectrum of primordial gravitational waves, the non-Gaussianity of density perturbations, and other observables. Future observations of the CMB and the large-scale structure of the universe may distinguish between these models — or reveal that inflation is wrong and we need a different framework entirely.

## V. The Synthesis: Three Observations Converge

We began with a puzzle: Hubble observed that galaxies are receding from each other. The universe is expanding. If we run the film backward, all the matter converges to a single point.

What started as a single observation has become a framework supported by three independent lines of evidence:

1. **The expansion itself:** Galaxies are receding. The farther away they are, the faster they move. This is measured directly by redshift.

2. **The light from the young universe:** The cosmic microwave background, a fossil from when the universe was 380,000 years old, tells us the temperature and density of the early universe and the geometry of space itself. The acoustic peaks in its power spectrum are consistent with predictions from the Big Bang model.

3. **The light element abundances:** Hydrogen, helium, and lithium exist in the precise ratios predicted by Big Bang nucleosynthesis. No other theory explains these abundances.

These three observations point to a single history: the universe began in a state of extreme heat and density and has been expanding and cooling for 13.8 billion years.

Yet this history raises new questions — about dark energy, about inflation, about the meaning of a "beginning" itself. The story is not finished. But the broad outlines are clear. We live in a universe that had a beginning. We can measure when that beginning was. We can infer what conditions were like in the earliest fractions of a second. And we are only at the start of understanding why.

What would change my mind: A discovery that the expansion of the universe is decelerating, or that the light element abundances deviate significantly from Big Bang nucleosynthesis predictions, or that the cosmic microwave background contains features inconsistent with the standard model would force a fundamental revision.

Still puzzling: We do not yet understand the physical nature of dark energy, the cause of the Hubble tension, or what mechanism triggered inflation. These are the frontier questions in cosmology.

## Key Concepts Summary

The universe began in an extremely hot, dense state called the Big Bang and has been expanding and cooling ever since. We know this from three independent observations: the recession of distant galaxies (Hubble's law), the existence and properties of the cosmic microwave background (a fossil from when the universe was 380,000 years old), and the primordial abundances of light elements (hydrogen, helium, lithium) which match Big Bang nucleosynthesis predictions perfectly. In the first three minutes, the universe was hot enough for nuclear fusion to occur, creating the light elements we observe today. The universe is now 13.8 billion years old and is currently expanding at an accelerating rate due to dark energy. Major unsolved questions remain about the nature of dark energy, the small tension between different measurements of the expansion rate, and the physical cause of cosmic inflation.
