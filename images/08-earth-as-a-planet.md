### Part One: The Differentiated Planet

**[FIGURE: Earth's Interior Cross-Section]**
- **Depth scale:** Vertical axis, 0 to 6,400 km
- **Layers shown:**
  - Crust: 0-6 km (oceanic, light color), 0-70 km (continental, lighter)
  - Mantle: 6-2,900 km (granular texture, indicating deformation/flow)
  - Outer core: 2,900-5,100 km (liquid, metallic sheen)
  - Inner core: 5,100-6,371 km (solid, bright metal)
- **Labels with values:**
  - Density gradient (arrows showing increase with depth)
  - Temperature profile (isotherms at 0°C, 1,000°C, 2,000°C, 3,000°C, 4,000°C, 5,000°C)
- **Caption:** "Earth's internal structure, inferred from seismic wave propagation and gravitational data. Density increases with depth due to compression and composition change. The core is primarily iron-nickel; the mantle is silicate rock. The crust (shown exaggerated in thickness for visibility) is the outermost, least dense layer."
- **Pedagogical note:** Shows why density measurements (5.5 g/cm³ average, but crust 3 g/cm³) imply a dense core.

**[FIGURE: Seismic Wave Shadow Zone and Refraction]**
- **Top:** Map view of Earth with epicenter, recording stations
- **Bottom:** Cross-section showing wave paths
  - Direct path along surface (small, fast-traveling wave)
  - Path through mantle (straight, then refracts at core boundary)
  - Shadow zone (no direct waves, only refracted ones)
- **Color code:** Different wave types (P-waves, S-waves) in different colors
- **Caption:** "How seismic waves reveal Earth's interior. Waves traveling through different materials refract at boundaries. Some seismograph stations receive waves; others sit in 'shadow' zones. By mapping these patterns across hundreds of stations, geophysicists reconstructed Earth's layer structure."
- **Pedagogical note:** Demonstrates the inference method (not drilling, but listening to vibrations).

### Part Two: The Magnetic Field

**[FIGURE: Earth's Magnetosphere and Solar Wind]**
- **Left side:** The Sun (bright, with arrow showing solar wind particles flowing outward)
- **Center:** Earth as a sphere
- **Around Earth:** Field lines drawn from magnetic dipole
  - Compressed on sunward side (60,000 km or "10 Earth radii")
  - Extended on nightside (reaching to Moon's orbit and beyond)
- **Shading:** Light-shaded region = magnetosphere (protected zone)
- **Inset:** Close-up showing solar wind particles (dots) bouncing off field lines
- **Caption:** "Earth's magnetosphere shields the planet from the solar wind. The solar wind (charged particles streaming from the Sun) compresses the field on the dayside and stretches it on the nightside. The magnetosphere extends roughly 60,000 km toward the Sun but can reach the Moon's orbit (384,000 km) on the opposite side."
- **Pedagogical note:** Shows protection mechanism visually — why losing the field would mean losing atmospheric gases to space.

**[FIGURE: Van Allen Belts Schematic]**
- **Cross-section view of Earth (equator facing viewer)**
- **Two doughnut-shaped regions** surrounding the equator, extending from about 1 Earth radius to 4 Earth radii
- **Inner belt:** denser, solid color (high-energy protons and electrons)
- **Outer belt:** more diffuse, lighter shade (electrons primarily)
- **Field lines:** shown as curves running pole to pole, with particles spiraling along them
- **Caption:** "Van Allen radiation belts trap charged particles in Earth's magnetosphere. The inner belt is denser and more stable; the outer belt fluctuates with solar activity. High-energy particles here can interfere with satellites and pose radiation hazards for space missions."
- **Pedagogical note:** Illustrates how magnetic field geometry creates particle traps.

### Part Three: Active Geology

**[FIGURE: Mid-Ocean Ridge and Subduction Zone]**
- **Seafloor spreading (left side of diagram):**
  - Ridge at bottom (hot rock wells up)
  - Cooler ocean crust spreads outward on both sides
  - Arrow showing spreading direction and rate (~2.5 cm/yr)
  - Age progression: youngest at ridge, older away from ridge
- **Subduction (right side of diagram):**
  - Older oceanic crust descends beneath continental crust
  - Arrows showing downward motion
  - Friction heating zone indicated (color gradient showing temperature increase)
  - Magma plume rising at volcanic arc
  - Volcano drawn on continental margin
- **Color coding:**
  - Red (hot mantle material)
  - Orange/yellow (cooling crust)
  - Brown (older crust)
  - Dark blue (water, atmosphere)
- **Caption:** "Plate tectonics cycle. New oceanic crust forms at mid-ocean ridges as hot mantle material wells up. This new crust pushes older material outward. Where plates collide, older, denser crust sinks into the mantle (subduction). The descending plate heats, partially melts, and material rises as magma, forming volcanoes. The entire ocean floor is recycled over tens of millions of years."
- **Pedagogical note:** Shows the complete cycle — creation and destruction balanced, erasing old surface.

**[FIGURE: Plate Tectonics Renewal]**
- **Top view of Earth's surface**
- **Mid-ocean ridges** shown as red lines (spreading centers)
- **Subduction zones** shown as purple lines with teeth (descending plates)
- **Continental plates** outlined in light color
- **Arrows** showing plate motion directions
- **Age coloring** of ocean floor (youngest at ridges, oldest near continental margins and subduction zones)
- **A few impact craters** marked with circles — show how they are being carried toward subduction zones and will eventually be recycled into the mantle
- **Caption:** "Plate boundaries and age of ocean floor. Red lines are spreading ridges; purple teeth mark subduction zones. The age of ocean floor (indicated by color, youngest in red, oldest in purple) shows the recycling: new crust at ridges, old crust subducted. Impact craters that form on the seafloor are eventually recycled into the mantle as the plates move."
- **Pedagogical note:** Visualizes why craters vanish — they're part of plates being destroyed.

### Part Four: The Atmosphere

**[FIGURE: Atmospheric Layers and Temperature Profile]**
- **Left side:** Altitude scale (0-100 km)
- **Middle:** Layer names and altitudes
  - Troposphere: 0-12 km (temperature decreases upward)
  - Stratosphere: 12-50 km (temperature increases upward due to ozone absorption)
  - Mesosphere: 50-85 km (temperature decreases again)
  - Ionosphere: 85+ km (sparse, ions shown as dots)
- **Right side:** Temperature profile curve (coldest ~-56°C at stratosphere-troposphere boundary)
- **Features marked:**
  - Clouds in troposphere (shown as white/gray)
  - Ozone layer in upper stratosphere (indicated by shading)
  - Jet stream winds (arrow)
- **Caption:** "Structure of Earth's atmosphere. Most weather occurs in the troposphere, where air circulation is driven by solar heating. The stratosphere, heated from above by ozone's absorption of ultraviolet light, is cold and stable. Beyond 85 km, air is so thin that satellites can orbit without friction."
- **Pedagogical note:** Helps locate ozone (where it absorbs UV) and explains why stratosphere is warmer above than below.

**[FIGURE: Greenhouse Effect — Radiation Diagram]**
- **Incoming solar radiation** (yellow arrows from top, labeled "visible light")
- **Earth's surface** (brown, at bottom)
- **Atmosphere** (thin blue layer)
- **Greenhouse gases** (CO2, CH4, H2O molecules shown as small icons)
- **Infrared radiation** (red arrows, going up from Earth's surface)
- **Some infrared escaping to space** (red arrows going straight up, labeled "some IR escapes")
- **Some infrared absorbed and re-radiated** (red arrows bouncing back down, labeled "absorbed by greenhouse gases and re-radiated back to surface")
- **Heat buildup** (shown as warming of surface and lower atmosphere, color gradient)
- **Caption:** "The greenhouse effect. Solar radiation (visible light) passes through the atmosphere and heats Earth's surface. The surface then emits infrared (heat) radiation. Greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4, H2O) absorb much of this infrared and re-radiate it, trapping heat in the lower atmosphere. More greenhouse gas = warmer surface needed to reach energy balance."
- **Pedagogical note:** Shows mechanism (absorption and re-radiation) not just the term "greenhouse effect."

### Part Five: Life and the Coevolution

**[FIGURE: Stromatolites, Fossil Cross-Section]**
- **Actual fossil stromatolite layering** shown in cross-section
- **Alternating layers** of sediment and microbial material (different textures)
- **Size scale** indicated (e.g., 10 cm, 1 m)
- **Inset photo:** Modern stromatolites in Shark Bay, Australia (for comparison)
- **Caption:** "Stromatolites: layered structures built by cyanobacteria. Fossil stromatolites, found in rocks 3.5 billion years old, are evidence that life existed early in Earth's history. Modern stromatolites still form in a few locations on Earth, showing the same growth pattern. Each layer represents a period of microbial growth and sediment accumulation."
- **Pedagogical note:** Concrete evidence of early life; still-existing forms show mechanism.

**[FIGURE: Tree of Life from Genetic Analysis]**

- **Three main branches:** Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya
- **Eukaryota branch** subdivided into:
  - Protists (many branches, most diversity)
  - Plants (small branch)
  - Fungi (small branch)
  - Animals (small branch)
- **Emphasis:** Most branches are microbial; plants, fungi, animals are small twigs
- **Caption:** "The tree of life, inferred from genetic comparisons. All life on Earth shares a common ancestor. The tree shows that microbial life (bacteria and archaea) represents the vast majority of diversity and evolutionary time. Plants, fungi, and animals are recent branches. The 99.9% genetic similarity between humans and other primates is evident in their proximity on the tree."
- **Pedagogical note:** Shows that life on Earth is dominated by microbes; humans are a small twig.

**[FIGURE: CO2 Concentration Over Time, 1900–2026]**

  - 1900: 295 ppm
  - 1950: 310 ppm
  - 2000: 369 ppm

- **Caption:** "Atmospheric CO2 concentration, 1900–2026. The curve shows acceleration: the increase in the past 75 years exceeds the natural fluctuation of the past 800,000 years. The gray band indicates the natural range; the red region shows the unprecedented rapid increase driven by fossil fuel burning."
- **Pedagogical note:** Visualizes both the absolute level change and the rate (slope), making the word "accelerating" concrete.

### Part Six: Comparative Habitability

**[FIGURE: Mars, Venus, and Earth Compared]**
- **Three globes drawn to scale** (Mars small, Venus and Earth similar size)
- **Distance from Sun** shown by position (Mars farther, Venus nearer, Earth middle)
- **Colors:** Mars (rust-red, barren), Venus (yellow/white clouds, featureless), Earth (blue/green/white, life-bearing)
- **Data boxes for each planet:**
  - **Mars:** radius 3,400 km, mass 1/10 Earth, distance 1.5 AU, magnetic field (weak), atmosphere (thin, mostly CO2), water (frozen underground), surface temp -60°C to 20°C
  - **Venus:** radius 6,000 km, mass similar to Earth, distance 0.72 AU, magnetic field (none), atmosphere (96 bar, CO2 + H2SO4), water (none), surface temp 460°C
  - **Earth:** radius 6,378 km, mass 5.97 × 10^24 kg, distance 1.0 AU, magnetic field (strong), atmosphere (1 bar, N2+O2), water (abundant), surface temp -50°C to 60°C, life (yes)
- **Caption:** "Comparative habitability: Mars, Venus, and Earth. All three formed in the same solar system, but their fates diverged. Mars, small and cool, lost its magnetic field and thus its atmosphere. Venus, close to the Sun, experienced a runaway greenhouse effect and lost its water. Earth, with the right mass, the right distance, an active interior, and magnetic field, maintained conditions for life."
- **Pedagogical note:** Shows that "habitable" is not inevitable — most planets are not.

**[FIGURE: Habitable Zone Around the Sun]**
- **Sun** drawn at center (bright yellow circle)
- **Planets shown in orbital positions:**
  - Mercury (very close, red hot)
  - Venus (close, 0.72 AU, hot)
  - Earth (middle, 1.0 AU, habitable)
  - Mars (far, 1.5 AU, cold)
  - Further planets shown faintly
- **Habitable zone** shaded as a band around the Sun (roughly 0.9–1.6 AU)
- **Arrows and labels:**
  - "Too hot" region (inside Venus' orbit)
  - "Habitable zone" (Earth's region)
  - "Too cold" region (outside Mars' orbit)
- **Temperature curves** showing how insolation decreases with distance
- **Caption:** "The habitable zone is the range of orbital distances where liquid water can exist on a planet's surface. For the Sun, this zone lies between roughly 0.9 and 1.6 AU. Earth sits comfortably in the middle. Venus is too close (too hot); Mars is borderline (too cold, and has lost most of its atmosphere)."
- **Pedagogical note:** Shows that distance from the star sets the baseline; other factors (magnetic field, plate tectonics, life) modulate whether that zone is actually habitable.

## Optional Supplementary Figures (for extended discussion or appendix)

**[FIGURE: Asteroid Impact Sequence — Chicxulub]**
- **Frame 1:** Asteroid approaching, planet surface below
- **Frame 2:** Impact, explosion, dust cloud rising
- **Frame 3:** Days later, dust envelope around entire planet, sun blocked

**[FIGURE: Iridium Layer at K-T Boundary]**
- **Rock strata** shown in cross-section
- **Cretaceous rocks** below (older)
- **Thin layer** with iridium-rich sediment (sharp boundary)
- **Paleogene rocks** above (younger)
- **Magnified view** of the boundary layer, showing iridium crystals (elements that arrived from space)
- **Caption:** "The iridium layer marks the K-T (Cretaceous-Paleogene) boundary. This globally distributed thin layer of sediment is enriched in iridium, a rare element on Earth but common in meteorites. The presence of this layer, worldwide, at exactly the same geological time, proves that a massive extraterrestrial impact occurred 65 million years ago."

**[FIGURE: Outgassing and Weathering Cycle]**
- **Volcano** emitting CO2 (arrows going up)
- **Atmosphere** with CO2 molecules
- **Rain** carrying CO2 (as carbonic acid)
- **Rock weathering** at surface (arrows into ground)
- **CO2 dissolved in water, creating weak acid, eating rock**
- **Sediment washed to ocean**
- **Sediment forming carbonate rocks** on ocean floor (shown as layering)
- **Subduction zone** pulling these rocks down into the mantle (arrows pointing down)
- **Mantle heating** the rock (color gradient indicating heat)
- **CO2 released from carbonate** as rock is heated (arrows pointing up and out of volcano)
- **Cycle complete:** back to atmosphere
- **Caption:** "The carbon cycle on geological timescales. CO2 from the atmosphere is consumed by weathering (rock is eaten by acid rain). The resulting sediments are buried and subducted. Heating in the mantle releases CO2, which is re-emitted by volcanoes. This cycle regulates atmospheric CO2 over millions of years."

**[FIGURE: Climate Feedbacks]**
- **Positive feedback loop (warming loop):**
  - More CO2 → warmer surface
  - Warmer surface → more evaporation
  - More water vapor (greenhouse gas) → more warming
  - Arrow cycle, drawn in red
- **Negative feedback loop (stabilizing loop):**
  - Higher temperature → faster weathering
  - Faster weathering → less CO2 in atmosphere
  - Less CO2 → cooler surface
  - Arrow cycle, drawn in blue
- **Interaction:** Show how positive feedback can overwhelm negative feedback if the rate of change is too fast
- **Caption:** "Climate feedbacks. The water-vapor feedback (positive, red) amplifies warming: warmer air holds more water vapor, a greenhouse gas, leading to further warming. The weathering feedback (negative, blue) stabilizes climate over millions of years: warming accelerates rock weathering, which removes CO2, which cools the surface. If change is too rapid, negative feedback cannot operate fast enough to compensate."

## Technical Specifications for LaTeX Integration

### Color Palette
- **Mantle:** RGB(255, 140, 0) — orange, indicating plasticity and heat
- **Core:** RGB(200, 100, 0) — darker orange, indicating high density and temperature
- **Crust:** RGB(150, 100, 50) — tan/brown
- **Ocean:** RGB(0, 100, 255) — bright blue
- **Atmosphere:** RGB(135, 206, 250) — light blue (sky color)
- **Magma:** RGB(255, 69, 0) — red-orange
- **Ice:** RGB(200, 230, 255) — pale blue

### Font and Labeling
- **Layer names:** 14pt, bold, sans-serif (e.g., "Mantle")
- **Depth/altitude scale:** 11pt, regular, sans-serif
- **Data values:** 10pt, monospace (for numbers like "2,900 km")
- **Arrows:** 2pt stroke weight, arrowhead 6pt

### Resolution and Export
- **Print resolution:** 300 dpi
- **Digital format:** PDF embedded in LaTeX, 600 dpi capture for clarity
- **Aspect ratio:** Figures should be 3.5 inches wide (single-column width for typical textbook)

## Caption Style

All captions follow this pattern:

1. **First sentence:** What the figure shows (concrete, observable)
2. **Second/third sentences:** How to interpret it or what it demonstrates mechanically
3. **Pedagogical note (if needed):** Why this figure matters for understanding

Example (Magnetosphere):
"Earth's magnetosphere shields the planet from the solar wind. The solar wind (charged particles streaming from the Sun) compresses the field on the dayside and stretches it on the nightside. The magnetosphere extends roughly 60,000 km toward the Sun but can reach the Moon's orbit on the opposite side. This shield is why Mars' loss of its magnetic field had catastrophic consequences for its atmosphere."

## Accessibility Notes

- All figures include descriptive captions (not just labels)
- Color is not the only distinguishing feature (patterns/hatching also used for colorblind readers)
- Scales (size, time, temperature) are explicitly labeled
- Arrows show direction of motion or process flow
- Complex figures (like plate tectonics) have inset magnifications for clarity
