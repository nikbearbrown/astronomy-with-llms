# Images and Figures: Black Holes and Curved Spacetime

## Figures Required (OpenStax baseline, adapted for Attenborough × Feynman voice)

### Figure 1: Warped Spacetime Grid Near a Black Hole
**Caption:** Spacetime is not a fixed stage. Mass warps it. Near a black hole, the curvature is so extreme that paths that would go outward instead curve inward. Light rays (geodesics) bend sharply, and the region inside the event horizon has no outward-pointing directions.

**Specifications:**
- 2D grid representing spacetime (x, time axis)
- Massive sphere at center creating a "well" or "saddle" in the grid
- Light rays shown as paths across the grid; some escape, some turn back, some reach the center
- Event horizon marked as the boundary where the curvature becomes critical
- Comparison: flat spacetime (far away) vs. curved spacetime (near black hole)

**Data source:** Theoretical; based on Schwarzschild metric

---

### Figure 2: Schwarzschild Radius Comparison
**Caption:** The Schwarzschild radius depends only on mass. Earth (1 cm), the Sun (3 km), and Sagittarius A* at the galactic center (12 million km) all follow the same formula R_S = 2GM/c². Yet the density near the event horizon varies wildly: stellar-mass black holes are extremely dense; supermassive ones are diffuse.

**Specifications:**
- Scale diagram showing relative sizes and distances
- Earth: 1 cm (smaller than a grape)
- Sun: 3 km (tinier than usual solar size representations)
- Sgr A*: 12 million km (for scale, Mercury orbits at ~58 million km)
- Label the trade-off: small black holes are denser near the horizon; large ones are diffuse

**Data source:** Calculated using Schwarzschild formula

---

### Figure 3: Accretion Disk and X-ray Emission
**Caption:** In a binary system, a black hole's companion star is drawn toward the black hole. Gas spirals inward in an accretion disk, heated by friction to X-ray-emitting temperatures. The flickering X-ray emission is the signature of a black hole.

**Specifications:**
- Binary system: large companion star and invisible black hole
- Stream of gas torn from the star's outer layers
- Accretion disk: pancake of gas orbiting the black hole
- Inner disk: hottest, brightest in X-rays
- Event horizon: tiny, at the center
- X-ray emission shown radiating outward

**Data source:** Conceptual; based on Remillard & McClintock review

---

### Figure 4: LIGO Schematic
**Caption:** The Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory uses laser light to measure distortions in spacetime. A passing gravitational wave stretches one arm and compresses the other, creating a phase shift in the laser light that reveals the wave's passage.

**Specifications:**
- Top view: L-shaped instrument, each arm 4 km long
- Vacuum tubes to avoid air turbulence
- Mirrors at the ends, suspended by wires
- Laser source at the vertex, beam splitter
- Detector monitoring the interference pattern
- Inset: how a gravitational wave deforms spacetime (one direction stretched, perpendicular direction compressed)

**Data source:** LIGO documentation

---

### Figure 5: Gravitational Wave Signal from GW150914
**Caption:** On September 14, 2015, LIGO detected gravitational waves from the merger of two black holes (36 and 20 solar masses). The signal shows the "chirp"—increasing frequency and amplitude as the black holes spiral inward—culminating in the merger. The same signal reached Louisiana ~7 milliseconds before Washington, pinpointing the source direction.

**Specifications:**
- Time series: strain (distortion) vs. time
- The chirp: increasing frequency over ~0.2 seconds
- Merger: sudden spike in amplitude
- Two panels: Louisiana and Washington detections, offset by 7 ms
- Frequency scale: starts around 35 Hz, rises to ~250 Hz at merger

**Data source:** LIGO Scientific Collaboration, PRL 2016

---

### Figure 6: Event Horizon Telescope Image of M87
**Caption:** The Event Horizon Telescope combined signals from radio telescopes across Earth to create an Earth-sized telescope. The image shows the shadow of M87's supermassive black hole (6.5 billion solar masses), surrounded by a bright ring of infalling gas. The asymmetry in brightness (one side brighter) is due to relativistic beaming effects.

**Specifications:**
- Orange/red ring of emission surrounding a dark central region
- The ring is the "photon ring"—the last light paths before they plunge past the event horizon
- Dark region is the event horizon shadow
- Asymmetry labeled: one side brighter due to Doppler beaming toward observer
- Scale bar and angular size

**Data source:** Event Horizon Telescope Collaboration 2019

### Figure 7: Schwarzschild Radius vs. Object Mass
**Caption:** The Schwarzschild radius scales linearly with mass. Plot R_S (in kilometers) vs. M/M☉. A line through the origin shows the relationship; several named objects are marked.

**Specifications:**
- x-axis: Mass in solar masses (logarithmic scale, 1 to 10^10)
- y-axis: Schwarzschild radius in kilometers (logarithmic scale, 10^-6 to 10^8)
- Points: Earth (1 cm, ~6×10^24 kg), Sun (3 km, 1 M☉), Sgr A* (12 million km, 4×10^6 M☉), M87 (20 million km, 6.5×10^9 M☉)
- Linear relationship showing R_S ∝ M

**Data source:** Calculated using Schwarzschild formula

### Figure 8: Tidal Forces Near Black Hole Event Horizon
**Caption:** As an astronaut approaches a black hole feet-first, the gravitational acceleration on the feet exceeds that on the head. For a 3 M☉ black hole, this difference becomes lethal before reaching the event horizon. For Sagittarius A* (4×10^6 M☉), the difference becomes lethal *inside* the event horizon.

**Specifications:**
- Two scenarios side-by-side
- Small black hole (3 M☉): tidal forces kill astronaut *before* event horizon
- Large black hole (4×10^6 M☉): tidal forces kill astronaut *after* event horizon
- Plots showing tidal force difference (F_feet - F_head) vs. distance from singularity
- Inset: astronaut being stretched (spaghettification)

**Data source:** Theoretical calculation; Thorne's Black Holes and Time Warps

---

### Figure 9: Mercury's Perihelion Precession
**Caption:** Mercury's orbit precesses (the major axis rotates) more than Newton's laws predict. General relativity accounts for the extra 43 arcseconds per century caused by the curvature of spacetime around the Sun.

**Specifications:**
- Elliptical orbit of Mercury around the Sun
- Perihelion marked at several successive orbits, showing slow rotation of the orbit
- Zoom on the rate of precession: observed vs. Newton's prediction vs. Einstein's prediction
- Einstein matches observation

**Data source:** Le Verrier 1859, Einstein's calculation; historical astronomy

---

### Figure 10: Light Deflection Near the Sun
**Caption:** Starlight passing near the Sun is deflected by 1.75 arcseconds—not because light is pulled by gravity (it has no mass), but because spacetime is curved there. The star appears in a slightly different direction than it would if the Sun weren't in the way.

**Specifications:**
- Sun at center
- Distant star at far left, true position and apparent position (displaced)
- Light ray path: straight line in flat spacetime; curved path in Einstein's spacetime
- Eddington's 1919 eclipse measurements marked (two sites, both confirming deflection)

**Data source:** Einstein's calculation; Eddington 1919 observations

## Voice Notes for Captions

Captions should be short, specific, and avoid passive constructions:
- Instead of "Stars are pulled into the black hole," write "Gas spirals inward, heated by friction to X-ray temperatures."
- Instead of "The black hole warps spacetime," write "Mass curves the fabric of spacetime; light follows the curves."
- Instead of "Gravitational waves are produced," write "The merger radiates energy as gravitational waves."
