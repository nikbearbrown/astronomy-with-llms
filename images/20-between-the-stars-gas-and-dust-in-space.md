# Image References and Captions: Between the Stars

Images referenced in the converted chapter, with captions and placement guidance.

## Figure 1: NGC 3603 and Its Parent Cloud
**Placement:** Chapter opening (Part 1, opening scene)
**Description:** A star-forming region showing both bright emission from ionized hydrogen (red) and dark lanes of dust silhouetted against the stellar background. Hot young stars are visible in and around the nebula, with their ultraviolet radiation ionizing nearby hydrogen gas.
**Teaching purpose:** Establishes the visual reality of the interstellar medium—showing that it is not invisible, but rather visible in multiple forms (bright and dark regions coexist). Sets the puzzle: what creates the dark lanes, and what is the bright red emission?
**Source:** NASA Hubble Space Telescope or ESO (European Southern Observatory)
**Alternative:** M42 (Orion Nebula) also shows clear H II region with dark dust clouds adjacent to bright ionized gas.

## Figure 2: Barnard 68 (Dark Nebula)
**Placement:** Part 3, section "Blocking Starlight: The Dark Cloud Evidence"
**Description:** A nearly spherical dark cloud approximately 1 light-year in diameter against a background field of stars. The surrounding region shows dense stellar population; the dark cloud's interior shows virtually no visible stars.
**Teaching purpose:** Demonstrates that dust clouds block light so effectively that the region behind them appears nearly empty. The contrast between the star-rich surroundings and the dark interior is striking.
**Source:** NASA/ESO
**Distance:** ~160 light-years
**Note:** This image is particularly useful because it clearly shows the effect of dust obscuration without ambiguity.

## Figure 3: Pleiades Star Cluster (Reflection Nebulae)
**Placement:** Part 3, section "Reflecting Light: The Nebula That Shows Its Source"
**Description:** The seven brightest stars of the Pleiades surrounded by blue nebulosity. The nebulae are brightest around the hottest (bluest) stars and fade toward cooler (redder) cluster members.
**Teaching purpose:** Shows that dust does not always appear dark. When illuminated by nearby stars, dust scatters light and glows. The blue color demonstrates the wavelength dependence of scattering: blue light scatters more efficiently than red, so the nebulae appear blue even though the illuminating stars are white or yellow.
**Source:** Wide-field telescope image, commonly available
**Note:** Excellent example of selective scattering determining observed color.

---

## Figure 4: Horsehead Nebula—Visible vs. Infrared Comparison
**Placement:** Part 3, section "Emitting Heat: The Infrared Signature"
**Description:** Two-panel image showing (left) the Horsehead Nebula in visible light (dark silhouette) and (right) the same region in infrared light (bright due to heated dust). The infrared image reveals structure within and around the dark nebula that is completely invisible in visible light.
**Teaching purpose:** Visually demonstrates that dust clouds that are opaque and dark in visible light become transparent and bright in infrared. Shows the power of observing at different wavelengths. Also shows that the cloud has structure and internal heating.
**Source:** NASA Spitzer Space Telescope (infrared) paired with optical image
**Key lesson:** The same cloud tells different stories depending on the wavelength at which you observe it.

## Figure 5: H II Region (Orion Nebula, M42)
**Placement:** Part 5, "Ionized Gas — The Fluorescent Nebula"
**Description:** A large, bright emission nebula glowing in red light (H-alpha line of hydrogen). Hot stars embedded in the nebula are visible as bright points. The nebula shows structure: bright knots and filaments, indicating varying density and ionization state.
**Teaching purpose:** Shows the appearance of an H II region, which glows because of fluorescence from ionized hydrogen near hot stars. The red color is characteristic. The structure within the nebula shows how the ionization is inhomogeneous, with denser filaments resisting ionization.
**Source:** Wide-field optical telescope (can be ground-based or space-based)
**Note:** This image visually connects to the mechanism explanation in Part 5.

## Figure 6: Vela Supernova Remnant (Hot Gas)
**Placement:** Part 2, section on "The Hot Phase," and Part 5 (context for H II regions)
**Description:** A shell-like or filamentary structure glowing in X-rays and ultraviolet, showing the hot gas ejected by a supernova explosion roughly 11,000 years ago. The structure shows both bright filaments and fainter diffuse gas, indicating a range of temperatures and densities.
**Teaching purpose:** Demonstrates that hot, ionized gas exists in the interstellar medium and originates from supernovae. Shows the scale of supernova energy: the explosion has expanded to a size of several light-years and is still being detected in X-rays.
**Source:** NASA Chandra X-ray Observatory or ROSAT data
**Note:** The connection between stellar death (supernovae) and the heating of the ISM is visually explicit.

## Figure 7: Model of an Interstellar Dust Grain
**Placement:** Part 4, "The Deep Dive — What Dust Is Made Of"
**Description:** A schematic diagram showing a dust grain cross-section: a rocky or carbon core (labeled as silicate or graphite) surrounded by a thicker layer of ice (water, methane, ammonia). The scale should indicate nanometers. Relative sizes should show the ice mantle as substantial (often nearly as thick as the core radius).
**Teaching purpose:** Visualizes the internal structure of dust grains, which cannot be directly observed but is inferred from spectroscopy and laboratory analysis. Shows that dust is not just silicate, but a composite structure with chemically important ices on the surface.
**Source:** Schematic (not observational) diagram
**Illustration best practice:** Use colors to distinguish core from mantle. Include a size scale reference (e.g., "comparable to wavelength of visible light").

## Figure 8: Interstellar Reddening Illustration
**Placement:** Part 3, section "Making Light Red: The Reddening Effect"
**Description:** A diagram showing starlight entering a dust cloud from the left, with blue and green photons being scattered away (shown as arrows leaving the beam) while red and infrared photons pass through more easily (shown as continuing straight). The star at the end appears redder in the telescope image (right side) than expected from its spectrum (left side).
**Teaching purpose:** Visualizes the selective scattering mechanism without requiring heavy physics notation. Shows the conceptual idea: blue is removed, red passes through, therefore the star looks redder.
**Source:** Schematic diagram
**Illustration best practice:** Use color-coded photons (blue, red) to make the wavelength selectivity obvious.

## Figure 9: Formation of the 21-Centimeter Line (Hydrogen Spin Flip)
**Placement:** Part 6, "Neutral Hydrogen — The Hidden Majority," section on the 21-cm line mechanism
**Description:** A diagram showing a hydrogen atom with proton and electron spins aligned (upper) vs. anti-aligned (lower), with an arrow indicating the energy difference and a radio wave symbol at 21 cm wavelength corresponding to that energy difference.
**Teaching purpose:** Visualizes the quantum transition that produces the 21-cm line. Shows that the energy difference is real and corresponds to a specific wavelength (much longer than visible light), explaining why we need radio telescopes to detect this radiation.
**Source:** Schematic diagram
**Illustration best practice:** Use spin vectors or arrows to show aligned vs. anti-aligned configurations. Label the energy difference and the corresponding wavelength.

## Figure 10: Harold Ewen and Edward Purcell (Historical Photo)
**Placement:** Part 6, "Neutral Hydrogen — The Hidden Majority," after the section on the 21-cm line discovery
**Description:** A photograph of the two physicists who made the first detection of the 21-cm line of neutral hydrogen in 1951. Can show them with the receiving antenna used for the observation.
**Teaching purpose:** Connects the history of ISM observation to individuals. Makes the point that crucial discoveries in astronomy often come from unexpected places (here, physicists, not astronomers, made the first detection). Humanizes the science.
**Source:** Physical Review records or MIT archives
**Note:** This is optional but reinforces the narrative of discovery.

## Figure 11: Distribution of Neutral Hydrogen in the Galactic Disk
**Placement:** Part 6, end of section, as a synthesizing figure
**Description:** A map of neutral hydrogen (likely from 21-cm observations) showing the Milky Way's disk in the plane of the galaxy. Shows the concentration in a thin disk, with clear structure and spiral-arm-like features. May include both a face-on and edge-on view to show the thinness of the disk.
**Teaching purpose:** Shows that the neutral hydrogen is not randomly distributed but concentrated in a flat disk structure, which has implications for galactic dynamics and star formation. The edge-on view particularly emphasizes the thinness (less than 300 light-years thick for a disk 100,000 light-years across).
**Source:** 21-cm survey data (ALFALFA or similar modern survey)

## Figure 12: Fullerene (C60) Molecular Structure
**Placement:** Part 7, "Molecular Clouds — Chemistry in the Dark," when discussing large organic molecules
**Description:** A ball-and-stick or space-filling model of the C60 fullerene (buckminsterfullerene), showing the 60 carbon atoms arranged in a truncated icosahedron (soccer ball shape).
**Teaching purpose:** Visualizes the complexity of the largest molecules confirmed in interstellar space. Shows that molecule structure can be non-trivial and elegant. Establishes scale: this is not a small molecule, but it has been detected in space.
**Source:** Standard chemistry textbook or molecular visualization software
**Note:** The symmetry and structure of fullerene is visually striking and helps convey the sophistication of chemistry occurring in the ISM.

## Summary of Placement Strategy

The images are distributed across the chapter to:

1. **Open with a puzzle** (NGC 3603): Multiple forms of the ISM visible simultaneously.
2. **Demonstrate blocking** (Barnard 68): Dust obscuration is visually striking.
3. **Show scattering** (Pleiades, Horsehead comparison): Wavelength-dependent behavior made visible.
4. **Illustrate fluorescence** (Orion, Vela): Glowing gas is the result of stellar radiation.
5. **Support mechanism explanation** (grain model, reddening diagram, 21-cm diagram): Schematic figures clarify physics.
6. **Convey scale and distribution** (H distribution map, fullerene): The ISM is not local or small.
7. **Connect to history** (Ewen and Purcell): The observations are recent and built by real people.

All images use captions that connect to the surrounding text and avoid decorative redundancy.
