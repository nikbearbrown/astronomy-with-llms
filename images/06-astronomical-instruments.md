## Part I: Three Components

**[FIGURE: Schematic of three-component system]**
Flow diagram showing light from a distant object → telescope (collector) → spectrometer or filter (sorter) → CCD detector (recorder) → computer for analysis. Illustrates the universal architecture whether the light is visible, radio, or X-ray.

**[FIGURE: Comparison of 1-meter and 4-meter aperture circles]**
Two circles drawn to scale, one 1 cm and one 4 cm in diameter (representing the actual 1m and 4m mirrors at some reduced scale). Overlay shows the area difference visually. Purpose: make the quadratic scaling rule intuitive.

## Part II: Refraction and Reflection

**[FIGURE: Chromatic aberration in a lens]**
Parallel light rays entering a lens from the left, with blue rays focusing closer to the lens than red rays, creating a split focal point. A resulting star image is shown as a blurred disk with colored fringes. Original reference: optical textbooks on refraction.

**[FIGURE: Mirror vs. Lens: Gravity support]**
Cross-section of a large lens (thin, supported only at edges, showing sag) next to a large mirror (thin, supported from behind at many points). Annotations show why one fails and the other succeeds at scale. Purpose: clarify why mirrors won the competition for large telescopes.

**[FIGURE: Segmented mirror design (Keck style)]**
Top view of 36 hexagonal mirror segments arranged in a honeycomb pattern, with gaps between them labeled as <3mm. Annotations show actuators beneath, controlled by computers. Purpose: show how to build apertures larger than any single piece of glass.

## Part III: Atmospheric Seeing

**[FIGURE: Atmospheric turbulence illustration]**
Parallel light rays from a star passing through a column of atmosphere, with small-scale "blobs" of gas at different temperatures shown as regions that bend the rays. Rays arrive at slightly different angles at the detector, creating a blurred spot. Time-sequence of same image over 1 second showing it jittering. Purpose: make turbulence and seeing concrete.

**[FIGURE: Site elevation vs. seeing]**
Graph showing seeing (in arcseconds) on the y-axis and elevation (in kilometers) on the x-axis. Data points for major observatories: Maunakea (~4.2 km, ~0.4"), Atacama sites (~5.0 km, ~0.3-0.5"), Mount Hopkins (~2.6 km, ~0.7"), La Palma (~2.4 km, ~0.5-0.8"). Purpose: show the relationship between altitude and atmospheric quality.

**[FIGURE: Adaptive optics correction]**
Before and after images of a galaxy or planetary nebula. The "before" shows a blurred halo from atmosphere. The "after" shows sharp detail recovered by adaptive optics. Alongside, a schematic showing the feedback loop: measure distortion → command deformable mirror → apply correction → repeat 500 times per second. Original reference: ESO adaptive optics images, particularly of the Galactic Center.

## Part IV: Detectors

**[FIGURE: CCD pixel array schematic]**
Cutaway diagram of a CCD showing the silicon lattice, incoming photon creating an electron, electrons trapped in potential wells (shown as valleys in an electric potential landscape), and readout circuitry. Purpose: show how photons are counted rather than imaged continuously.

**[FIGURE: Photographic vs. CCD efficiency]**
Bar chart comparing quantum efficiency. Photography: ~1%. CCD visible: 60-80%. CCD infrared (best): >90%. Purpose: make the 60-90-fold improvement in efficiency visually obvious.

## Part V: Multi-Wavelength Astronomy

**[FIGURE: Electromagnetic spectrum with telescope windows]**
Horizontal spectrum from radio (1 meter wavelengths) through microwave, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-ray, to gamma-ray (10⁻¹² meter). Underneath, marked regions showing which telescopes operate at each wavelength. Visual indicators (ground-based = green, space-based = blue, partially blocked = orange). Purpose: organize the entire spectrum and show the patchwork of instruments needed.

**[FIGURE: Same astronomical object at different wavelengths]**
Multi-panel image of a familiar object (the Orion Nebula, galaxy, or supernova remnant) shown in radio, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, and X-ray. Each reveals different structures and components. Purpose: demonstrate that the universe's appearance fundamentally depends on wavelength.

**[FIGURE: Radio interferometry baseline illustration]**
Two radio dishes separated by kilometers, with the baseline vector shown. Incoming plane wave of radio energy shown at different arrival times at the two dishes. Annotation showing how the phase difference depends on baseline, allowing resolution to scale with B rather than individual dish size. Purpose: clarify why interferometry works.

**[FIGURE: ALMA array in the Atacama]**
Photograph or illustration of ALMA's dishes arranged across the high desert. Shows the scale: 66 dishes, some separated by 16 km. Purpose: give concrete sense of size and separation.

**[FIGURE: Infrared detector cryogenic system]**
Cross-section showing nested shields and cooling stages, with temperatures labeled: ~300 K at the telescope structure, ~50 K at first shield, ~20 K at second, ~10 K at detector. Annotation noting liquid helium or passive radiative cooling. Purpose: show why infrared observation requires extreme cooling.

## Part VI: Space-Based Observatories

**[FIGURE: Hubble Space Telescope repair timeline]**
Schematic timeline showing: launch (1990, with blurry optics) → discovery of mirror flaw → astronaut repair mission (1993, installing corrective optics) → subsequent servicing missions adding new instruments. Before/after comparison of Hubble images. Purpose: illustrate the power of servicing and the importance of the repair strategy.

**[FIGURE: Hubble Ultra-Deep Field]**
The actual image showing ~10,000 galaxies in a tiny patch of sky. Annotations pointing out galaxies of different ages and redshifts. Purpose: stunning visual demonstration of what escaped atmosphere permits.

**[FIGURE: James Webb Space Telescope in orbit at L2]**
Deployed Webb at the L2 Lagrangian point, showing sun shield orientation relative to sun and Earth. Annotation of heat flows and radiative balance. Purpose: make clear why L2 is different from Earth orbit for infrared cooling.

**[FIGURE: James Webb first-light images]**
Panel showing several famous early Webb images: galaxy cluster, Carina Nebula, exoplanet spectrum, etc. Annotations explaining what infrared wavelengths reveal about each. Purpose: show what Webb can do that Hubble cannot.

**[FIGURE: High-energy space observatories]**
Photographs or schematics of Chandra X-ray Observatory and Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope. Annotations noting their wavelength bands and key discoveries. Purpose: show that space-based observatories span the spectrum.

## Part VII: Next-Generation Telescopes

**[FIGURE: ELT mirror design]**
Illustration of the European Extremely Large Telescope's 798-segment mirror, shown from above. Shows the hexagonal packing and the computer-controlled actuators beneath. Annotation comparing size to a football field. Purpose: visualize the next-generation segmented design.

**[FIGURE: Telescope size comparison]**
Figure showing outlines of major telescopes drawn to scale: Hale (5.1m), Keck (10m), Hubble (2.4m), JWST (6.5m), ELT (39m), overlaid on a basketball court or football field for scale. Purpose: make clear how large next-generation ground-based telescopes will be.

**[FIGURE: Vera Rubin Observatory field of view]**
Illustration showing the Vera Rubin survey strategy: large field of view (9.6 square degrees, about 20× the full moon), repeated over the entire southern sky on a ~4-night cycle over 10 years. Animation or time-lapse concept showing how the "movie of the sky" accumulates. Purpose: contrast with deep narrow surveys (like JWST); illustrate why wide, shallow surveys find transients.

## Supporting Diagrams

**[FIGURE: Refraction in a lens]**
Parallel rays from left, entering a converging lens, bending toward the center, converging at focal point on the right. Annotations showing focal length, focal point. Purpose: teach the fundamental optical principle.

**[FIGURE: Reflection in a mirror]**
Parallel rays hitting a concave mirror, reflecting back to a focus. Annotations matching the lens diagram. Purpose: show the optical equivalence of mirrors and lenses.

**[FIGURE: Spectroscopy setup (simple prism spectrometer)]**
Light from source → slit → collimating lens → prism (light dispersed by wavelength) → focusing lens → detector (CCD or photographic plate). Annotations showing how different colors are bent by different amounts and end up at different positions. Purpose: show the essential elements of spectroscopy.

**[FIGURE: Doppler shift in spectral lines]**
Rest spectrum showing hydrogen-alpha line at 656.3 nm. Redshifted spectrum showing the same line shifted to longer wavelength (e.g., 660 nm), labeled as "receding at velocity ~1000 km/s". Blueshifted spectrum showing the line shifted shorter. Purpose: show how spectroscopy reveals motion.
