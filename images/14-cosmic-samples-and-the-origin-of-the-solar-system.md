## Part One: Dust in Motion

### Figure 1.1: Meteor Photographed Against Night Sky

**Description:** Photograph or illustration of a meteor streak across the night sky. The meteor should be a bright line, roughly 2–3 degrees in arc length (one-tenth of the sky from horizon to zenith). If the image shows multiple meteors (for August Perseid context), they should radiate from a point below the horizon, illustrating the perspective illusion of the radiant.

**Purpose:** Visual anchor for the opening of "When a Grain Burns Bright." Shows the phenomenon that the reader has likely experienced (or imagined).

**Caption options:**
- "A meteor in the night sky, the visible trace of a dust grain burning as it enters Earth's atmosphere at orbital velocity."
- "The Perseid meteor shower: multiple radiant points converge below the horizon, a perspective illusion from parallel-moving particles."
- "A single meteor, the end of a grain less than a pea-sized, its kinetic energy released as light visible from hundreds of kilometers away."

## Part Two: What the Stones Tell Us

### Figure 2.1: Three Meteorite Types

**Description:** Three side-by-side photographs or illustrations of polished meteorite cross-sections.

**Left panel (Iron meteorite):** A polished surface showing the Widmanstätten pattern—a crystalline lattice visible as interlocking lines that form when iron-nickel alloy cools slowly over billions of years. Colors should be metallic silver with faint gray shadows to show crystal boundaries. Include scale notation: "1 cm" or similar.

**Middle panel (Carbonaceous stone meteorite):** A dark, sooty fragment with visible grains and flecks. The surface should be matte, showing the primitive silicate and carbon-rich composition. Include notation of fissures or visible grain structure.

**Right panel (Gray ordinary chondrite):** A lighter stone, gray-brown, with visible millimeter-scale spheres (chondrules, the rounded silicate particles that formed in the nebula and stuck together). The texture should show these embedded grains clearly.

**Purpose:** Anchor the three visual categories of meteorites so readers can recognize them conceptually.

**Caption:** "Three meteorite types (left to right): iron meteorite showing Widmanstätten crystalline pattern from slow cooling in a planetary core; carbonaceous meteorite, dark and sooty, preserving primitive nebular composition; ordinary chondrite, gray stone with visible chondrules, the mm-scale silicate spheres that formed in the solar nebula."

## Part Three: The Age of the Solar System

### Figure 3.1: Radioactive Decay Curve

**Description:** A standard exponential decay graph plotted on Cartesian axes.

**X-axis:** Time in billions of years (0 to 14 Ga, with markings at 0, 4.5, 9, 13.5).

**Y-axis:** Fraction of original radioactive atoms remaining (0 to 1.0, in increments of 0.25).

**Curve:** An exponential decay curve, starting at (0, 1.0) and asymptotically approaching zero.

**Annotation:** 
- A vertical dashed line at 4.468 billion years labeled "Half-life of U-238 (4.468 Ga)."
- A horizontal dashed line at 0.5 showing where the curve intersects the half-life line.
- A second vertical dashed line at approximately 8.9 Ga showing the second half-life, where the curve intersects 0.25.
- A shaded region between x=0 and x=4.567 labeled "Age of the solar system: 4.567 Ga" with a star marker at that x-value.

**Purpose:** Visual explanation of radioactive decay and why meteorites dated at 4.567 Ga have a specific meaning (one half-life of U-238, plus a small additional fraction of decay).

**Caption:** "Radioactive decay of uranium-238: The curve shows the fraction of original atoms remaining over time. The half-life of U-238 is 4.468 billion years. The solar system age of 4.567 billion years (marked by the star) corresponds to measured uranium-to-lead ratios in primitive meteorites."

## Part Four: The Nebula and Planets

### Figure 4.1: Collapse of Rotating Cloud to Solar Nebula

**Description:** A sequence of four cross-sectional diagrams showing the collapse phase.

**Panel 1 (Initial):** A roughly spherical cloud with spin vectors shown as small arrows around the equator, indicating rotation.

**Panel 2 (Collapse begins):** The cloud is noticeably flattened, slightly disk-like. Spin vectors more prominent. A bright spot at the center indicates the forming star heating the center.

**Panel 3 (Intermediate):** A thin disk shape. The center is very bright (hot nebula and central star). The disk extends outward, thin and nearly flat.

**Panel 4 (Final):** A clear thin disk structure with the Sun at the center. Concentric circles or rings inside the disk indicate the temperature zones (labeled: "Hot region (metals only)," "Intermediate (silicates)," "Cool region (ice)" from center outward).

**Purpose:** Shows the conservation of angular momentum and the flattening mechanism that produces a disk from a spherical cloud.

**Caption:** "Collapse of the solar nebula: As the rotating gas cloud collapses under gravity, angular momentum causes it to spin faster, flattening into a thin disk. The center becomes hot (the forming Sun); the outer regions cool."

### Figure 4.2: Temperature Gradient and Condensation Sequence

**Description:** A diagram showing distance from the Sun (left to right, labeled in AU with markers at 0, 0.5, 1, 3, 5, 10, 20+) and temperature zones (color-coded).

**Layout:** 
- Horizontal axis: distance from Sun in AU
- Vertical axis: temperature in Kelvin (0–2000 K scale on left)
- A curved temperature profile showing hot near the Sun, cooling outward exponentially.
- Color zones overlaid:
  - Red zone (hottest): "Metals condense here"
  - Orange zone: "Silicates (rock) condense"
  - Light blue zone: "Water ice forms"
  - Dark blue zone (coolest): "Methane, ammonia ices form"

**Planetary positions marked along the bottom:**
- Mercury orbit (0.4 AU)
- Venus (0.7 AU)
- Earth (1 AU)
- Mars (1.5 AU)
- Jupiter (5.2 AU)
- Saturn (9.5 AU)
- Beyond (Kuiper Belt, marked as "Icy region")

**Purpose:** Shows why the compositional gradient exists: temperature determines what can condense.

**Caption:** "The condensation sequence in the solar nebula: Materials condense from vapor to solid as temperature drops with distance from the Sun. Metals and silicates form near the hot Sun; water ice forms at Jupiter's distance; methane and ammonia ices form in the outer nebula. This explains why terrestrial planets are rocky and the giant planets are gas-rich."

### Figure 4.3: Protoplanet Growth in Disk

**Description:** A schematic cross-sectional view of the solar nebula with dust particles and growing bodies.

**Panel sequence (4 time steps, left to right):**

**T1 (earliest):** Tiny dust grains distributed throughout the disk, mostly uniform.

**T2:** Grains cluster into pebbles, then km-scale planetesimals. Larger bodies just beginning to form.

**T3:** Multiple protoplanets visible as dark bodies of various sizes, some Mars-sized, some smaller. Gaps or depletions visible around the largest ones where they've cleared material.

**T4 (latest):** A few large protoplanets of lunar-to-terrestrial sizes, surrounded by smaller planetesimals and dust still infalling.

**Labeling:**
- Small arrows show dust infalling toward growing bodies.
- Size scale notation at bottom: 100 km, 1000 km, etc.
- Time labels: "~10,000 years," "~100,000 years," "~1 million years," "~10 million years."

**Purpose:** Illustrates planetary assembly through accretion over millions of years.

**Caption:** "Growth of protoplanets in the solar nebula: Dust grains stick together, forming planetesimals (km-sized), then larger bodies. Gravity-driven accretion—the accumulation of material through collision—grows the protoplanets toward planetary mass within a few million years."

### Figure 4.4: HL Tau Protoplanetary Disk (ALMA Observation)

**Description:** A millimeter-wavelength observation (or accurate illustration based on the ALMA 2014 image) of the HL Tau disk showing concentric rings and gaps.

**Layout:**
- Top-down view of the disk surrounding a central bright spot (the star, HL Tau).
- Multiple concentric rings of bright dust lanes separated by darker gaps.
- At least 4–6 clear rings should be visible, with gaps between them.
- A scale bar at the bottom indicating the size in AU: "10 AU," with markers showing that the innermost rings are at ~0.1–0.3 AU and extend outward to ~20+ AU.

**Annotations:**
- Arrows pointing to a few of the gaps, labeled "Gaps cleared by protoplanets."
- A label indicating the age: "~1 million years old."
- Distance scale showing that the disk extent is comparable to our solar system's inner region.

**Purpose:** Shows planetary formation in progress, providing real-time visual evidence for the theory.

**Caption:** "The protoplanetary disk around HL Tau, observed by the ALMA telescope in millimeter wavelengths (2014): Concentric rings of dust separated by clear gaps indicate protoplanets already large enough to clear their orbital zones. This system is approximately 1 million years old—planets form faster than earlier models predicted."

## Production Notes for Illustrator

1. **Color palette:** Use cool blues and oranges for temperature regions, metallic grays for iron meteorites, dark browns/blacks for carbonaceous meteorites.

2. **Scale accuracy:** HL Tau and the solar nebula diagrams should maintain rough spatial accuracy (distances in AU) so readers can visually compare the protoplanetary disk to our solar system.

3. **Labels:** All labels should be in sans-serif font, clear and legible at reading size. Avoid small text; use legends or captions instead.

4. **Data sources:** Figure 3.1 uses the standard half-life of U-238 (4.468 × 10⁹ years). Figure 4.4 is based on the ALMA collaboration's 2014 HL Tau observations (Brogan et al. and ESO press releases).
