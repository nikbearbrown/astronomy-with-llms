# Cratered Worlds: What the Moon and Mercury Tell Us About the Early Solar System

## Possible titles

- The Architecture of Catastrophe: Why the Moon and Mercury Bear Witness to Cosmic Violence
- Two Scarred Worlds: Impact, Formation, and the Memory of Things
- Cold Stone Keeps the Record: How Cratered Surfaces Reveal the Solar System's Violent Youth

---

## TL;DR

The Moon and Mercury, small airless worlds covered in craters, preserve a history erased on Earth by active geology and erosion. By reading their scarred surfaces — counting craters, dating rocks, measuring isotopes — we learn that the early solar system experienced a heavy bombardment of giant impacts, including one that may have torn the Moon from Earth itself.

---

## Opening: The Footprint That Changed History

July 20, 1969. An astronaut steps onto the Moon. His name is Neil Armstrong. Later, when asked to describe the surface, he says something that sounds almost casual: *"The surface is fine and powdery. I can pick it up loosely with my toe. But I can see the footprints of my boots and the treads in the fine sandy particles."*

That detail — the clarity of a bootprint pressed into dust — is the linchpin of what follows. A footprint means the dust has no air to blow it around. No rain to wash it away. No frost-heave to churn it into mud. Earth preserves nothing that is not buried or cemented. But the Moon preserves everything, because the Moon is dead.

The footprint lasts. And because it lasts, we can read the surface. Because we can read the surface, we learn that this world was remade in violence — not once, but many times. The craters you see on the Moon are not geological decoration. They are a record written in stone. And the story they tell is one of catastrophe so complete that it reshaped the Earth as well.

Mercury, a world of extremes closer to the Sun than any other, tells a version of the same story. Its surface, too, is scarred. Its composition — so metal-rich it is more core than mantle — speaks of violence so severe that it stripped away half its original rocky material. These two worlds, dead by Earth's standards, are alive with information.

## Section 1: Two Worlds, One Pattern

Both the Moon and Mercury are small. Both are airless. Both are geologically dormant — their internal heat exhausted billions of years ago. And both are dominated by a single geological process: impact cratering.

The Moon is one-eightieth Earth's mass. Mercury is one-eighteenth. Compared to Earth, they are toys. Yet they have taught us more about the history of the solar system than Earth itself ever could.

Why? Because they remember.

Earth is a palimpsest — a page written over and over until the original text is erased. The granite peaks of the Rockies have been worn down and rebuilt. Continents have collided and separated. The oldest rocks on Earth's surface are 4 billion years old, and they are geologically active: folded, fractured, heated, recrystallized. The events of Earth's first half-billion years are gone, written over by the process of plate tectonics itself.

The Moon? The Moon has plate tectonics the way a brick has weather resistance. It is one solid body, inert, frozen.

Here is the specification move that matters: **Geologically dead does not mean scientifically dead.** The Moon's inactivity is its value. Because nothing is erased, the surface retains a nearly complete record of impacts that would have arrived and left no mark on Earth at all.

The same applies to Mercury. Its small size gives it extreme temperatures — up to 700 K (430 Celsius) on the daylit side, down to 100 K (-170 Celsius) at night. No atmosphere to buffer the swings. No weather to erase. The surface preserves every impact in stark detail.

Stand this alongside Earth's temperate, active, self-renewing surface, and you see the trade: dynamism against memory. Earth has been remade. The Moon and Mercury bear witness to what happened before the remaking began.

## Section 2: The Moon's Two Faces

In the early days of telescopes, observers noticed that the Moon has two kinds of regions. The dark areas looked like seas — *maria* in Latin, from which we get the word "sea." Mare Tranquillitatis. Mare Imbrium. Sea of Serenity. Sea of Rains. The light areas were called highlands, and astronomers of the time thought they might be continents and oceans, a second Earth.

They were wrong about the oceans. But they were right to notice the difference.

The highlands are lighter in color and heavily cratered. They cover about 83 percent of the Moon's surface, though they are less visible from Earth because they concentrate on the far side. These regions are made of a rock called *anorthosite* — a silicate with low density that floated to the top as the Moon cooled in its early history, much as slag floats on the surface of a smelter.

The maria are the opposite: darker, less cratered, made of basalt — volcanic lava poured out billions of years ago and pooled in vast depressions. These depressions themselves are the signature of something extreme: *impact basins*. Rocks so large, striking so fast, that they excavated holes hundreds of kilometers across and penetrated to the Moon's interior.

The maria fill only 17 percent of the surface, but they are what you see when you look at the Moon from Earth. They are what made ancient observers think there was water. They are what give the Moon the face we recognize, the face that has been imprinted on human art and story for millennia.

Here is the timing that makes the chronology work: The highlands formed very early — between 4.1 and 4.4 billion years ago, within the first few hundred million years of the Moon's existence. The major volcanic eruptions that filled the basins with lava happened much later, primarily between 3.3 and 3.8 billion years ago. Then volcanic activity ceased almost entirely. After that, the Moon was finished. No more lava. No more internal heat reshaping the surface. From 3.3 billion years ago to now — more than three billion years — the Moon has been a fossil.

This matters because it means the maria are younger than the highlands. And younger surfaces have fewer craters. A simple rule: older equals more cratered. More cratered equals older. By counting the holes, you can date the surface without radioactive clocks.

But the highlands are extraordinary. Heavily, densely, saturated with craters. And when scientists brought back samples and measured their ages with radiometric dating — counting the decay of uranium, potassium, thorium — they found that the highlands were only about 200 million years older than the maria. 4.2 billion years versus 3.8 billion years.

Do the math: 200 million year difference, but 10 times more craters.

If the cratering rate had been constant, the highlands would have had to be 10 times older. That would put their formation at 38 billion years ago. But the universe itself is only 13.8 billion years old. Something was wrong with the assumption.

The resolution comes from relaxing the assumption. The impact rate was not constant. Early in solar system history — the first 500 million years, before the major volcanoes on the Moon began — there was a bombardment so intense that it saturated the surface with craters. A "heavy bombardment." After 3.8 billion years ago, the flux of large projectiles fell to much lower levels. The highlands were sculpted by the early violence. The maria were filled by the violence's aftermath — huge basins already made by the impacts, now filled with lava from the Moon's interior.

Two surfaces, two ages: one tells the story of catastrophe, one tells the story of recovery.

## Section 3: How Impacts Actually Work

To understand a crater, you must understand what happens when something moving at hypervelocity strikes solid rock.

The Moon's escape velocity — the minimum speed at which an object, attracted by the Moon's gravity, will strike the surface — is 2.4 kilometers per second. But typical asteroids and comets in the solar system are also moving at 10 kilometers per second or more relative to the Moon. So the impact speed is not the escape velocity alone. It is the escape velocity *plus* the incoming projectile's existing speed. Total: roughly 15-20 kilometers per second or more.

At these speeds, something violent happens. The projectile is not simply a bullet punching a hole. It is a kinetic weapon detonating.

Here is the deep-dive mechanism: When the impact occurs, the projectile penetrates two or three times its own diameter before stopping. In those few seconds — a fraction of a second, really — the kinetic energy is converted into two things: a **shock wave** and **heat**. The shock wave radiates outward through the rock, fracturing and shattering everything in its path. The heat vaporizes most of the projectile and some of the surrounding target material, turning rock into silicate vapor that expands explosively, like a nuclear detonation at ground level.

The size of the excavated crater depends primarily on the projectile's speed. The rule of thumb: the crater is 10 to 15 times the diameter of the impactor.

Imagine a rock the size of a mountain, moving at 20 kilometers per second, striking the Moon. For a few seconds, the entire region around the impact becomes a plasma. Rock turns to vapor. The shock wave radiates outward, cracking and fracturing the target. The expanding vapor creates a hemispherical cavity — a bowl shape. If the impact is large enough, the bottom rebounds slightly, creating a central peak. The rim is lifted by the explosive force. Around the rim, the shock wave causes landslides that create terraces.

The ejecta — the material thrown out by the explosion — falls back to create a rough, hilly region around the crater, typically as wide as the crater diameter. But some ejecta moves faster. It can travel hundreds or thousands of kilometers before it lands, digging secondary craters where it strikes. These secondary craters are themselves deep enough to contain their own internal structures. From a distance, you see rays of bright material extending from the crater — splashed ejecta, the signature of a young impact where the lighter color has not yet been darkened by billions of years of solar radiation.

Grove K. Gilbert, a geologist with the U.S. Geological Survey, proposed this mechanism in the 1890s. He noticed something simple: terrestrial volcanic craters are small and deep and sit at the tops of mountains. Lunar craters are large and shallow with raised rims and flat floors. The only explanation that fits is impact. But even Gilbert did not fully understand why all impact craters are circular. The answer came later: at these extreme velocities, impact direction matters almost not at all. Whether the projectile arrives at a steep angle or a shallow angle, the explosion is nearly symmetrical because the energy release creates a pressure wave that radiates equally in all directions. Circular, therefore, nearly always.

The evidence for this mechanism comes from multiple sources. Engineers who studied bomb and shell craters from World War II found they matched the circular shape and interior structure of lunar craters exactly. Computer simulations of hypervelocity impacts produce the same crater shapes. And the samples brought back from the Moon show shocked quartz and other evidence of extreme pressure and temperature — proof that impact, not volcanism, made these features.

## Section 4: Reading the Surface — Crater Counting and the Cratering Chronology

Here is a practical method for dating a planetary surface: count the craters.

The method works only where geological activity has stopped — where new processes are not erasing or burying the old craters. It works on the Moon. It works on Mercury. It does not work on Earth, where rain and ice and life are constantly reshaping the surface.

To use the method, you need a calibration: how many craters per unit area correspond to how much time? The cratering rate cannot be measured directly — the time between large impacts is longer than human civilization has existed. But it can be estimated two ways.

First, you can count the craters visible on the lunar maria and calculate how long it would have taken to produce that many at various assumed impact rates. You can estimate the rate from the asteroids and comets currently in the solar system — count the objects, estimate their collision probability with the Moon, calculate how often an impact of a given size occurs.

Both methods give roughly the same answer: roughly one crater one kilometer in diameter every 200,000 years. One 10-kilometer crater every few million years. A 100-kilometer crater every billion years or so.

Now: take that rate and apply it backward. If the maria have the number of craters we observe, and the rate is constant, how long did it take to make them? Answer: 3.3 to 3.8 billion years. The radioactive dating of samples from the maria gave the same answer: 3.3 to 3.8 billion years old.

Two independent methods agreeing is powerful. It suggests the assumption of a constant cratering rate is correct — at least for the past 3.8 billion years. And it means that the current population of asteroids and comets has been striking planetary surfaces at roughly constant rate for billions of years.

But the highlands break the pattern. They are only about 200-400 million years older than the maria, yet they contain roughly 10 times more craters. If the rate were constant, they would have to be 10 times older — 38 billion years. That is impossible.

The resolution: the rate was not constant. In the early history of the solar system, the bombardment was much heavier. The highlands accumulated most of their craters during that period of "heavy bombardment." After 3.8 billion years ago, the number of large projectiles in the inner solar system fell sharply. The bombardment tapered. By the time the media were forming and being filled with lava, the rate had dropped to the lower, more consistent level we observe today.

This is one of the major findings of lunar science: **the early solar system was catastrophic.** Large bodies were colliding with planets. Objects of planetary mass may have been destroyed or fragmented. The worlds we see today — Earth, Mars, Mercury, the Moon — may have been rearranged and reformed by impacts that were orders of magnitude larger than anything we can imagine today.

## Section 5: The Moon's Origin — A Giant Impact

If the early solar system was so chaotic, how did the Moon form?

For a long time, scientists proposed three possibilities: the fission theory (the Moon split off from Earth), the sister theory (the Moon formed alongside Earth), or the capture theory (the Moon formed elsewhere and was captured). Each had fatal problems.

The capture theory is the easiest to reject. To capture a large object into a circular orbit requires a catastrophic loss of energy — something the Moon would not experience drifting through the solar system. Moreover, if the Moon were a completely separate object that formed elsewhere, we would expect it to have very different composition from Earth. But oxygen isotope ratios in Moon rocks match Earth's oxygen isotope ratios too closely. The Moon cannot be a stranger.

The fission theory — that the Moon split from a spinning Earth — fails modern calculations. Spin a ball of rock fast enough to fission off a piece, and you get an unstable system. The ejected piece would not settle into a stable orbit. And again, we would expect a Moon made entirely of terrestrial material to have the chemical properties of Earth, not the strange deficiencies we observe.

The sister theory — that Earth and Moon formed together in the early solar system — struggles with the same problem: if they formed together from the same material, the Moon should have a large iron core like Earth. It does not. The Moon is depleted in metals. It is depleted in volatiles. It is different.

Enter the giant impact hypothesis.

The logic is this: suppose the early solar system did indeed contain objects of planetary mass. Suppose one of them — a body roughly the mass of Mars, perhaps 10 percent of Earth's mass — collided obliquely with the young Earth. The impact would be nearly as large as the planet could experience without being shattered entirely.

What happens? The energy released is so extreme that vast amounts of material are ejected into space — perhaps several percent of Earth's mass, mostly from the rocky mantles of Earth and the impacting object, not from their dense iron cores. This hot material cools and forms a ring orbiting Earth. That ring of debris gradually accumulates through collisions and gravity. It condenses into the Moon.

This hypothesis solves the major problems. First, the Moon's raw material comes from the mantles of Earth and the impactor, not from the cores. That explains why the Moon is iron-poor. It is made of silicate rock, the stuff of mantles, not the stuff of cores.

Second, the volatiles are lost during the high-temperature chaos after the impact. Rock is vaporized. Light elements escape. By the time the Moon has accumulated from the debris ring and cooled, most volatile compounds have been driven away. That explains the Moon's deficiency in water and other volatile materials.

Third, because the Moon is derived primarily from terrestrial mantle material, it shares the isotopic signature of Earth — matching oxygen ratios, for example. It is not a captured outsider. It is made from Earth's own substance, chemically modified by the extreme conditions of the impact.

The giant impact hypothesis is not proven — we cannot yet show that it is the only explanation. But it offers plausible answers to problems that fission, capture, and sister theories could not solve. It explains the Moon's composition. It explains its formation early in solar system history, during the period of heavy bombardment we have already documented. And it connects the Moon's existence to the same catastrophic processes that reshaped Mercury, Mars, and the Earth itself.

## Section 6: Mercury — Extreme Worlds Reveal Their Origins

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun. It orbits in 88 Earth days, moving at 48 kilometers per second. This makes it the fastest planet in the solar system. It is also small — only 4,878 kilometers in diameter, less than half of Earth's width. It is made mostly of rock, but with an unusually large iron core.

Here is the puzzle: Mercury should have formed with a composition similar to Earth or Venus — a balance of metals and silicates typical of terrestrial planets. Yet Mercury is 60 percent iron by mass. Its core extends to within 700 kilometers of the surface. Mercury is essentially an iron ball the size of the Moon, wrapped in a thin rocky crust.

How did it lose so much of its rocky material?

There are two leading explanations, and both involve catastrophe.

**The first:** Mercury experienced one or more giant impacts early in its history, comparable to the impact that made the Moon. But on Mercury, the impact was so violent that it did not just eject material. It stripped away a substantial portion of Mercury's mantle and crust, leaving behind the dense iron core. This is plausible — Mercury, being small and close to the Sun in the early solar system, would have been a target for projectiles throughout the heavy bombardment era.

**The second:** The young Sun was hot and active. Radiation from the young star could have evaporated the lighter, more volatile silicates from the material that formed Mercury, leaving the denser metals behind. Over time, as the solar material disk cooled and thinned, Mercury accumulated from material already depleted in silicates.

Both explanations hinge on extreme conditions in the early solar system. The first invokes impacts. The second invokes stellar heat and radiation. Neither suggests a gentle formation. Mercury's composition is a scar or a burn mark.

The surfaces of Mercury and the Moon look remarkably similar. Both are heavily cratered. Both preserve the impact record back to the early bombardment. Mercury's craters are even more remarkable for their clarity — because Mercury is hotter and drier even than the Moon, smaller craters are less subject to erosion and weathering.

But Mercury has features the Moon does not. Long scarps — cliffs that can be a kilometer or more high and stretch hundreds of kilometers across the surface. These scarps cut across craters, which means they formed after the craters. They appear to be the result of compression. At some point in Mercury's history — the evidence suggests within the last 4 billion years — the planet shrank. Its crust wrinkled like a drying apple. The scarps are the creases.

A world shrinking. A world cooling. A world whose surface tells of violence past and structural contraction that continued long after impact bombardment had ceased.

Mercury also has something surprising: water ice in permanently shadowed craters at the poles. This seems impossible. Mercury orbits closer to the Sun than any other planet. Its daytime temperature climbs above the boiling point of water. Yet the poles, due to the planet's oblique axis and the geometry of the craters, receive almost no direct sunlight. The bottoms of polar craters are cold enough — cold traps, scientists call them — that water brought to Mercury by comets and asteroids has been preserved for billions of years, frozen in shadow while the rest of the surface is being roasted.

## Section 7: What We Know, What We Don't

The Apollo program, which landed humans on the Moon between 1969 and 1972, brought back nearly 400 kilograms of lunar samples. These rocks have been studied intensively for more than 50 years. We have cut them, heated them, bombarded them with radiation, analyzed them with instruments that did not exist when they were collected. The Moon rocks teach us about the Moon's formation, age, composition, and geological history in ways that no telescope observation could match.

Yet Apollo was cut short. The last human left the Moon in December 1972, and humans have not returned in more than 50 years. Political and economic pressures ended the program when it was still in full stride. The giant rockets that had taken people to the Moon were left to rust. Only recently have some been moved indoors to museums.

In that half-century gap, robotic spacecraft from multiple nations have renewed lunar science. NASA's Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter has mapped the surface in exquisite detail. China has landed rovers and sample-return missions. India has crashed spacecraft into the Moon to confirm the presence of water ice. NASA's recent LCROSS mission deliberately crashed a spacecraft into a lunar polar crater at 9,000 kilometers per hour — an impact with the energy of two tons of dynamite. The plume of vaporized material and ejecta was visible to orbiting spacecraft. It confirmed what radar had suggested: water ice really is in those cold traps, perhaps hundreds of billions of tons of it.

The quantity is deceptive. As a liquid, it would only fill a lake 100 miles across. But compared to the rest of the dry lunar crust, it is remarkable. It is enough to sustain a lunar base. It is enough to split into hydrogen and oxygen and manufacture rocket fuel. It is enough to imagine future humans living on the Moon.

Yet the Moon still holds mysteries. The exact mechanism of the giant impact — did it happen once or multiple times? Did multiple smaller impacts accumulate the debris into the Moon, or one catastrophic event? How did the Moon's interior heat engine run, producing volcanism for three billion years, and then switch off almost entirely? We know more about the Moon than about any other world in the solar system except Earth. And we are still not finished learning.

Mercury remains more difficult. Only two spacecraft have visited — Mariner 10 in 1974 and MESSENGER in the 2010s. We do not have samples from Mercury. We do not have seismometers on the surface. We do not have rovers roaming the equator measuring magnetic fields and subsurface density. The knowledge we have comes from remote sensing — photographs and spectroscopy from orbit, radar from Earth, gravitational measurements.

The BepiColombo spacecraft, a joint European-Japanese mission, is currently en route to Mercury and will arrive in late 2025. It will map the surface at higher resolution than ever before. It will measure Mercury's internal structure with unprecedented precision. Perhaps it will finally explain the scarps — whether they are the result of compression from a shrinking planet, or evidence of interior cooling, or both.

## Section 8: Synthesis and Scale

Begin with a footprint in lunar dust. Step back. See a surface preserved, unchanged, for billions of years.

Count the craters on that surface. More craters means older. The highlands are crater-saturated. The maria are less so. The difference tells a story: early bombardment, then quiet, then volcanic activity in the depression left by the bombardment.

Step back further. Not just the Moon, but Mercury. Another small, airless world. Another scarred surface. Another cratering chronology that tells the same story: the early solar system was chaotic. Large bodies were colliding. The worlds we see — Earth, Mars, Mercury, the Moon — may have been remade by impacts.

Step back even further. The Moon's origin. The giant impact hypothesis. A Mars-sized body colliding with Earth. Material ejected into orbit. A ring of debris coalescing into the Moon. The Moon did not form gently. It was born from catastrophe. Its composition is the signature of that catastrophe — depleted in iron because the core of the impactor sank into Earth. Depleted in volatiles because they burned away in the heat. Made of Earth's own mantle because that is what was splashed into orbit.

This is the scale oscillation that matters. We begin with a human standing on the lunar surface, able to see his own footprint. We end with an understanding that the human, the Moon beneath his feet, and the Earth he came from are all products of a collision so violent that it nearly tore the planet apart.

The Moon, long thought to be a dead world, is revealed as a record of violence. Mercury, burning on the Sun-facing side and frozen on the dark side, is revealed as a world that may have been stripped of its mantle by impacts and compressed by its own cooling. Neither world has oceans or life or weather. But both worlds have memory.

And that memory, read correctly, tells us that the solar system in which we live was formed in chaos, not calm. The processes that made the Moon and Mercury and shaped Earth are the same processes that continue today — smaller, rarer, but still present. An asteroid millions of kilometers away right now might be on a trajectory that, in thousands or millions of years, will bring it to Earth. The impact that made the Moon is not impossible. It is simply rare.

The score of impacts written on the Moon's surface is the solar system's autobiography. And the autobiography says: I was born in violence. I am still learning what that means.

## Worked Example: Using Crater Counts to Date a Surface

**Problem:** Suppose an orbital image shows a small lunar region containing 200 craters larger than 1 kilometer in diameter. The region covers 50 square kilometers. Using the cratering rate of approximately 1 crater per square kilometer per 200,000 years for craters 1 km and larger, estimate the age of this surface.

**Solution:**

Step 1: Calculate the crater density (craters per square kilometer).
- Number of craters: 200
- Area: 50 km²
- Density: 200 craters ÷ 50 km² = 4 craters/km²

Step 2: Use the calibrated rate to estimate time.
- The rate is 1 crater/km² per 200,000 years.
- If we have 4 craters/km², we need 4 times the standard time unit.
- Age: 4 × 200,000 years = 800,000 years

**Answer:** The surface is approximately 800,000 years old.

**Note:** This is a minimum age — it assumes no craters have been erased by weathering or new impacts. On the Moon, where erosion is negligible, this is a good assumption. On Earth or Mars, where erosion and newer impacts can erase older craters, the method is less reliable.

**Application:** The Apollo 15 landing site at Mare Imbrium has roughly 3 craters per square kilometer larger than 1 km in diameter. Using the calibrated rate, estimate its age and compare it to the measured age of 3.3 billion years. Discuss why your estimate might differ from the measured age.

## Summary

The Moon and Mercury are small, airless worlds whose surfaces have been preserved in a way Earth's surfaces cannot. By reading their craters, scientists have learned that the early solar system experienced intense bombardment by asteroids and comets, particularly in the first 500 million years. Crater counts reveal surface ages: older surfaces have more craters. The Moon's two major terrains — highlands and maria — tell the story of early bombardment followed by later volcanic activity.

The Moon itself likely formed from debris ejected during a giant impact of a Mars-sized object with the early Earth. This impact explains the Moon's depleted composition (lacking an iron core and volatile elements) while preserving its chemical kinship with Earth (matching oxygen isotope ratios). Mercury, with its anomalously large iron core, may have lost its rocky mantle to giant impacts or to solar heating in the early system. Both worlds bear witness to a chaotic early solar system — a system remade by collisions that no longer occur at the scales required to significantly alter the present-day planets.

---

## What Would Change My Mind

If we discovered that crater saturation is not a reliable chronometer — that is, if the cratering rate varied by orders of magnitude even during the current epoch (the past 3 billion years) — it would undermine the entire crater-dating framework and require a complete recalibration of lunar and planetary timescales.

---

## Still Puzzling

I do not yet fully understand how the Moon's interior heat engine sustained volcanism for three billion years and then switched off so abruptly. The calculations that model thermal evolution show the Moon should have cooled faster than it did. Whether the explanation involves impacts, internal composition, or some process we have not yet identified remains an open question.
