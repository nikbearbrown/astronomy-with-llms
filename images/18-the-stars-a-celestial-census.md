### Figure 2: Selection Effect — Brightest vs. Nearest Stars
**Type:** Conceptual comparison
**Purpose:** Illustrate the difference between magnitude-limited and distance-limited surveys

**Narrative connection:** The chapter opens with the brightness trap — the naked-eye sky is dominated by distant luminous stars, not nearby ones. This figure illustrates why.

**Visual elements needed:**
1. A map showing the local solar neighborhood (21 LY sphere) with nearby red dwarfs marked.
2. The same region showing which stars are visible to the naked eye (very few).
3. A larger sphere showing the 1000+ LY distance needed to make luminous O, B, A stars visible without telescopes.

### Figure 3: Binary Star Orbits — Center of Mass
**Type:** Diagram
**Source:** OpenStax Astronomy, adapted
**Purpose:** Show the mechanism of binary star motion

**Visual elements:**
1. Two stars of different sizes (representing different masses).
2. The center of mass point, offset toward the more massive star.
3. Orbital paths showing the more massive star in a tighter orbit, the less massive star in a larger orbit.
4. Velocity vectors showing that the less massive star moves faster.

**Key equation visible:** Label the center of mass and show how mass ratio determines orbital radius ratio.

### Figure 4: Spectroscopic Binary — Doppler Shift Pattern
**Type:** Diagram with spectral lines
**Source:** OpenStax Astronomy
**Purpose:** Show how Doppler shifting of spectral lines reveals a spectroscopic binary

**Visual elements:**
1. Top panel: Two stars orbiting, one approaching Earth (blue arrow), one receding (red arrow).
2. Middle panels: Composite spectrum at different orbital phases.
   - Star A approaching → blueshift (lines shifted left).
   - Star B receding → redshift (lines shifted right).
   - Both lines visible and separated.
3. Bottom panel: Radial velocity curve showing the oscillation pattern of the Doppler shift over time.

**Key insight:** The double-line pattern and its oscillation reveal both the period and the velocity amplitudes.

### Figure 5: Radial Velocity Curve for Spectroscopic Binary
**Type:** Graph
**Purpose:** Show how velocity oscillates as the binary orbits

**Axes:**
- X-axis: Time (typically in units of orbital period, 0 to 1 and beyond).
- Y-axis: Radial velocity (km/s, positive = moving away, negative = approaching).

**Pattern:** Sinusoidal oscillation. The amplitude gives the velocity, the period gives the orbital period.

### Figure 6: Sirius Binary System Illustration
**Type:** Diagram
**Purpose:** Show the relative sizes and properties of Sirius A and Sirius B

**Visual elements:**
1. Sirius A: Large, bright yellow star (representing main-sequence A-type star).
2. Sirius B: Tiny white dot nearby (representing white dwarf companion).
3. Scale bars indicating actual relative sizes.
4. Labels showing:
   - Separation: ~20 AU
   - Period: ~50 years
   - Sirius A mass: ~2.2 solar masses
   - Sirius B mass: ~1.0 solar mass

**Key insight:** White dwarfs are far smaller than regular stars but carry comparable or greater mass.

### Figure 8: The Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram (Labeled)
**Type:** Plot with stars
**Purpose:** Show the main populations and regions

**Regions marked:**
1. **Main Sequence:** Diagonal band from upper-left to lower-right.
   - Label specific stars: Sun (at G, L=1), Sirius A (at A, L=23), Vega (at A), Procyon (at F).
2. **Giant Region:** Upper-right corner.
   - Examples: Aldebaran (K0 giant), Arcturus.
3. **Supergiant Region:** Upper-left corner.
   - Examples: Betelgeuse (M supergiant), Rigel (B supergiant).
4. **White Dwarf Region:** Lower-left corner.
   - Examples: Sirius B, Procyon B, 40 Eridani B.

**Distribution:** Show ~90% of stars on main sequence, ~9% white dwarfs, ~1% giants/supergiants.

### Figure 10: Mass-Luminosity Relation Plot
**Type:** Scatter plot with power-law fit
**Purpose:** Show empirically measured relation between mass and luminosity

**Axes:**
- X-axis: Mass (M☉, log scale).
- Y-axis: Luminosity (L☉, log scale).

**Data points:** Multiple stars with known masses (from binary measurements) plotted.

**Fitted line:** Power law $L \propto M^{3.9}$, shown as a straight line on the log-log plot.

**Outliers:** Mark the ~10% of stars that deviate from the relation (white dwarfs, giants, brown dwarfs).

**Key insight:** The tight correlation for main-sequence stars validates the mass-luminosity relation; outliers tell interesting stories about stellar evolution.

### Figure 11: Stellar Sizes Comparison
**Type:** Scale diagram
**Purpose:** Show the relative sizes of different types of stars

**Stars shown:**
1. Red dwarf (M-type): Small sphere, ~0.1 solar radii.
2. Sun (G-type): Reference sphere, 1 solar radius.
3. Sirius A (A-type): Larger sphere, ~1.7 solar radii.
4. Red giant: Much larger, ~10-100 solar radii (e.g., Aldebaran).
5. White dwarf: Tiny sphere, ~0.01 solar radii (Earth-sized).
6. Betelgeuse (red supergiant): Enormous, ~700-900 solar radii (orbit of Mars shown for scale).

**Scale bar:** Clearly label which represents 1 solar radius.

### Figure 12: Eclipsing Binary Light Curve
**Type:** Graph
**Purpose:** Show how a binary eclipse produces a dip in brightness

**Axes:**
- X-axis: Time (orbital phase, 0 to 1 and beyond).
- Y-axis: Brightness (relative flux).

**Light curve features:**
1. First contact: Brightness begins to dip.
2. Second contact: Eclipse becomes total.
3. Totality: Constant lower brightness.
4. Third contact: Secondary star begins to emerge.
5. Last contact: Eclipse ends, brightness returns to normal.

**Two eclipses per orbit:** If the orbit is edge-on and both stars are bright, you see two eclipses (one of each star by the other).

## Caption Guidelines

Each figure in the chapter should include:
1. **Figure number and title**
2. **Brief description** (1-2 sentences) of what the figure shows
3. **Key elements** (what to look for)
4. **Connection to text** (how it relates to the surrounding discussion)

Example:
> **Figure 8: The Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram**
> 
> This plot shows luminosity (vertical axis) against surface temperature (horizontal axis, increasing to the left). Stars cluster into distinct regions: the main sequence (diagonal band), giant stars (upper-right), supergiants (upper-left), and white dwarfs (lower-left). The position of a star on this diagram reveals its mass and life stage. Approximately 90% of all stars lie on the main sequence, spending most of their lives fusing hydrogen in their cores.
