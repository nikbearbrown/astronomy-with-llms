**Type:** Line graph overlay

- Y-axis: Intensity (arbitrary units), 0–1

- Arrow or annotation showing "hotter → shorter wavelength"
**Caption:** "Temperature determines both the peak wavelength (Wien's law) and the shape of the blackbody radiation curve. ### Figure 3: Three Types of Spectra
**Location:** Section 3.4, "Emission, Absorption, and Continuum Spectra"

### Figure 4: Hydrogen Atom Energy Levels and Transitions
**Location:** Section 4.2, after "The Bohr Atom" subheading
**Type:** Energy level diagram
**Content:**
- Vertical axis: Energy (eV), from −13.6 (bottom, n=1) to −0.9 (top, n=4), with intermediate levels at −3.4 (n=2) and −1.5 (n=3)
- Horizontal lines representing each allowed orbit/level, labeled n=1, n=2, n=3, n=4
- Arrows showing transitions:
  - n=2 → n=1: Lyman-alpha, 121 nm, UV (labeled, pointing down, ultraviolet zone shaded)
  - n=3 → n=2: Balmer-alpha (H-alpha), 656 nm, red (labeled, pointing down, visible zone shaded in red)
  - n=4 → n=2: Balmer-beta (H-beta), 486 nm, blue-green (labeled, pointing down, visible zone shaded in cyan)
  - Ground state (n=1) labeled as "ionization threshold at 13.6 eV"
**Annotations:**
- Series names: "Lyman series" (ending at n=1), "Balmer series" (ending at n=2), "Paschen series" (ending at n=3, shown as dashed lines for higher transitions)
- Energy differences labeled for key transitions
**Caption:** "Electrons in hydrogen atoms can only occupy specific orbits with fixed energies. Transitions between orbits release photons with wavelengths determined by the energy difference: hf = E₂ − E₁. The Balmer series (ending at n=2) produces the visible hydrogen lines; Lyman (n=1) is ultraviolet; Paschen (n=3) is infrared. Different elements have different energy level spacings, producing different spectral lines."

### Figure 5: Doppler Shift in Spectral Lines
**Location:** Section 3.5, "Doppler Shifts — Reading Motion in Light"
**Type:** Spectral line comparison or schematic
**Content:**
- Three spectral line profiles side-by-side:
  - **Left (Approaching star):** Narrow line, BLUE-SHIFTED (moved left toward shorter wavelength), labeled "toward us, v < 0, blueshift"
  - **Center (Stationary star):** Line at rest position, labeled λ₀ (656.3 nm for H-alpha reference), "at rest, v = 0"
  - **Right (Receding star):** Line RED-SHIFTED (moved right toward longer wavelength), labeled "away from us, v > 0, redshift"
- Wavelength scale at bottom, marked in nm
- Arrows showing direction of shift
- Optional: Graph showing velocity vs. Δλ/λ with linear relationship
**Caption:** "When a source of light moves toward us, its spectral lines shift toward shorter wavelengths (blueshift). When it moves away, they shift toward longer wavelengths (redshift). The magnitude of the shift (Δλ/λ) is proportional to velocity. This simple measurement revealed that galaxies beyond the Milky Way are moving away from us, leading to the Big Bang theory."

### Figure 6: Inverse Square Law for Light
**Location:** Section 1.4, "Light and Distance"
**Type:** Geometric diagram
**Content:**
- Light source (bulb, star, point) at center
- Three expanding spheres around it, showing:
  - Sphere at distance r: surface area 4πr², intensity I
  - Sphere at distance 2r: surface area 4π(2r)² = 16πr², intensity I/4
  - Sphere at distance 3r: surface area 4π(3r)² = 36πr², intensity I/9
- Arrows radiating outward showing light spreading equally in all directions
- Concentric circles or 3D rendering of spheres
- Annotations: "Total energy is conserved; intensity = energy / area"
**Caption:** "Light from a point source spreads uniformly in all directions, covering an expanding sphere. Since the surface area of a sphere is 4πr², the intensity (energy per unit area) falls as 1/r². This is why distant stars look so faint: Alpha Centauri A is 270,000 times farther than the Sun, so it appears 7.3 × 10¹⁰ times fainter — despite radiating about the same total power."

### Figure 7: Spectral Line Broadening (Rotation and Pressure)
**Location:** Section 3.6, "What Spectral Lines Reveal"
**Type:** Spectral line profiles (cross-section)
**Content:**
- Three line profiles overlaid or side-by-side:
  - **Narrow line (unbroadened):** Sharp Gaussian profile, labeled "Low pressure, no rotation"
  - **Broadened symmetric line:** Wider profile, labeled "High pressure (collisional broadening)" or "Rapid rotation (Doppler broadening)"
  - **Asymmetric line:** Skewed profile, labeled "Stellar wind or mass loss (one-sided broadening)"
- Wavelength scale at bottom
- Optional: Small schematic showing rotating star with blue-shifted (approaching) and red-shifted (receding) hemispheres producing the broadening
**Caption:** "Spectral lines are broadened by collisions between atoms in dense gas (pressure broadening) and by the Doppler effect in rotating stars (one side approaching, one side receding). By measuring line width and shape, astronomers infer rotation speed, atmospheric pressure, and evidence of stellar winds."

## Design Principles for All Figures

1. **Clarity over decoration:** Use clean lines, readable fonts, high contrast. Avoid unnecessary 3D perspective.
2. **Color intentionality:** Use the visible spectrum colors where relevant (red for H-alpha, cyan for H-beta). Grayscale acceptable for energy diagrams.
3. **Label all axes and constants:** Every quantity should be labeled with units.
4. **Captions that teach:** Each caption should explain what the figure shows and why it matters for understanding the chapter concept.
5. **Accessibility:** Ensure figures are readable at print size (8.5" × 11") and on screen. Avoid red-green color dependencies for colorblind readers.
