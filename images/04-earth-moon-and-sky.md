## FIGURE 1: Earth in Orbit Showing Distance and Tilt Orientation

**Location in chapter:** Section 1 (after "The explanation lies in angle")

**Purpose:** Establish that distance varies by only 3% while tilt is constant at 23.5 degrees.

**Elements:**
- Sun at center
- Earth's orbit as ellipse with eccentricity ~0.0167
- Distances marked: perihelion (~147.1 M km) and aphelion (~152.1 M km) with percentage difference noted
- Earth's axis shown as tilted arrow (23.5° from perpendicular to orbital plane)
- Arrow direction constant throughout orbit (pointing to same background star location)
- Northern and Southern hemispheres shaded or color-coded
- Key dates marked: June 21 (summer solstice NH), December 21 (winter solstice NH), March 21 and September 21 (equinoxes)

**Caption:** "As Earth orbits the Sun, its distance varies by only 3%, but the tilt of its axis remains constant at 23.5°. This fixed tilt, not the changing distance, drives the seasons. Note that Earth is closest to the Sun in early January—during the Northern Hemisphere's winter."

## FIGURE 2: Sun Angle and Light Intensity Comparison

**Location in chapter:** Section 2 (after "That 47-degree swing...")

**Purpose:** Illustrate how the same amount of solar energy spreads over different areas depending on the angle of incidence.

**Elements:**
- Two diagrams side by side: one for high sun angle (summer), one for low sun angle (winter)
- Sun rays shown as parallel lines hitting Earth's surface
- Each diagram shows a circular area of ground with the same number of sun rays hitting it
- Summer diagram: rays hit at steep angle, concentrated in a small area
- Winter diagram: same rays hit at shallow angle, spread over a larger area
- Relative intensity values noted: summer ~0.96× max, winter ~0.45× max, ratio 2.1:1
- Numerical altitude values included: June at 40°N = 73.5°, December = 26.5°

**Caption:** "Sunlight at a steep angle (summer) concentrates energy on a smaller area and arrives more directly. Sunlight at a shallow angle (winter) spreads the same energy over a larger area and must pass through more atmosphere. At 40°N latitude, the summer Sun has 2.1 times the intensity of the winter Sun due to angle alone."

## FIGURE 3: Day Length Throughout the Year at Different Latitudes

**Location in chapter:** Section 3 (after "Move north. At 45°N...")

**Purpose:** Show how day length changes with latitude and season.

**Elements:**
- Horizontal axis: months (J F M A M J J A S O N D)
- Vertical axis: hours of daylight (0 to 24)
- Multiple curves for different latitudes: 0° (equator), 30°N, 45°N, 60°N, 90°N (North Pole)
- Equatorial curve (0°) is flat at 12 hours year-round
- 30°N curve: gentle undulation, ~13.5 hours max, ~10.5 hours min
- 45°N curve: pronounced undulation, ~15 hours max (June), ~9 hours min (December)
- 60°N curve: extreme undulation, ~18.5 hours max, ~5.5 hours min
- North Pole curve: step function, 0 hours from Sept 21 to March 21, 24 hours from March 21 to Sept 21

**Caption:** "Day length varies dramatically with latitude. At the equator, the Sun is always above the horizon for 12 hours. At higher latitudes, the contrast between summer and winter day length grows extreme. At the North Pole, there are exactly 6 months of continuous daylight and 6 months of continuous darkness."

## FIGURE 4: Lunar Phases Diagram

**Location in chapter:** Section 4 (after "Move it back around the circle...")

**Purpose:** Show the geometry of the Sun-Earth-Moon system and explain the phases.

**Elements:**
- Central circle representing Earth
- Large circle around Earth representing the Moon's orbit, divided into 8 or 9 positions
- Sun represented at left or right, with rays showing direction of sunlight
- At each position, a small circle representing the Moon, half-shaded to show illumination
- Positions labeled: New (0°), Waxing Crescent (45°), First Quarter (90°), Waxing Gibbous (135°), Full (180°), Waning Gibbous (225°), Last Quarter (270°), Waning Crescent (315°)
- Observer on Earth looking outward; diagram should clarify that observer faces the Moon in each phase (i.e., imagine yourself on Earth, facing the Moon)
- Key: the lit hemisphere of the Moon always faces the Sun; the fraction visible from Earth depends on the angle

**Caption:** "The Moon orbits Earth while always remaining half-illuminated by the Sun. The phase visible from Earth depends on the Sun-Earth-Moon angle. When the Moon is between Earth and Sun (0°), its lit side faces away—we see darkness (new moon). When it is opposite the Sun (180°), its lit side faces us—we see the full moon. At 90° angles, half the lit side is visible (quarters)."

## FIGURE 5: Sidereal Month vs. Synodic Month

**Location in chapter:** Section 4 (after "Over a month, those 50-minute delays...")

**Purpose:** Explain why the lunar phase cycle (29.5 days) is longer than the orbital period (27.3 days).

**Elements:**
- Timeline showing two sidereal months (27.3 days each) = 54.6 days
- During the first sidereal month, Moon completes one orbit relative to stars; Earth moves ~27 degrees around Sun
- During the second sidereal month, Moon completes another orbit relative to stars; Earth moves another ~27 degrees
- Mark the new moon positions: one at the beginning, one after 29.53 days (the synodic month), one at 54.6 days (two sidereal months = one synodic month plus a bit)
- Show that the Moon must "lap" the Sun-Earth line, requiring extra orbital distance
- Numerical values highlighted: 27.3 days (sidereal), 29.53 days (synodic), 2.21 days difference

**Caption:** "During a sidereal month (27.3 days), the Moon orbits Earth once relative to the stars. But Earth has moved along its orbit during this time, so the Sun-Earth-Moon angle has changed. The Moon must travel an additional 2.21 days worth of its orbit to return to the same phase with respect to the Sun. This is why the lunar month (phase cycle) is 29.53 days, not 27.3 days."

## FIGURE 6: Julian vs. Gregorian Calendar Drift

**Location in chapter:** Section 5 (after "By 1582, the drift had become visible...")

**Purpose:** Show the cumulative error in the Julian calendar and how the Gregorian reform corrects it.

**Elements:**
- Horizontal axis: years (0 to 3300)
- Vertical axis: error in days (-1 to 5)
- Line for Julian calendar: straight line with slope of 11 minutes/year, crossing zero at year 0, reaching ~1 day error at ~128 years, ~8 days at ~1000 years
- Mark the point at 1582 showing the error had accumulated to ~10 days by the time of Pope Gregory's reform
- Line for Gregorian calendar: much flatter slope, with small oscillations due to the 400-year leap-year cycle
- Mark key events: October 4/15, 1582 (the 10 days dropped); September 2/14, 1752 (England and American colonies adopt); note that Russia held out until 1917 (dropping 13 days)

**Caption:** "The Julian calendar's error of ~11 minutes per year accumulated to about 10 days by 1582. Pope Gregory XIII's reform skipped 3 leap years per 400 years, reducing the error to ~1 day per 3,300 years. Different countries adopted the Gregorian calendar at different times, requiring the adjustment of various numbers of days."

## FIGURE 7: Eclipses—Orbital Geometry

**Location in chapter:** Section 7 (after "The three bodies—Earth, Moon, Sun—rarely line up...")

**Purpose:** Explain why eclipses are rare: the Moon's orbit is tilted 5° relative to the ecliptic.

**Elements:**
- Top-down view of the solar system (looking down from above Earth's north pole)
- Sun at center
- Earth's orbit (ecliptic) shown as a circle or ellipse (for this figure, can be a circle)
- Moon's orbit around Earth shown as a small circle around Earth
- Moon's orbit tilted 5° relative to ecliptic
- Two intersection points marked as "nodes" (where Moon's orbit crosses ecliptic plane)
- Show the Moon at several positions: one at a node during new moon (solar eclipse possible), one at a node during full moon (lunar eclipse possible), and several positions away from nodes (no eclipse possible even if at new or full moon)
- Mark the eclipse season: brief window when Moon is near a node

**Caption:** "The Moon's orbit is tilted about 5° relative to Earth's orbital plane (the ecliptic). Eclipses occur only when the Moon is near the intersection points (nodes) of its orbit with the ecliptic plane. This tilt is why eclipses are rare—only a few weeks out of the year (eclipse season) can an eclipse occur."

## FIGURE 8: Solar and Lunar Eclipses—Shadow Cones

**Location in chapter:** Section 7 (after "Only observers within that narrow path...")

**Purpose:** Illustrate the geometry of solar and lunar eclipses and why lunar eclipses are visible from a larger area.

**Elements:**

### For Solar Eclipse:
- Sun on left
- Moon in the middle
- Earth on the right
- Moon's shadow (umbra, a narrow cone) reaching Earth
- Path of totality on Earth's surface marked as a narrow band (perhaps 160 km wide)
- Penumbra shown as a larger, lighter shadow region where eclipse is partial
- Label: "Only observers in the narrow umbra see totality"

### For Lunar Eclipse:
- Sun on left
- Earth in the middle
- Moon on right
- Earth's shadow (umbra and penumbra) extending to the Moon
- Moon passing through Earth's shadow cone
- Observation point on Earth's night side can see the entire Moon in darkness
- Label: "Earth's larger shadow allows the lunar eclipse to be visible from half of Earth"

**Caption:** "During a solar eclipse, the Moon's small shadow creates a narrow path of totality (perhaps 160 km wide). During a lunar eclipse, the Moon passes through Earth's larger shadow, visible from anywhere on the night side of Earth. This is why lunar eclipses are more frequently observed from any given location."

## FIGURE 9: The 400-Year Gregorian Cycle

**Location in chapter:** Section 5 (if space permits; may be optional)

**Purpose:** Show which century years are leap years in the Gregorian calendar.

**Elements:**
- Table or grid showing years 1600, 1700, 1800, 1900, 2000, 2100, 2200, 2300, 2400
- Leap year status marked for each: 1600 (leap), 1700 (not leap), 1800 (not leap), 1900 (not leap), 2000 (leap), 2100 (not leap), etc.
- Rule stated: "Century years are leap years only if divisible by 400"
- Count: in 400 years, there are 97 leap years (not 100), giving an average year length of 365.2425 days

**Caption:** "The Gregorian calendar skips 3 leap years every 400 years. Century years (1700, 1800, 1900, 2100) are not leap years unless they are divisible by 400 (like 2000). This gives an average year of 365.2425 days—accurate to about 1 day in 3,300 years."

## Notes on Presentation

- All diagrams should use color to distinguish the Sun, Earth, Moon, and orbital planes.
- For the seasonal diagrams (Figures 1 and 3), use consistent color coding: perhaps blue for winter, orange for summer, with transitions shown.
- The lunar phase diagram (Figure 4) is crucial for student understanding and should be large and clear. Many textbooks show it poorly (with the Moon and Sun overlapping, which is geometrically inaccurate). Be sure the scale makes clear that the Moon is far from Earth and the Sun.
- The tilt angle in Figure 1 should be exaggerated slightly for visibility, but labeled with the accurate 23.5° value.
- All LaTeX equations referenced in the chapter (for sun altitude, day length, insolation intensity) can be included as separate text inserts or in figure captions where appropriate.
