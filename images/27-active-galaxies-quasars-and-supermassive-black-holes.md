# Figures and Images: Active Galaxies, Quasars, Supermassive Black Holes

## Required figures for Attenborough × Feynman chapter

### 1. Maarten Schmidt and the spectrum of 3C 273
**Caption**: Schmidt's spectrum showing the Balmer lines of hydrogen shifted to the red end. The lines are marked at both their rest wavelengths and their observed (redshifted) positions in 3C 273. A scale showing the corresponding recession velocity (45,000 km/s) makes the discovery concrete.

**Purpose**: Anchors the historical discovery moment. Readers see what Schmidt saw. The visual pattern of the recognized lines is more memorable than description.

**Source mapping**: Corresponds to source file 02-m59962.md, the section "Redshifts: The Key to Quasars." Original source text shows this was an actual spectrum from Caltech observations.

---

### 2. Size comparison: Quasar energy source vs. solar system scales
**Caption**: Three concentric circles showing scale: (innermost) the quasar's energy-producing region (few light-days to few light-months, depending on variability timescale); (middle) the orbit of Neptune (5 light-hours); (outermost) the Oort cloud (50,000 light-years). Labels emphasize that the quasar source is smaller than planetary orbits, yet produces more energy than a trillion Suns.

**Purpose**: Visceral demonstration of the brightness problem. Text alone doesn't convey the scale paradox. Visual juxtaposition makes it memorable.

**Source mapping**: Elaborates on source 02-m59962.md section "The Size of the Energy Source," which states quasar variations are sometimes "in some cases, days" and the region "is not much larger than our solar system."

---

### 3. Accretion disk geometry with jets perpendicular to the disk
**Caption**: Side view of a black hole surrounded by a rotating accretion disk (shown as a thin disk with material spiraling inward). Two jets erupt perpendicular to the disk, one above and one below, traveling at relativistic speeds. Magnetic field lines shown threading through the disk, guiding the jets into collimated beams.

**Purpose**: Shows the geometry visually. The key insight—that jets escape perpendicular to the plane—is abstract without visualization. Magnetic field lines make the collimation mechanism concrete.

**Source mapping**: Addresses the jets section of source 03-m59965.md ("Radio Jets") and the chapter discussion of disk geometry.

---

### 4. M-sigma relation scatter plot
**Caption**: Horizontal axis: bulge velocity dispersion (km/s, logarithmic scale, 50-300 km/s). Vertical axis: central black hole mass (solar masses, logarithmic scale, 10^6 to 10^10). Data points from nearby galaxies (M87, NGC 4261, Andromeda, Milky Way, others) plotted with error bars. A best-fit line shows the correlation M_bh ≈ 0.001 M_bulge (or black hole is ~1/200 of bulge mass).

**Purpose**: Makes the correlation visual and quantitative. Readers can see both the trend and the scatter, recognizing that the relationship is real but not perfectly deterministic.

**Source mapping**: Source 04-m59966.md, "Codependence of Black Holes and Galaxies" section, provides the conceptual framework. The actual data come from Kormendy & Ho (2013) and similar observational surveys.

---

### 5. Evolution of quasar number density vs. cosmic time
**Caption**: Horizontal axis: cosmic time (in billions of years, from Big Bang 13.8 Gyr ago to present). Vertical axis: number density of quasars (arbitrary units). A curve peaks around 2 billion years after the Big Bang, then declines steadily to the present. A secondary curve shows star formation rate (SFR) over the same timescale, peaking at similar epoch.

**Purpose**: Demonstrates the coupling of quasar activity and star formation over cosmic time. Both peak early and decline together. This isn't coincidence; it reveals shared fuel source (interstellar gas).

**Source mapping**: Source 04-m59966.md, "The Evolution of Quasars" and "Codependence of Black Holes and Galaxies" sections.

---

### 6. AGN unification diagram
**Caption**: Central black hole and accretion disk shown in three views: (A) Face-on (looking down at the disk): bright nucleus visible, jets visible, disk obscured. This is a type 1 Seyfert or quasar if jets are beamed at us. (B) Edge-on (looking along the disk plane): thick dust torus blocks the nucleus. We see scattered light, jets edge-on. This is a type 2 Seyfert. (C) Intermediate angle: mixture of direct and scattered light visible. Annotations show how the same object's appearance changes with viewing angle.

**Purpose**: Transforms the abstract concept of "unification" into concrete visual form. Shows that object identity doesn't change; only appearance changes. Makes the model immediately credible.

**Source mapping**: Implicit in source 03-m59965.md and explicit in the chapter's "The unification model" section.

---

### 7. Quasar host galaxy with Hubble Space Telescope
**Caption**: HST image showing a bright central quasar point source (core) and the faint galaxy surrounding it. The contrast is stark—the quasar outshines the galaxy by 10-100×. The galaxy's redshift and the quasar's redshift are labeled as identical, proving they're at the same distance.

**Purpose**: Historical evidence that quasars are embedded in galaxies. Readers see the actual observation that settled the "are quasars really at cosmological distance?" debate.

**Source mapping**: Source 02-m59962.md, "Quasars Obey the Hubble Law" section.

---

### 8. Light travel time and variability
**Caption**: A 10-light-year diameter cluster of stars shown as a sphere. A timeline below shows: T=0 (all stars brighten simultaneously), T=5 years (light from near stars reaches Earth), T=10 years (light from far stars reaches Earth). Graphs show what an observer on Earth sees: gradual brightening over 10 years, not instantaneous brightening, even though the physical event was instantaneous.

**Purpose**: Explains the conceptual foundation for deriving source size from variability timescale. This principle is crucial for the entire chapter's argument.

**Source mapping**: Source 02-m59962.md, "The Size of the Energy Source" section, includes the exact argument and figure reference OSC_Astro_27_01_Limitation.

---

### 9. M87 Black Hole shadow (Event Horizon Telescope)
**Caption**: The 2019 EHT image of M87's black hole shadow—a dark region surrounded by a glowing ring of hot infalling material. False color (reds/oranges for brightness) used to highlight the shadow against the surrounding emission. Caption notes this is the shadow cast by the event horizon, not the black hole itself (which is invisible).

**Purpose**: Modern observational triumph. Readers see what a billion-solar-mass black hole actually looks like. Validates the accretion model with contemporary data.

**Source mapping**: Source 03-m59965.md, "Observational Evidence for Black Holes" section, explicitly mentions EHT M87 observations in 2019.

---

### 10. Accretion disk pressure and perpendicular ejection
**Caption**: Side cutaway of an accretion disk showing material flowing inward spirally. Arrows indicate pressure directions: inward (gravity), outward (radiation pressure and thermal pressure), and perpendicular to the plane (escape route). Material escapes preferentially perpendicular because pressure is lowest in that direction. Magnetic field lines shown as guiding the escaped material into collimated jets.

**Purpose**: Explains the jet mechanism mechanically. Why perpendicular? Because it's the path of least resistance. Pressure/density diagram would be too technical; this shows the concept intuitively.

**Source mapping**: Chapter section "The jets: perpendicular outflow from compressed disks" and source 03-m59965.md "Radio Jets" section.

## Image sourcing strategy

**Primary sources** (use if available):
1. Maarten Schmidt's original 3C 273 spectra (Caltech archives or published reproductions)
2. NASA/Chandra X-ray Observatory images of active galaxies
3. HST observations of quasar host galaxies (publicly available in MAST archive)
4. EHT Collaboration M87 black hole image (public domain, widely distributed)
5. Kormendy & Ho (2013) M-sigma relation data and figures

**Secondary sources** (adapt/redraw if originals unavailable):
1. Schematic diagrams of accretion disks (from any modern astrophysics textbook, e.g., Kippenhahn et al., or draw from scratch based on simulation visualizations)
2. Evolution curves of quasar number density (from observational surveys; can reconstruct from published data tables)
3. Unification diagram (standard concept diagram, appears in many sources; create bespoke version for this chapter)
4. Light-travel-time illustration (basic geometry, create from scratch to match chapter pedagogy)
