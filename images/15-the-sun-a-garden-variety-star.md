## Figures embedded in chapter

### Figure 1: Cross-section of Sun's interior

**Location:** End of Section 3 (The Sun's Interior)

**Purpose:** Establish internal structure and temperature gradient. Show energy flow pathways.

**Key elements:**
- Central core (yellow, hottest, labeled 15 MK)
- Radiative zone (orange-red gradient, labeled with density indicator)
- Convection zone (red-orange, cellular convection pattern visible)
- Photosphere (thin bright yellow line, labeled ~5,800 K)
- Chromosphere (red layer, thin, labeled ~10,000 K)
- Corona (light halo, extending several solar radii outward, labeled 1–3 MK)
- Arrows showing photon direction (inward to outward, slow in radiative zone, faster in convection)
- Labels for "energy transport by radiation" and "energy transport by convection"

**Specifications:**
- Cross-section view (left-right half cut-away is common)
- Color-coded by temperature
- Accurate relative thickness (photosphere and chromosphere are thin compared to interior)
- Scale bar showing solar radius and Earth-size reference

**Source:** Derived from solar models (e.g., MESA, standard solar models). NOT from a specific figure in the source text, but a standard pedagogical diagram used in all astronomy textbooks. Check NASA SOHO, SDO, or Parker Solar Probe mission websites for accurate interior model diagrams.

**Caption:** 
"The Sun's interior structure. Energy generated in the core by nuclear fusion radiates outward through the dense radiative zone (slow transport over 100,000 years), then rises through the convection zone by churning plasma motion (fast transport). The visible photosphere marks where gas becomes transparent. Above it, the chromosphere and corona extend outward, with the surprising temperature inversion showing the corona hotter than the photosphere."

### Figure 2: Temperature profile of Sun's atmosphere

**Location:** End of Section 4 (The Sun's Atmosphere)

**Purpose:** Visualize the temperature inversion. Show the puzzle graphically.

**Key elements:**
- Horizontal axis: Height above photosphere (in thousands of kilometers, ranging from 0 to ~2,000 km)
- Vertical axis: Temperature (in thousands of Kelvin, from 0 to 3,000 K or more)
- Line 1 (red or orange, dashed or solid): Temperature as a function of height
  - Starts at ~5,800 K at the photosphere (height 0)
  - Drops to ~4,500 K at base of chromosphere
  - Rises through chromosphere to ~10,000 K
  - Rapid rise in transition region
  - Levels off at 1,000,000+ K in the corona
- Shaded regions marking chromosphere, transition region, and corona boundaries
- Labels: "Photosphere," "Chromosphere," "Transition Region," "Corona"

**Specifications:**
- Linear or semi-log scale depending on desire for clarity (log scale compresses high corona values)
- Smooth curve from data or models, not jagged observations
- Uncertainty bands could show measurement uncertainty in transition region and corona
- Arrows or annotations pointing to key transitions

**Source:** Standard solar temperature profile. Based on observations from SDO (Solar Dynamics Observatory), IRIS (Interface Region Imaging Spectrograph), and other UV/X-ray observatories. The shape is well-established.

**Caption:**
"Temperature as a function of height in the Sun's atmosphere. The photosphere is coolest (~5,800 K). The temperature rises through the chromosphere and transition region to reach 1–3 million Kelvin in the corona—a paradoxical inversion still not fully explained. The transition region's extreme temperature gradient across a few tens of kilometers remains one of the major puzzles in solar physics."

### Figure 3: Sunspot pair with magnetic field topology

**Location:** End of Section 5 (The Sun's Magnetic Field and Sunspot Cycle)

**Purpose:** Show that sunspots come in pairs and are connected by magnetic structure. Illustrate field polarity.

**Key elements:**
- Photosphere surface view (top-down, showing granulation if desired)
- Two sunspots (dark areas) with labeled umbra (darker center) and penumbra (lighter border)
- Magnetic field lines (curves) connecting the two spots, emerging from one and entering the other
- Field direction arrows showing the loop geometry
- Polarity labels: "N" pole at one spot, "S" pole at the other
- Zeeman effect spectra (small insets) showing line splitting above each spot, indicating field strength

**Specifications:**
- Realistic sunspot sizes (larger is better, showing Earth for scale reference if desired)
- Field lines should follow 3D structure (they curve upward above the photosphere)
- Color code: One polarity could be red/orange, the other blue/violet, for clarity
- The loop structure should be clearly visible

**Source:** SDO (Solar Dynamics Observatory) magnetogram data. Many magnetograms available from NASA showing the Sun's surface magnetic field with sunspot overlays. Alternatively, derived from simulation of emerging flux tubes in the photosphere.

**Caption:**
"A sunspot pair as seen in visible light (top) and the associated magnetic field (bottom). The two sunspots represent the north and south poles of a single magnetic loop emerging from below the photosphere. The umbra (dark center) shows the strongest field; the penumbra (lighter border) shows weaker, more inclined field. The Zeeman splitting in the spectra (insets) indicates field strengths of thousands of Gauss."

### Figure 4: Solar active region at multiple heights

**Location:** End of Section 6 (The Active Sun)

**Purpose:** Show that sunspots, plages, prominences, and flares are vertically stacked manifestations of the same underlying magnetic structure.

**Key elements:**
- Multiple layers stacked vertically or in columns
- Layer 1 (bottom): Photosphere with sunspots
- Layer 2: Chromosphere with plages (bright regions above sunspots)
- Layer 3: Chromosphere/corona interface with prominences (loops of plasma)
- Layer 4 (top): Corona with flare (bright region, X-ray emission)
- Vertical lines or field lines connecting corresponding features across all layers
- Shared latitude and longitude coordinate overlay (showing all features in same vertical column)
- Different intensity or color scales appropriate to each layer (visible for photosphere, Hα for chromosphere, X-ray for corona)

**Specifications:**
- Three or four side-by-side or stacked panels, one for each atmospheric layer
- Same coordinate system throughout (latitude/longitude grid overlay)
- Realistic relative brightness levels for each temperature regime
- Labels identifying each structure type

**Source:** Composite of observations from multiple instruments. The concept is standard in solar astronomy: SDO (visible and extreme UV), IRIS (chromospheric detail), Hinode (chromosphere and corona), Parker Solar Probe (in-situ corona). A schematic version is often clearer for a textbook than raw observational data.

**Caption:**
"A solar active region observed at different heights in the Sun's atmosphere. The sunspot (visible, dark) is at the photosphere. Above it, the bright plage marks a chromospheric hotspot. Higher still, a prominence—a loop of dense plasma—bridges the transition region. At the top, coronal heating concentrates near the active region, and flares release energy explosively. All these phenomena are manifestations of the concentrated, twisted magnetic field at this location."

### Figure 5: Magnetogram and solar cycle (implicit in section 5, could be explicit)

**Location:** End of Section 5 (optional visual aid for magnetic cycle discussion)

**Purpose:** Show the Sun's surface magnetic field directly and how it evolves during the solar cycle.

**Key elements:**
- Map or image of the Sun's surface showing magnetic field direction/polarity
- Positive polarity (outward) shown in one color (e.g., red or orange)
- Negative polarity (inward) shown in another color (e.g., blue or violet)
- Sunspots marked as dark regions
- Multiple magnetograms arranged side-by-side, showing different times in the solar cycle:
  - Solar minimum (dipolar, simple field pattern)
  - Rising phase (more spots appearing, field becoming complex)
  - Solar maximum (field highly complex, many spots and active regions)
  - Declining phase (field simplifying again)
  - Next minimum (dipolar, but with opposite polarity compared to 22 years earlier)

**Specifications:**
- Full-disk Sun views
- High contrast between positive and negative polarity
- Clear labels marking the cycle phase
- Arrows or annotations showing how field complexity evolves

**Source:** NASA SOHO and SDO magnetograph data. Magnetograms are routinely produced and available from NASA's Stanford Solar Center, NOAA, and other sources.

**Caption:**
"The Sun's surface magnetic field at four stages of the solar cycle. The field is shown in white (outward polarity) and black (inward polarity). At solar minimum, the field is relatively simple and dipolar—similar to Earth's field. As the cycle progresses, sunspots and active regions multiply, and the field becomes increasingly twisted and complex. At solar maximum, numerous active regions and spot groups crowd the surface. As the cycle declines, the field simplifies again, and a new dipole field emerges—but with reversed polarity, completing the 22-year magnetic cycle."

### Figure 6: Aurora visibility map (September 1–2, 1859)

**Location:** End of Section 7 (Space Weather — Carrington Event)

**Purpose:** Illustrate the geographic reach of the Carrington Event. Make the event visually concrete.

**Key elements:**
- World map showing Earth's continents and oceans
- North and South Poles marked clearly
- Typical aurora visibility zones (within about 10–20° of the magnetic poles) marked in light gray or dashed lines
- Carrington Event aurora visibility zone marked in red/orange, extending from poles to Hawaii, the Caribbean, low northern latitudes, and southern latitudes
- Labels for key locations: "London," "North America," "Hawaii," "Caribbean," "Aurora visible to latitude..."
- Legend indicating what the colors mean

**Specifications:**
- Mercator or polar projection (polar is more accurate for aurora)
- Emphasis on the abnormal extent of visibility
- Historical accounts or quotes indicating the surprise observers felt

**Source:** Historical accounts from September 1–2, 1859. Not a direct measurement, but documented observations from many locations. Sources include scientific journals of the time, newspapers, and historical summaries (e.g., Carrington's own papers, modern summaries of the event in Sky & Telescope or Astronomy magazines).

**Caption:**
"Geographic extent of aurora visibility during the Carrington Event of September 1–2, 1859. The aurora, normally confined to high latitudes (gray zone), was visible from as far south as Hawaii and the Caribbean (red zone). This unprecedented global auroral display was accompanied by major disruptions to telegraph systems worldwide, establishing the first clear link between solar activity and effects on Earth."

## Media recommendations for supplementation

- **Video: Solar Granulation** (Swedish Institute for Solar Physics) — 30-second time-lapse showing the boiling surface of the photosphere. Viscerally demonstrates convection.

- **Video: Solar Flare and CME** (SDO mission, NASA) — Shows a flare and associated CME in near-real-time, with dramatic brightening and ejection visible in UV/X-ray.

- **Interactive: Sunspot Number Over Time** — Allow students to explore the 300+ year record of sunspot numbers and verify the ~11-year cycle themselves.

- **Image: Parker Solar Probe Approach** — Recent image (2024+) from NASA's Parker Solar Probe showing its closest approach to the Sun. Emphasizes modern exploration.

- **Aurora Time-Lapse** — High-latitude auroras over several hours, showing the dynamic curtain behavior.
