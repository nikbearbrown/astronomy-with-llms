### Part Two: The Mapping Problem

**[FIGURE: Herschel's 1785 wheel model vs modern disk structure]**
- Caption: Herschel correctly identified the disk shape but incorrectly placed the Sun at the center. Modern observations show the Sun is located roughly 26,000 light-years from the galactic center, in the suburbs.
- Placement: After Herschel's star-counting method explanation
- Source: Historical diagram + modern schematic
- Function: Illustrates the right-shape, wrong-position conclusion and motivates the need for better methods

**[FIGURE: Dust obscuration in visible vs radio/infrared]**
- Caption: Interstellar dust blocks visible light by a factor of a trillion but is transparent to radio waves and infrared radiation. Radio observations of the 21-centimeter hydrogen line revealed the Galaxy's true structure.
- Placement: After dust physics explanation
- Source: Schematic showing light paths through dust particles
- Function: Explains the technical shift that allowed galactic mapping from within the disk

**[FIGURE: Globular clusters distribution in three dimensions]**
- Caption: Shapley's mapping of 93 globular clusters showed they were arranged in a sphere centered not on the Sun but on a point in Sagittarius, about 26,000 light-years away. This was the Galaxy's center.
- Placement: After Shapley discovery section
- Source: 3D plot or artistic rendering of cluster positions
- Function: Shows the geometric insight that displaced the Sun from the galactic hub

### Part Three: The Architecture

**[FIGURE: Galaxy face-on and edge-on schematic views]**
- Caption: The Milky Way Galaxy viewed face-on reveals the thin disk (100,000 light-years diameter, 2,000 light-years thick) with a central bar and spiral arms. Edge-on, the bulge and dark matter halo become visible.
- Placement: Opening of Part Three, before component description
- Source: Astronomy textbook schematics or NASA artwork
- Function: Provides spatial reference frame for all subsequent component descriptions

**[FIGURE: Galaxy components labeled: thin disk, thick disk, bulge, halo, dark matter halo]**
- Caption: Major structural components of the Milky Way, showing concentric layers from the dense nuclear bulge at center, through the thin and thick disks, to the stellar halo and the invisible dark matter halo extending to at least 200,000 light-years.
- Placement: Part Three, after architecture section
- Source: Detailed cross-section diagram
- Function: Consolidates all structural components into single visual reference

**[FIGURE: Barred spiral structure with central bar and spiral arms]**
- Caption: The Galaxy contains a central bar (approximately 20,000 light-years long) from which two main spiral arms emerge, along with fainter arms and spurs. The Sun is located in the Orion Spur, about 26,000 light-years from the center.
- Placement: After barred spiral description
- Source: Modern infrared observations or composite artwork
- Function: Shows spiral organization and Sun's position within arm structure

**[FIGURE: Orion Spur region, local context]**
- Caption: The Orion Spur (Local Arm) is a short spiral arm containing the Orion Nebula (site of ongoing star formation), the Cygnus Rift (dark nebula), and the Sun's neighborhood. It is roughly 10,000 light-years long.
- Placement: After Orion Spur discussion
- Source: Infrared survey data (2MASS, Spitzer) or artwork
- Function: Provides immediate neighborhood context for solar system

**[FIGURE: Spiral arm as density wave, not winding rope]**
- Caption: Spiral arms are density waves — traffic jams where gas compresses and star formation is triggered. As individual stars orbit and leave the arm, new stars form to maintain the pattern. The arm persists through continuous replacement of its constituent stars.
- Placement: Part Three, after spiral arm mechanism section
- Source: Diagram showing material flow through density wave
- Function: Illustrates the counter-intuitive mechanism: persistence without permanence

### Part Four: The Rotation Curve

**[FIGURE: Galactic rotation curve — observed vs Kepler prediction]**
- Caption: The observed rotation curve (velocity vs distance from galactic center) remains roughly constant at ~220 km/s out to great distances. Kepler's laws predict it should decrease as 1/√r. The difference implies an invisible dark matter halo.
- Placement: Opening of Part Four, before explanation
- Source: Vera Rubin's classic rotation curve data or modern equivalents
- Function: Shows the observational puzzle that dark matter was inferred to solve

**[FIGURE: Mass distribution implied by flat rotation curve]**
- Caption: To maintain constant orbital velocity at large distances, the Galaxy's mass must increase linearly with radius — implying that most of the Galaxy's mass resides in a spherical halo beyond the visible disk boundary.
- Placement: After rotation curve explanation
- Source: Diagram showing mass profile
- Function: Connects kinematic observation to mass distribution

### Part Five: The Galactic Center

**[FIGURE: Galactic center region, radio wavelengths]**
- Caption: Radio image of the region ~1,500 light-years across, centered on Sagittarius A. Much of the emission comes from hot gas, supernova remnants (visible as hollow circles), and magnetic structures (bright threads). The black hole Sgr A* is at the very center.
- Placement: Opening of Part Five, before center description
- Source: VLA or other radio observatory data
- Function: Provides first view of galactic center at accessible wavelengths

**[FIGURE: Galactic center, X-ray view]**
- Caption: X-ray image of a 400×900 light-year region centered on Sgr A*. Hundreds of hot white dwarfs, neutron stars, and stellar black holes with accretion disks appear as bright points. The diffuse haze is 10-million-Kelvin gas.
- Placement: Part Five, after radio view
- Source: Chandra X-ray Observatory data
- Function: Shows high-energy processes and stellar density at galactic center

**[FIGURE: Sagittarius A* and surrounding gas streamers]**
- Caption: Radio map showing ionized gas streamers orbiting the supermassive black hole Sgr A*. The structure indicates rapid motion of material around the black hole and occasional flaring as objects are consumed.
- Placement: Part Five, after Sgr A* introduction
- Source: Radio observations (ALMA, VLA, etc.)
- Function: Shows black hole's dynamic environment

**[FIGURE: Near-infrared view of Keck telescopes showing stellar orbits]**
- Caption: High-resolution infrared image from the Keck Observatory showing stars orbiting the galactic center. Individual stellar orbits have been tracked for over 20 years, with one star completing a full 15.6-year orbit that brings it within 124 AU of the black hole.
- Placement: Part Five, after Keck methodology section
- Source: Keck Observatory near-infrared images (public archive)
- Function: Shows the actual observational data underlying the black hole mass determination

**[FIGURE: Stellar orbital diagram with complete 15.6-year orbit marked]**
- Caption: The orbit of one tracked star around Sgr A*, showing the closest approach (124 AU, ~17 light-hours from the black hole) and the perihelion distance. Kepler's third law applied to this orbit yields a central mass of 4.6 million Suns.
- Placement: Part Five, after orbital period discussion
- Source: Diagram based on published orbital elements
- Function: Makes the Kepler's law calculation concrete and visual

### Part Six: Stellar Populations

**[FIGURE: Andromeda Galaxy showing Population I and II]**
- Caption: The Andromeda Galaxy displays two distinct stellar populations: old, reddish Population II stars in the nuclear bulge and halo; young, blue Population I stars concentrated in the spiral arms. This pattern is common in large spiral galaxies.
- Placement: Part Six, opening of populations section
- Source: Hubble Space Telescope image or artistic rendition
- Function: Establishes visual distinction that catalyzed population concept

**[FIGURE: Chemical enrichment through stellar generations]**
- Caption: First-generation stars form from primordial hydrogen and helium. Massive stars create heavy elements, explode as supernovae, and scatter elements back into the interstellar medium. Subsequent generations are born from progressively enriched gas, accumulating metals over billions of years.
- Placement: Part Six, after enrichment mechanism explanation
- Source: Diagram showing material cycling through generations
- Function: Illustrates the mechanism connecting age to chemical composition

**[FIGURE: Stellar spectra showing absorption lines for heavy elements]**
- Caption: Spectral absorption lines reveal the chemical composition of stars. Iron (4404 Å), calcium (3933 Å), and other elements leave dark lines at characteristic wavelengths. The depth of these lines indicates abundance. Population II stars show very shallow lines for heavy elements; Population I stars show deeper lines.
- Placement: Part Six, after spectra discussion
- Source: Example stellar spectra from spectroscopy archive
- Function: Demonstrates how we read stellar chemistry from light

### Part Seven: Galactic Formation and Evolution

**[FIGURE: Monolithic collapse model]**
- Caption: Early model of galaxy formation: a spherical protogalactic cloud collapses under gravity, flattening into a rotating disk as angular momentum is conserved. Stars form first in the halo before collapse; disk forms afterward.
- Placement: Part Seven, opening of formation section
- Source: Diagram showing collapse sequence
- Function: Introduces the simple model that subsequent evidence complicated

**[FIGURE: Sagittarius dwarf galaxy being disrupted by tidal forces]**
- Caption: The Sagittarius dwarf galaxy, currently only 50,000 light-years from the galactic center, is being torn apart by the Milky Way's gravity. Tidal forces create long streams of stars spiraling through the halo toward eventual merger.
- Placement: Part Seven, after Sagittarius discovery
- Source: Diagram or artistic rendition showing galactic tides
- Function: Shows ongoing galactic merger and its visual signature

**[FIGURE: Tidal streams in the galactic halo]**
- Caption: Stars from disrupted dwarf galaxies form long streams that orbit through the halo of the Milky Way. At least a dozen such streams have been identified, evidence of the Galaxy's violent past and continued growth through merger and accretion.
- Placement: Part Seven, after tidal stream discussion
- Source: Halo structure diagram with streams marked
- Function: Consolidates multiple merger evidence into single view

**[FIGURE: Predicted Andromeda-Milky Way merger sequence]**
- Caption: In 3–4 billion years, Andromeda and the Milky Way will collide. Computer simulations show the galaxies will undergo a complex multi-billion-year interaction, eventually merging into a single large elliptical galaxy. The Sun's orbit will be perturbed but the Earth will survive.
- Placement: Part Seven, after Andromeda collision discussion
- Source: N-body simulation results or artists' rendition
- Function: Conveys the scale of future galactic change and its inevitability
