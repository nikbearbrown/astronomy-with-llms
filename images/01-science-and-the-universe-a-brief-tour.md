## Figure Requests

The chapter text includes five implied figures that would strengthen visual understanding:

### 1. Ptolemaic vs. Heliocentric Models (Concept 1 mechanism)

**Where in text:** After the Ptolemy epicycle explanation, before Copernicus.

**What it shows:** Side-by-side comparison of two models:
- Left: Ptolemy's Earth-centered system with epicycles (small circles riding on larger circles)
- Right: Copernicus's Sun-centered system with simple circular orbits

**Purpose:** Visual proof that the second model is indeed simpler. Students often read "fewer epicycles" without really grasping the reduction in complexity. A diagram makes it immediate.

**Suggested source:** OpenStax has good public-domain diagrams of both systems. If using those, attribute "OpenStax Astronomy, Figure 2.X."

### 2. Binary Star System Orbital Mechanics (Concept 2 worked example)

**Where in text:** In the binary star worked example section.

**What it shows:** 
- Two stars orbiting a common center of mass
- Arrows indicating their velocities at different points in their orbit
- Light waves shifted blue (approaching) and red (receding) at opposite orbital positions

**Purpose:** Concretizes the Doppler shift mechanism and shows how orbital geometry encodes mass information.

**Suggested source:** Astronomy textbooks (like OpenStax) have standard diagrams of this. Commission if no public-domain version exists.

### 3. Powers-of-Ten Scale Ladder (Concept 3 mechanism)

**Where in text:** After the cold open but before the scientific notation explanation.

**What it shows:** A vertical ladder or zooming sequence showing scale jumps:
- Room: 10 meters
- City block: 100 meters
- Town: 1 km
- Region: 100 km
- Continent: 1000 km
- Earth: 10,000 km
- Earth-Sun: 1.5 × 10^8 km
- Solar System: 10^12 km
- Light-year: 10^13 km
- Galaxy: 10^21 km
- Observable universe: 10^27 km

**Purpose:** Students see that scale changes are not arbitrary — they follow a pattern. Powers of ten are not a notation trick; they reflect how the universe is organized.

**Suggested approach:** Could be a logarithmic scale illustration (each step is 10× larger).

### 4. Light Travel Time Illustration (Concept 2, "trade-off" section)

**Where in text:** After the statement "the farther out in space we look, the longer the light has taken to get here."

**What it shows:**
- A star or galaxy at distance D
- Light path from source to Earth
- Time labels indicating "light left this source X years ago"
- A note: "The light we see tonight left this object X years ago"

**Purpose:** Drives home the non-intuitive fact that observing distant objects is observing the past.

**Suggested source:** NASA or Hubble Space Telescope images paired with text overlay.

### 5. Cosmic Calendar Timeline (Chapter Summary / Connections Forward)

**Where in text:** Could be placed after the "Cosmic Calendar" challenge exercise, or in the next chapter's opening.

**What it shows:** A year-long timeline compressed to show:
- January 1, 12:00 AM: Big Bang
- September 10: Solar system forms
- December 31, evening: Humans appear
- December 31, 11:59 PM: Modern astronomy begins

**Purpose:** Gives students a visceral sense of humanity's role in cosmic time.

**Note:** Carl Sagan's *The Dragons of Eden* originally used this. OpenStax does not include the figure, but it is worth adding for Attenborough × Feynman voice (scale as moral weight).

## Sourcing Strategy

1. **Public-domain first:** Check OpenStax *Astronomy* and NASA educational resources for figures that can be reused under CC-BY license.

2. **Attribution:** If reusing, include "Based on [source]" or "Adapted from [source]."

3. **Original commission:** If public-domain versions do not exist or are poor quality, commission simple diagrams. Focus on clarity over decoration.

4. **Alt text:** Every figure should have descriptive alt text for accessibility.
