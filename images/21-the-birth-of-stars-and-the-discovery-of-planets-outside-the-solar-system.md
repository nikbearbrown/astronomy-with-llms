# Images and Figures: Star Birth and Exoplanet Discovery

Reference document for figures to be sourced or created for Chapter 21.

## Figure 1: Orion Nebula, visible and infrared

**Purpose:** Cold open, establishing the active site of star formation.
**Description:** Visible-light image of Orion Nebula showing Trapezium stars. Separate infrared image showing dense dust clumps and young stars hidden in visible light.
**Caption:** "Orion in Visible and Infrared: The Trapezium stars are visible in optical light, but infrared reveals over 2,000 young stars obscured by dust, many still in the proto stellar stage."
**Source:** Hubble Space Telescope (visible), ALMA or Spitzer (infrared). Public domain NASA images.

## Figure 2: Molecular cloud core collapse sequence

**Purpose:** Visualize the progression from cloud → dense core → protostar.
**Description:** Three panels showing decreasing size and increasing density. Temperature and density values labeled. Illustration of pressure (arrows outward) and gravity (arrows inward) with gravity dominating in the denser panels.
**Caption:** "Gravitational Collapse in a Molecular Cloud Core: As density increases, the Jeans mass decreases (gray curve), bringing more mass above the instability threshold. Gravity (red arrows) overwhelms pressure (blue arrows), and collapse accelerates."
**Source:** Illustration based on standard Jeans instability diagrams.

## Figure 3: Protostar with rotating disk and jets

**Purpose:** Show the anatomy of a newborn star system.
**Description:** Schematic cross-section showing protostar (central dot), rotating disk (flat structure around equator), and bipolar jets (arrows pointing north and south along rotation axis). Envelope of infalling material (stippled region). Angular momentum vectors shown.
**Caption:** "Protostar Anatomy: The young star's rotation accelerates inward material from collapsing clouds, creating a spinning disk. Jets ejected along the rotation axis carry away angular momentum, allowing accretion to continue without runaway spin."
**Source:** Illustration. Schematic based on standard formation models.

## Figure 4: Herbig-Haro objects

**Purpose:** Show evidence of jets and their visibility.
**Description:** Two-panel figure. Left: infrared image of a hidden protostar with Herbig-Haro objects visible as glowing knots along a linear path. Right: illustration showing how jets collide with ambient gas, heating it to emission.
**Caption:** "Herbig-Haro Objects: Jets from young protostars collide with nearby gas clouds, exciting atoms to emit light. These bright knots trace the jet's path and reveal the location of hidden, dust-shrouded stars."
**Source:** Images of HH objects from JWST or previous missions (public). Illustration schematic.

## Figure 5: HL Tau protoplanetary disk (ALMA)

**Purpose:** Show planets forming in real time (gaps and rings).
**Description:** ALMA high-resolution image of HL Tau's disk showing rings of dust and gaps. Gaps are darker (depleted of dust), rings are brighter. Label the rings and note their spacing (~1–2 AU).
**Caption:** "Planets Carving Gaps: ALMA observations of HL Tau reveal nested rings and gaps in a disk younger than 1 million years. Gaps are cleared by forming planets; the ring spacing indicates planet masses and orbital spacing."
**Source:** ALMA Science Verification data, public release.

## Figure 6: Dust grain growth and the meter-size barrier

**Purpose:** Illustrate the pebble-to-planetesimal transition.
**Description:** Graph with size on x-axis (logarithmic, from mm to km) and growth timescale on y-axis (log). Curve showing collision growth rate (fast initially, then slowing). Overlay showing meter-size barrier—region where drag forces cause rapid orbital decay. Show escape path (growth to 100 meters and beyond).
**Caption:** "The Meter-Size Barrier: Dust grains grow by collision, but at the centimeter-to-meter scale, drag forces from the disk gas cause objects to spiral into the star in ~50,000 years unless they grow rapidly to kilometer size (planetesimals)."
**Source:** Illustration. Data from dust evolution models (e.g., Birnstiel & Andrews).

## Figure 7: Radial velocity method

**Purpose:** Illustrate the Doppler shift detection technique.
**Description:** Three panels. Top: star and planet orbiting common center of mass. Middle: star's velocity component toward and away from Earth, shown as sinusoidal curve. Bottom: spectral line shifting in wavelength (Doppler shift) corresponding to velocity changes. Label the period and amplitude.
**Caption:** "Radial Velocity Method: As a star and planet orbit their common center of mass, the star's motion toward and away from Earth shifts its spectral lines. A planet's gravitational tug can be detected through velocity changes as small as 1 meter per second."
**Source:** Illustration. Example data from 51 Pegasi or similar system.

## Figure 8: Transit light curve

**Purpose:** Show how a transiting planet dims a star's light.
**Description:** Left: illustration of planet crossing in front of star at three stages (approaching, in transit, departing). Right: light curve showing brightness dip during transit. Label the transit depth and duration.
**Caption:** "Transit Method: When a planet passes in front of its star, the star's brightness dips. The dip depth depends on the planet's size relative to the star; the duration depends on the orbital speed. Multiple transits confirm the planet's period."
**Source:** Illustration with example light curve (e.g., Kepler data).

## Figure 9: Exoplanet discovery history

**Purpose:** Show the growth in exoplanet discoveries over time.
**Description:** Graph with time on x-axis (1995–2025) and cumulative number of exoplanet discoveries on y-axis (log scale). Curve shows exponential growth, with annotations for key milestones: first discovery (1995, 51 Peg), Kepler launch (2009), Kepler discoveries (thousands by 2013), TESS (2018).
**Caption:** "Exoplanet Discovery History: The number of known exoplanets has grown exponentially, driven by technological improvements in spectrographs and space telescopes. Over 5,800 exoplanets are now known."
**Source:** Data from NASA Exoplanet Archive.

## Figure 10: Exoplanet size distribution

**Purpose:** Show the diversity of planetary sizes and the absence of super-Earths in our solar system.
**Description:** Histogram showing the number of exoplanets (y-axis) versus planet radius (x-axis, in Earth radii). Clear peaks at 1–1.5 Earth radii (super-Earths) and 2.5–4 Earth radii (mini-Neptunes). Include comparison bars for Earth, Neptune, and Jupiter. Label the "gap" where our solar system has few planets.
**Caption:** "The Exoplanet Size Distribution: The most common exoplanets are super-Earths (1.4–2.8 Earth radii) and mini-Neptunes (2.8–4 Earth radii), categories absent from our solar system. This diversity reveals that planetary systems are far more varied than our own example."
**Source:** Kepler mission data, NASA Exoplanet Archive.

## Figure 11: Exoplanet orbital architecture diversity

**Purpose:** Compare our solar system to other known systems.
**Description:** Two rows. Top row: solar system planets shown on a semi-major axis scale (AU). Bottom rows: three example exoplanet systems—one with hot Jupiters, one with compact super-Earths, one with an eccentric giant. Use size and color to distinguish planet type.
**Caption:** "Planetary System Diversity: Our solar system (top) has a particular arrangement—small rocky planets close-in, giant planets farther out. Many exoplanet systems differ dramatically, hosting hot Jupiters, tightly packed super-Earths, or other configurations, suggesting planets migrate after formation."
**Source:** Illustration. Data from NASA Exoplanet Archive.

## Figure 12: Hot Jupiter migration pathways

**Purpose:** Illustrate the mechanisms by which planets can end up close to their stars.
**Description:** Three diagrams showing: (1) Type I migration—a planet gradually spiraling inward through the disk via tidal torques; (2) Type II migration—a large planet following the gap it creates as the disk evaporates; (3) Gravitational scattering—one planet ejected, another deflected into a close-in orbit.
**Caption:** "Hot Jupiter Formation: Planets likely form farther out and migrate inward through interactions with the disk (Type I and II) or gravitational scattering with other planets. A gas giant can reach the inner solar system in a million years or less."
**Source:** Illustration. Diagrams based on standard migration models.

## Figure 13: Grand Tack scenario for the solar system

**Purpose:** Propose that our own system may have undergone migration early on.
**Description:** Three panels showing solar system at three ages: (1) Jupiter and Saturn form at 3–5 AU; (2) Jupiter migrates inward to 1.5 AU, Saturn follows (Grand Tack); (3) Jupiter and Saturn scatter back outward, settling in their current configuration. Show the effect on inner planets (destabilization, ejection of some).
**Caption:** "Grand Tack: Evidence suggests Jupiter may have migrated inward early in solar system history, then been scattered outward by Saturn. This migration would have reshuffled the inner solar system and may explain the current arrangement of terrestrial planets and asteroid belt gaps."
**Source:** Illustration based on Walsh et al. (2011) Grand Tack model.

## Figure 14: Kepler mission field of view and results

**Purpose:** Show the scale and scope of the Kepler survey.
**Description:** Left: sky map showing Kepler's field of view (a patch near Cygnus). Right: distribution of Kepler's confirmed exoplanet detections by orbital period and planet radius, shown as a scatter plot with color coding for number of detections in each bin.
**Caption:** "Kepler Mission: The spacecraft observed 150,000 stars in a small patch near Cygnus (left). Its discoveries (right) revealed that planets with short orbital periods and moderate sizes (super-Earths and mini-Neptunes) are common."
**Source:** NASA Kepler mission archives.

## Figure specifications

All figures should include:
- Clear, legible labels with units
- Color-blind-friendly palettes (avoid red-green contrast alone)
- Captions of 1–2 sentences, no longer
- Sources cited (mission, instrument, or reference)
- High resolution suitable for print and screen
