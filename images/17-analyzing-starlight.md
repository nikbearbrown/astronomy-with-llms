### Figure 5: [FIGURE: Absorption Lines in Stars of Different Temperatures]
**Location**: Concept 3, spectral line explanation.

### Figure 9: [FIGURE: Spectral Line Widths]
**Location**: Concept 4, line widths and stellar size.
**Original caption**: "Spectral Lines."
**Use in text**: "A giant star has a large, extended photosphere... broad spectral lines. / A dwarf star is smaller, denser... broad spectral lines."
**Alt description**: Two spectra side-by-side: one from a giant star (narrow absorption lines), one from a dwarf star (broad absorption lines), illustrating the pressure-broadening effect.
**Source**: OSC Astronomy, Fig. 17.04, credited.

### Figure 10: [FIGURE: Doppler-Shifted Stars]
**Location**: Concept 4, Doppler shift and radial velocity.
**Original caption**: "Doppler-Shifted Stars."
**Use in text**: "A star moving toward us compresses the wavelengths it emits. The absorbed light shifts toward the blue. A star moving away stretches the wavelengths. The absorbed light shifts toward the red."
**Alt description**: Three spectra showing a reference star's absorption lines in the center, with a star moving toward us (lines shifted to shorter wavelengths / bluer) and a star moving away (lines shifted to longer wavelengths / redder).
**Source**: OSC Astronomy, Fig. 17.04, credited.

### Figure 11: [FIGURE: Large Proper Motion]
**Location**: Concept 4, proper motion (referenced but not detailed).
**Original caption**: "Large Proper Motion."
**Use in text**: "Unlike radial motion, which is along our line of sight (i.e., toward or away from Earth), this motion, called proper motion, is *transverse*: that is, across our line of sight."
**Alt description**: A celestial map showing a star's position at two times (e.g., 1900 and 2000), with an arrow indicating its apparent movement across the sky.
**Source**: OSC Astronomy, Fig. 17.04, credited.

### Figure 12: [FIGURE: Changes in the Big Dipper]
**Location**: Concept 4, proper motion example.
**Original caption**: "Changes in the Big Dipper."
**Use in text**: "For example, some 50,000 years from now, terrestrial observers will find the handle of the Big Dipper unmistakably more bent than it is now..."
**Alt description**: Four images of the Big Dipper's asterism at different epochs: ~50,000 years ago, today, and ~50,000 years in the future, showing the gradual bending of the handle due to proper motions of its component stars.
**Source**: OSC Astronomy, Fig. 17.04, credited.

### Figure 13: [FIGURE: Using Spectrum to Determine Stellar Rotation]
**Location**: Concept 4, line broadening and rotation.
**Original caption**: "Using a Spectrum to Determine Stellar Rotation."
**Use in text**: "A non-rotating star has a sharp absorption line. A rotating star produces a smeared, broadened line..."
**Alt description**: Two spectra side-by-side: one from a non-rotating star (sharp absorption lines), one from a rapidly rotating star (broad, smeared lines), showing the effect of Doppler broadening from rotation.
**Source**: OSC Astronomy, Fig. 17.04, credited.

### Figure 14: [FIGURE: Comparison of Rotating Stars]
**Location**: Concept 4, rotation rate effects on stellar shape.
**Original caption**: "Comparison of Rotating Stars."
**Use in text**: "Some stars, like Vega, rotate so fast (one rotation every 12.5 hours) that they are distorted — flattened at the equator like an oblate spheroid, 23% wider at the equator than at the poles."
**Alt description**: Comparative images of stellar shapes: the Sun (nearly spherical), Vega (noticeably flattened/oblate), and Altair (highly flattened, equatorial bulge prominent).
**Source**: OSC Astronomy, Fig. 17.04, credited.

### Figure 15: [FIGURE: Space Velocity and Proper Motion]
**Location**: Concept 4, space velocity diagram.
**Original caption**: "Space Velocity and Proper Motion."
**Use in text**: "To know the true space velocity of a star — that is, its total speed and the direction in which it is moving through space relative to the Sun — we must know its radial velocity, proper motion, and distance."
**Alt description**: A 3D diagram showing the components of a star's motion: radial velocity (along the line of sight), proper motion (across the sky), and the resultant space velocity vector.
**Source**: OSC Astronomy, Fig. 17.04, credited.
