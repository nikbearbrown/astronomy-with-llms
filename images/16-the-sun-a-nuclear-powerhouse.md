# Image References: Chapter 16 — The Sun Burning

## Figure 1: Coulomb Barrier and Quantum Tunneling
**Location in chapter:** Section III, after "Quantum Tunneling: The Escape Route"

**Description:**
Split-panel illustration showing two protons approaching each other. Left side shows classical physics prediction: a high barrier (Coulomb repulsion) that stops protons with insufficient energy, bouncing them apart. Right side shows quantum mechanics: the same barrier, but with a proton represented as a wave function (shown as a Gaussian envelope). The wave extends beyond the classical turning point, penetrating into the barrier region with small but nonzero amplitude. On the far side of the barrier, the region is labeled "Strong nuclear force acts here."

**Source notes:**
- This illustration should emphasize the contrast between classical and quantum behavior
- Key labels: "Coulomb barrier," "classical turning point," "quantum tunneling," "strong nuclear force region"
- Vertical axis: potential energy; horizontal axis: distance between proton centers
- Could use color (e.g., red for barrier potential, blue for wave function amplitude) to distinguish classical vs. quantum regions

**Related concept:** 
This figure directly supports the explanation that quantum tunneling makes fusion possible at temperatures where classical physics says it should not occur.

## Figure 2: The Three Steps of the Proton-Proton Chain
**Location in chapter:** Section IV, after "Three Steps to Helium"

**Description:**
Three-panel flow diagram showing the proton-proton chain reactions:

**Panel 1 (Step One):**
Two hydrogen-1 nuclei (protons) approaching each other with an arrow showing collision. Output: one deuterium nucleus (1 proton + 1 neutron), one positron (e+), one neutrino (ν). Include brief label: "Bottleneck step; limited by quantum tunneling probability."

**Panel 2 (Step Two):**
One deuterium nucleus being struck by another hydrogen-1 nucleus. Output: one helium-3 nucleus (2 protons + 1 neutron) and a gamma-ray photon (γ). Label: "Fast step; occurs ~6 seconds after step one on average."

**Panel 3 (Step Three):**
Two helium-3 nuclei fusing. Output: one helium-4 nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons) and two hydrogen-1 nuclei. Label: "Slow step; two He-3 nuclei merge once per ~million years on average."

**Bottom panel:**
Summary equation showing the net result: 
$$^1_1\text{H} + ^1_1\text{H} + ^1_1\text{H} + ^1_1\text{H} \longrightarrow {^4_2\text{He}} + 2e^+ + 2\nu + \gamma$$

**Source notes:**
- Use standard nuclear physics notation for atomic number (subscript) and mass number (superscript)
- Color-code nuclei consistently (hydrogen in one color, deuterium slightly different, helium-3 and helium-4 in contrasting color)
- Show scale of time (in parentheses) so reader understands the vastly different rates

## General Image Notes

**Consistency across astronomy textbook:**
- Use consistent color schemes for elements (hydrogen, helium, etc.) if other chapters in the book have established conventions
- Standard physics notation: mass number (superscript), atomic number (subscript)
- Temperature color convention (if used elsewhere): typically red = hot, blue = cool

**Avoid:**
- Overly cartoon-like representations of nuclei (use symbolic representation unless pedagogically necessary)
- Complicated 3D renderings for what are inherently 2D concepts (tunneling, layering, oscillations)
- Unlabeled or ambiguous arrows

**Consider:**
- Small "scale bar" graphics showing relative sizes where relevant (e.g., nucleus diameter vs. atom diameter for tunneling figure)
- Callout boxes with key equations or facts
- Simplified but accurate nuclear notation
