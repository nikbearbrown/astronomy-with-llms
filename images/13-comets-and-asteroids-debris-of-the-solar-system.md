The belt is mostly empty space.

**Location in chapter:** Concept 2, after the worked example on Rosetta.

**Type:** Annotated surface imagery with cross-section diagram.

**Content:**
- Large image: High-resolution photograph of Comet 67P from Rosetta (actual image data)
- Smaller inset: Cross-section diagram showing:
  - Irregular nucleus shape (double-lobed)
  - Internal structure (ice matrix + embedded dust and organic compounds)
  - Surface features labeled: pit, overhang, smooth region, rough terrain
  - Sublimation vents (shown as small arrows emanating from specific locations)
  - Scale bar indicating 5 km

**Labels:**
- Active vents
- Dust/organic compounds
- Ice core
- Porous zones
- Surface roughness features

**Caption (80–100 words):**
The nucleus of Comet 67P, imaged by Rosetta at unprecedented resolution, reveals an irregular, two-lobed structure roughly 5 kilometers across. The surface is rough and varied — pitted in some regions, smooth in others, with pronounced overhangs. Sublimation of water ice and other volatiles is not uniform; instead, it is concentrated in narrow jets from localized active vents. The interior is porous — roughly 50% empty space — making the comet fragile and unlikely to survive intact if perturbed into closer orbits.

**Source/Reference:**
European Space Agency, Rosetta mission archive. High-resolution images from August 2014 – September 2015.

## Figure 3: Comet Tail Geometry — The Always-Away Configuration

**Location in chapter:** Concept 2, after the mechanism section on tail formation.

**Type:** Orbital diagram with three snapshots.

**Content:**
Three panels showing comet position at different points in its orbit:
1. **Approaching the Sun** (still far)
   - Comet nucleus (small dot)
   - Faint tail pointing away from Sun
   - Orbital direction (arrow showing direction of motion)
   
2. **Near Perihelion** (closest approach)
   - Bright nucleus
   - Large tail perpendicular to the line from Sun to comet
   - Tail much longer, both dust and ion components visible
   - Sun rays (to show direction)

3. **Receding from the Sun**
   - Nucleus
   - Tail still pointing away from Sun
   - Tail now leads the comet's motion (opposite to orbital direction)

**Annotations:**
- Dust tail (curved line, shows slight curvature following orbit)
- Ion tail (straight line, exactly radial from Sun)
- Direction of motion (arrow on comet's path)
- Direction away from Sun (arrows on tails)

**Caption (60–80 words):**
Comet tails always point away from the Sun, pushed by radiation pressure and the solar wind. As the comet approaches the Sun, its tail grows and becomes more prominent. Near perihelion, the tail can span millions of kilometers. As the comet recedes, the tail still points outward — now it leads the comet's motion. The dust tail curves slightly due to orbital mechanics; the ion tail points directly away from the Sun in straight lines.

**Source/Reference:**
Standard orbital mechanics; imagery from Comet Halley (1986), Hale-Bopp (1997), and modern comets observed by SOHO.

## Figure 4: Impact Energy Across Historical and Modern Events

**Location in chapter:** Concept 3, worked example on historical impacts.

**Type:** Horizontal bar chart with logarithmic scale.

**Content:**
Bars representing energy release (megatons TNT equivalent) for:
1. **Hiroshima bomb** (~0.015 megatons) — baseline reference
2. **Chelyabinsk 2013** (~0.5 megatons) — small asteroid, 20 meters
3. **Tunguska 1908** (~10 megatons) — mid-sized asteroid, ~50 meters
4. **Largest nuclear weapon tested** (~50 megatons) — reference for technological scale
5. **Chicxulub 65 Ma** (~100+ megatons) — dinosaur extinction event, ~10 km asteroid

**Scale:** Logarithmic y-axis (10⁻² to 10³ megatons)

**Annotations:**
- Bar colors: Blue for natural events (impacts), Red for human-made explosions
- Numbers displayed above each bar
- Small asteroids shown with orange circles; larger ones shown to scale on secondary diagram
- Annotation: "Diameter scales as (energy)^(1/3)"

**Caption (60–80 words):**
Impact energy increases steeply with asteroid size. The Chelyabinsk impact of 2013, a relatively small asteroid just 20 meters across, released 500 kilotons of energy — enough to injure 1,500 people. The 1908 Tunguska event, from a 50-meter asteroid, released 10 megatons. The dinosaur-extinction impact near Chicxulub 65 million years ago, from a 10-kilometer asteroid, released energy millions of times larger. Energy grows with the cube of diameter; small increases in size produce huge increases in destructive power.

**Source/Reference:**
Brown et al. (2013) — Chelyabinsk analysis; Schultz et al. (2010) — Chicxulub impact evidence; standard impact crater energy calculations.

## Figure 5: Asteroid Spectral Classification

**Location in chapter:** Concept 1, worked example on asteroid types.

**Type:** Spectral reflectance curves + summary table.

**Content:**
- Large panel: Reflectance curves (0–1.0 relative reflectance) vs. wavelength (0.3–2.5 micrometers)
  - C-type: Low reflectance across spectrum (~0.04), gently curved, absorptions in near-infrared
  - S-type: Higher reflectance (~0.15), steeper slope toward infrared, absorbed bands at ~1 and 2 micrometers
  - M-type: Moderate reflectance (~0.15), flat spectrum, minimal features
- Small inset table:
  - Type | Abundance | Composition | Example
  - C-type | 75% | Water-bearing minerals, organics | Mathilde
  - S-type | 17% | Silicates, some metal | Eros
  - M-type | 8% | Iron-nickel | Psyche

**Annotations:**
- Color-code curves (C = gray, S = red, M = blue)
- Mark characteristic absorption features
- Label solar spectrum for reference

**Caption (60–80 words):**
Asteroid composition can be inferred from their spectral reflectance without physical sampling. C-type asteroids (carbonaceous) are dark and absorb most sunlight; they formed in cooler regions of the solar nebula and retain water-bearing minerals. S-type asteroids (silicaceous) are lighter and reflect more infrared light; they show signatures of thermal alteration. M-type asteroids (metallic) have flat spectra indicating exposed metal. The distribution of types varies with distance from the Sun, reflecting the temperature gradient in the early solar nebula.

**Source/Reference:**
Bus & Rivkin (2004) — SMASS taxonomy; spectral data from USGS spectral library.

## Figure 6: DART Impact and Momentum Transfer

**Location in chapter:** Concept 3, worked example on planetary defense.

**Type:** Sequence diagram + momentum calculation visualization.

**Content:**
- Top panel: Before/after comparison
  - Left: Didymos and Dimorphos in original orbit (orbital period labeled)
  - Right: Same system with Dimorphos orbit slightly tighter (orbital period reduced by 32 minutes)
  - Arrows showing velocity changes (exaggerated for visibility)

- Middle panel: Impact sequence (three frames)
  1. DART approaching Dimorphos
  2. Impact moment (bright flash)
  3. Debris plume ejecting material away from asteroid

- Bottom panel: Momentum transfer visualization
  - Bar 1: Spacecraft momentum alone (narrow bar)
  - Bar 2: Actual momentum transferred (3× wider bar)
  - Annotation: "Debris enhancement β ≈ 3"
  - Explanation: "Ejected material carries momentum; net transfer amplified"

**Labels:**
- DART (small cube approaching target)
- Dimorphos (small moon, ~160 meters, labeled)
- Debris plume (radiating arrows)
- Didymos (larger asteroid in background)
- Orbital period before: 12 hours
- Orbital period after: 11 hours 28 minutes

**Caption (80–100 words):**
The DART spacecraft struck Dimorphos, a small moon orbiting the asteroid Didymos, at 6 kilometers per second on September 26, 2022. The spacecraft itself carried momentum of only 3.6 × 10⁶ kg·m/s. But the impact excavated material, and the ejected debris — escaping Dimorphos's weak gravity — carried additional momentum away from the impact site. The net momentum transferred was 3.6 × 10⁷ kg·m/s, roughly ten times larger than the spacecraft alone. This momentum enhancement, called β, depends on the asteroid's composition and structure. The result: Dimorphos's orbital period around Didymos shortened by 32 minutes — the first deliberate change in the trajectory of a celestial object.

**Source/Reference:**
NASA DART mission data; Thomas et al. (2023) — impact analysis.

## Figure 7: Near-Earth Asteroid Discovery Rate Over Time

**Location in chapter:** Concept 3, mechanism section on Spaceguard Survey.

**Type:** Line graph with cumulative total and annual discovery rate.

**Content:**
- X-axis: Year (1990–2025)
- Left y-axis: Cumulative number of NEAs discovered (0–40,000)
- Right y-axis: Annual discovery rate (0–3,000 per year)

- Two curves:
  1. Cumulative total (thick blue line, rising exponentially)
  2. Annual discoveries (thin red line, showing peaks and valleys)

**Annotations:**
- Mark the year 2012 where Spaceguard Survey reached 90% of 1-km+ asteroids
- Vertical line or annotation: "1 km+ target achieved: ~1,000 asteroids"
- Note on acceleration: "Discovery rate accelerated with improved telescopes (2010s onward)"
- Horizontal dashed line at 38,000 (2025 value)

**Caption (70–90 words):**
The discovery rate of near-Earth asteroids has accelerated dramatically since 1990, driven by improvements in automated survey technology and wider sky coverage. By 2012, astronomers had discovered approximately 1,000 asteroids larger than 1 kilometer — the size threshold for global impact effects. This represented 90% of the estimated population of 1-km+ objects. Since 2012, the pace of discovery has continued, focusing on smaller asteroids in the 100-meter to 1-kilometer range. By 2025, more than 38,000 near-Earth asteroids were known. The Rubin Observatory and planned dedicated NEO survey missions will further accelerate discovery of smaller, more frequent impactors.

**Source/Reference:**
NASA NEO Confirmation Page and Minor Planet Center database; Spaceguard Survey statistics.

## Technical Notes for Design

- **All figures should use consistent color scheme:** Primary colors (blue, red, green) for clarity; avoid red-green contrast for accessibility.
- **Scales:** Always include scale bars or reference objects (e.g., Earth diameter for size comparisons).
- **Annotations:** Use clear, sans-serif fonts; avoid excessive jargon without definition on first use.
- **Figure quality:** High-resolution imagery (300 dpi minimum) for print; actual spacecraft/telescope images preferred where available.
- **Credits:** Acknowledge NASA, ESA, and specific mission teams (Rosetta, DART, Spaceguard, etc.).
