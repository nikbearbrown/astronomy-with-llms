**Reference:** Original source material, figure id="OSC_Astro_23_01_Dwarf"

### Figure 10: Change in Brightness of SN 1987A over Time
**Reference:** Original source material, figure id="OSC_Astro_23_03_Change"
**Caption:** Change in the Brightness of SN 1987A over Time and Supernova 1987A Observed with the James Webb Space Telescope
**Purpose:** Show light curve. Early rise (explosion), then sustained brightness (powered by radioactive decay of Ni-56 → Co-56).
**Key features:**
- Day 0: explosion (brightness jumps ~1000x in 1 day)
- Days 1–40: explosion energy dominates
- Days 40+: radioactive heating dominates (Ni-56 half-life ~6 days, Co-56 half-life ~77 days)
- Fade consistent with predicted decay chain
**Pedagogical value:** Quantitative confirmation of element synthesis theory. The light curve shape itself is evidence for what's happening inside.

### Figure 16: Evolution of a Binary System (Type Ia supernova)
**Reference:** Original source material, figure id="OSC_Astro_23_05_Binary"
**Caption:** Evolution of a Binary System
**Purpose:** Show Type Ia mechanism: white dwarf + main-sequence companion. Material flows from companion to white dwarf. Eventually white dwarf approaches Chandrasekhar limit and explodes.
**Frames:**
1. Two stars in orbit (early)
2. Main-sequence star swells to giant
3. Material streams to white dwarf
4. Supernova explosion
**Pedagogical value:** Makes clear that Type Ia requires binary system and mass transfer. Contrasts with Type II (single massive star).

### Table 3: The Ultimate Fate of Stars and Substellar Objects with Different Masses
**Source:** Original source material
**Columns:** Initial Mass | Final State
**Data:**
- < 0.01: Planet
- 0.01 to 0.08: Brown dwarf
- 0.08 to 0.25: White dwarf (He)
