**Added:**

- Specific numbers with context (70 km/s/Mpc, not "about 70")
- Direct mechanism exposition: "Here is why this matters..."

**Restructured:**
- Collapsed 3 sections on expansion models into deep-dive on three independent lines of evidence
- Moved nucleosynthesis from abstract discussion to concrete timeline with worked ratio
- Repositioned dark energy from explanatory footnote to major open question
- Made the H₀ tension a distinct unsolved problem (not buried in "some measurements...")

### Voice moves (Attenborough × Feynman v1.1)

1. **Cold opens within sections:** Each major subsection (A, B, C, III) begins in scene or specific fact, not abstraction.
   - "It is 1964. Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson..."
   - "At 10^-6 seconds, the universe is about 1 trillion Kelvin..."

2. **Scale oscillation:** Frequently zoom in (specific measurement, specific person, specific number) then back out (implication for the whole universe).
   - "A variation that looks like 2.73000 Kelvin in one direction looks like 2.73001 Kelvin in another. Yet these tiny fluctuations contain *everything* we see today..."

3. **Mechanical clarity:** Walk through processes step-by-step without jargon.
   - BBN: "Step one... Step two... Step three..." with names and quantities at each step
   - Recombination explained as "like looking into thick fog → becomes transparent"

4. **Trade-off naming:** Explicitly state what you gain and lose in each scenario.
   - Constant expansion vs. non-constant: gains simplicity, loses age accuracy
   - Flat vs. curved space: flatness works only if total energy density matches prediction

5. **Moral weight through accumulation:** Three independent observations don't prove, but together they make skepticism unreasonable.

## Ideas Harvested for Future Use

### Narrative angles

1. **Accidental discovery as validation:** Penzias and Wilson hunting for noise, finding proof of ancient light. Good setup for "sometimes the universe reveals itself when you're looking for something else."

2. **The singularity as a boundary of knowledge:** Can't go past 10^-43 seconds. Not "we don't know yet" but "equations break down here, knowledge ends." Honesty about limits.

3. **Why the abundances matter:** Light elements in universe's composition are like tree rings or rock strata — a fossil record of conditions. Teaches the reader how evidence *is* read.

4. **Dark energy as the honest unknown:** Not hidden in jargon. Explicitly: we don't know what it is, calculations are off by 10^120, this is a real problem. More interesting than "it exists."

5. **Hubble tension as open frontier:** Two groups of astronomers measuring the same thing, getting different answers at high confidence. Teaching moment about how science actually works.

### Structural moves

1. **Three pillars framework:** Instead of "here's the evidence," present three separate, independent chains of reasoning that all point to the same conclusion. More persuasive than single argument.

2. **Worked example as clarity:** The 1:6 neutron-to-proton ratio → 25% He calculation shows mechanism working. Concrete beats abstract.

3. **Timeline with dual axes:** Temperature and density on right side make it visible *why* the early universe was different. Not just "it was hot" but "how hot and how dense."

4. **Specification move:** Define vague terms before using them.
   - "Dark energy — an unknown component that makes up 68% of the universe's energy density and is causing expansion to accelerate" (not "mysterious energy from the vacuum")
   - "Acoustic peaks — the signature of sound waves in the early universe" (not "characteristic features in the power spectrum")

### Analogies worth keeping

1. **Fog → transparency at recombination:** Fog you can't see through → light can travel. Visceral and correct.

2. **Tree rings of light elements:** Fossil record metaphor. Teaches how to read evidence.

3. **Sound waves frozen in time:** Pressure waves moving through plasma, then frozen at recombination. Visual and mechanistic.

### Unresolved questions made visible

All four open questions identified in source but scattered across sections:
- Dark energy nature (cosmological constant problem: 10^120 discrepancy)
- Hubble tension (67.4 vs. 73 km/s/Mpc)
- Inflation trigger (what field? what initial conditions?)
- Phase transitions in first nanosecond (quark-gluon plasma details)

Each framed not as "we don't know" but as "here's what's unsettled and why it matters."

## Gaps or limits in source material

1. **No mention of primordial gravitational waves:** Inflation predicts a spectrum of relic GWs. Could be next frontier observation.

2. **Limited discussion of matter-antimatter asymmetry:** Why is universe matter-dominated, not matter-antimatter soup? Requires beyond-Standard-Model physics.

3. **No connection to structure formation:** How do CMB acoustic peaks become galaxies? Requires ~100 million years of evolution not covered.

4. **Inflation largely speculative:** Source mentions inflation but doesn't explain why it's needed or what predictions distinguish models. More could be said.

5. **No measurement uncertainty propagation:** When we say H₀ = 70 ± 5 km/s/Mpc, what does that do to age estimate? Source touched this but didn't develop it.

## Lessons for Attenborough × Feynman application

### What works in this voice:

1. **Precision + storytelling:** Specific numbers (13.8 Gyr, 2.73 K, 10^120) are more interesting than approximations, if you tell the story of how we measure them.

2. **Mechanism first, then implications:** "Here's how the universe worked in the first three minutes. Here's what that predicts. Here's what we measure. They match."

3. **Honesty about uncertainty:** "We do not yet know what dark energy is" is stronger than "dark energy remains mysterious."

4. **Visible work:** Show the 1:6 ratio calculation. Don't hide it in "physicists have shown..."

5. **Scale shifts:** Zoom in on Penzias cleaning pigeon droppings. Zoom out to "this is evidence for the entire universe's history."

### What requires care:

1. **Avoiding "awe" without substance:** "The universe is vast and old" is less interesting than "here's precisely how old and why we know it."

2. **Jargon slippage:** Easy to say "acoustic peaks" without explaining what acoustic means. Must define on first use, even in a sophisticated chapter.

3. **False certainty:** Big Bang model is strong but not proven. Source sometimes said "the universe is" when it should say "observations are consistent with." Converted to more careful language.

4. **Teleology:** Avoid "the universe had to have a beginning because..." Better: "if it's expanding now, running backward suggests..." (observation, not necessity).
