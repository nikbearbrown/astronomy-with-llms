# Image Brief — Chapter 11: The Giant Planets

## Chapter-Level Hero Image

**Purpose:** Chapter opening (Voyager 2 at Neptune)

**Content:** Voyager 2 spacecraft approaching Neptune, with the planet filling the frame. Neptune should show distinctive features: pale blue color from methane, white cloud bands, hint of the Great Dark Spot. Background shows the faint Sun and distant stars. The image should convey isolation, distance, and the scale of the achievement (robot cameras capturing the first close-up of a distant world).

**Suggested source:** NASA Voyager 2 archive imagery of Neptune (August 24, 1989)

**What student should notice:** The pale blue color (methane), the cloud structure, the isolation of the spacecraft billions of kilometers from Earth.

## Concept 1 Images

**Figure 1A: Solar Nebula Composition Gradient**

**Purpose:** Show the frost line and compositional structure

**Content:** Schematic diagram of early solar system. Sun on left, expanding outward. Zones should be labeled:
- Inner zone (inner solar system): Hot, metals + rocks only
- Frost line boundary (around 3-4 AU): Sharp transition
- Outer zone (outer solar system): Cold, rocks + ices, then ices + H/He

Arrows should show hydrogen and helium drifting outward, condensed material remaining closer in.

**What student should notice:** The frost line as a compositional boundary; hydrogen/helium escaping the inner solar system but trapped in the outer region.

**Figure 1B: Planetary Masses and Composition by Distance**

**Purpose:** Show the relationship between distance, available material, and planetary mass

**Content:** Bar chart or scatter plot showing:
- X-axis: Distance from Sun (AU) for Mercury through Neptune
- Y-axis: Planetary mass (in Earth masses)
- Color or shape coding: Rocky (red/square), Ice (blue/triangle), Gas (yellow/circle)

The chart should show the sharp drop in available material farther out, yet Jupiter capturing half the gas in the nebula.

**What student should notice:** The mass hierarchy; the types of materials available at different distances.

## Concept 2 Images

**Figure 2A: Interior Structure of Giant Planets**

**Purpose:** Show the layered structure and the differences between types

**Content:** Four cross-sections, side by side:
1. Jupiter: Small rocky core, large region of liquid hydrogen, thin metallic hydrogen shell, gaseous atmosphere
2. Saturn: Slightly larger core (proportionally), liquid hydrogen dominates, small metallic region, atmosphere
3. Uranus: Larger rocky/ice core, ice mantle (water/methane/ammonia), thin H/He layer, atmosphere
4. Neptune: Similar to Uranus, slightly different proportions

Colors should distinguish phases: solid (brown), liquid (blue), metallic (orange/gold), gaseous (light gray). Labels should show approximate pressures and temperatures.

**What student should notice:** The differences between gas giants and ice giants; the contrast between small cores and massive gas envelopes in Jupiter/Saturn vs. larger cores in Uranus/Neptune.

**Figure 2B: Pressure and Temperature Profile in Jupiter**

**Purpose:** Show how pressure and temperature increase with depth

**Content:** Two curves on the same graph, depth (km) on x-axis:
- Blue curve: Temperature (K), rising sharply toward center
- Red curve: Pressure (bars), rising dramatically toward center

Vertical lines should mark phase transitions: gas boundary, liquid boundary, metallic hydrogen boundary, core boundary.

**What student should notice:** The extreme conditions in the interior; the phase transitions at specific pressures.

## Concept 3 Images

**Figure 3A: Jupiter's Cloud Bands and the Great Red Spot**

**Purpose:** Show atmospheric structure and storm systems

**Content:** High-resolution image (Juno or Hubble) of Jupiter showing:
- Light zones (rising air, ammonia clouds) and dark belts (sinking air)
- The Great Red Spot clearly visible
- Smaller white ovals
- Direction of zonal winds labeled (eastward at equator, alternating bands)

**What student should notice:** The regular banding pattern; the size and location of the Great Red Spot; the organized nature of the circulation.

**Figure 3B: Wind Speeds on Giant Planets**

**Purpose:** Compare atmospheric circulation patterns across the four giants

**Content:** Four wind profile graphs stacked vertically, one for each planet:
- X-axis: Latitude (from pole to equator to pole)
- Y-axis: Wind speed (km/h)
- Curves should show the characteristic pattern for each planet: Jupiter's strong eastward equatorial jet, Saturn's even stronger jet, Uranus's weaker pattern, Neptune's variable pattern

**What student should notice:** The speed differences (Saturn fastest, Jupiter strong, Uranus weak); the pattern of alternating eastward and westward jets.

**Figure 3C: Comparison of Great Red Spot, Great Dark Spot, and Earth Hurricanes**

**Purpose:** Show scale and structure of storms across planets

**Content:** Three images side by side:
1. Earth hurricane (visible from satellite, showing spiral structure, eye)
2. Jupiter's Great Red Spot (oval, no eye-like center, surrounded by atmospheric circulation)
3. Neptune's Great Dark Spot (oval, similar to Red Spot)

All three should be at the same scale for size comparison. Labels should indicate diameters.

**What student should notice:** The size difference (GRS and GDS much larger than Earth hurricanes); the structural differences (no clear eye in planetary storms); the visual similarity between GRS and GDS despite different planets.

## Integration Section Image

**Figure 4: Cascade of Initial Conditions to Planetary Outcome**

**Purpose:** Show how composition, structure, and atmosphere flow from initial conditions

**Content:** Flow diagram:
- Top: Distance from Sun → Temperature → Available materials
- Middle: Available materials → Planetary growth history → Final mass
- Lower middle: Final mass → Internal pressure → Phase transitions → Internal heat source
- Bottom: Internal heat + rotation + lack of surface → Atmospheric structure and storms

Arrows should connect each stage. Colors can distinguish the three concept domains: composition (blue), structure (orange), atmosphere (green).

**What student should notice:** The cascade of causality; that each planetary property depends on earlier properties; that distance from Sun sets the cascade in motion.

## Connections Forward Images

**Figure 5: Exoplanet Diversity**

**Purpose:** Preview the fact that Jupiter-sized planets exist at all distances

**Content:** Simple schematic showing habitable zone and a few example exoplanet systems:
- Our solar system (with giant planets in the outer zone)
- A system with a "hot Jupiter" (gas giant close to star)
- A system with multiple Earth-sized planets
- A system with ice giants in the habitable zone

**What student should notice:** That planetary systems vary widely; that our solar system is one solution, not the only one.

## Media Notes

All figures should use consistent color coding throughout:
- Rocky/solid material: Brown/rust tones
- Ice/frozen volatiles: Blue/cyan
- Liquid hydrogen: Medium blue
- Metallic hydrogen: Gold/orange
- Hydrogen/helium gas: Light gray/white
- Clouds: White, orange, red, brown (varied)

High-contrast imagery. Clear labels. Minimal decoration. Every figure should have a clear learning purpose.
