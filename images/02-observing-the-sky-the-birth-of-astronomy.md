# Images & Figures Reference: Observing the Sky

## Figures to Create or Source

### Section 1: The Celestial Sphere and Apparent Motion

**[FIGURE 1: The Celestial Sphere]**
- Central perspective: observer at center
- Celestial poles (north and south) at top and bottom
- Celestial equator as a great circle
- Horizon circle showing zenith and nadir
- A few bright stars (Polaris, Sirius, Vega) labeled at their positions
- Coordinate lines (declination, right ascension) lightly drawn
- Caption: "The celestial sphere is a coordinate system centered on the observer. The sphere rotates (or appears to) due to Earth's rotation. All positions in the sky can be described using angles from the equator (declination) and from a reference direction (right ascension)."

**[FIGURE 2: Star Circles at Different Latitudes]**
- Three panels showing the sky from different observers:
  - North Pole: all stars circle the zenith without rising or setting
  - Mid-northern latitude (40°N): some stars circle the north pole (circumpolar), others rise and set
  - Earth's equator: all stars rise straight up from the east and set straight down in the west
- Horizon line clearly marked in each panel
- Celestial pole shown at its altitude angle
- Caption: "The apparent motion of the sky depends on latitude. At the pole, all visible stars are circumpolar. At the equator, all stars rise and set. At intermediate latitudes, some stars never set while others never rise."

**[FIGURE 3: The Ecliptic and Seasonal Motion]**
- The celestial sphere viewed from above Earth
- The ecliptic drawn as a tilted circle on the sphere
- Celestial equator drawn perpendicular to poles
- The 23.5° angle between them shown clearly
- Sun position marked on the ecliptic at four points (solstices and equinoxes)
- Zodiac constellations labeled along the ecliptic path
- Caption: "The ecliptic is tilted 23.5° from the celestial equator. This tilt is caused by Earth's axial tilt and explains why the Sun's path appears to shift north and south through the year."

### Section 2: Proving Earth's Shape and Measuring Its Size

**[FIGURE 4: Earth's Round Shadow]**
- A lunar eclipse sequence showing the Moon entering Earth's shadow
- Three stages: edge touching, half immersed, fully in shadow
- The shadow boundary always curved (never straight or angular)
- A small inset showing what a flat-disk shadow would look like at various angles
- Caption: "Only a sphere casts a circular shadow from every angle. A disk viewed edge-on casts a line. Earth's shadow on the Moon during a lunar eclipse is always curved, proving Earth is spherical."

**[FIGURE 5: Eratosthenes' Measurement Setup]**
- Map-view showing two cities on a curved Earth
- Syene at the bottom: Sun directly overhead, sunlight straight down the well, no shadow on the ground
- Alexandria to the north: a tall column casting a shadow at 7° from vertical
- Parallel light rays from the Sun arriving at both cities
- The angle between the two rays shown as 7° at Earth's center
- Distance between cities marked as 5,000 stadia
- Caption: "Eratosthenes measured the shadow angle at Alexandria (7°) while the Sun hung directly overhead at Syene. Since the Earth is curved, this 7° angle equals 1/50 of Earth's circumference. Thus the circumference is 50 × 5,000 stadia ≈ 40,000 km."

**[FIGURE 6: Parallax—A Shift in Perspective]**
- Observer's-eye view of a nearby tree and distant hills
- Two observation points shown (separated by distance)
- The tree appears to shift position relative to the hills depending on observer location
- A train passing a background landscape shown as analogy
- Caption: "Parallax is the apparent shift of a nearby object relative to a distant background when the observer moves. The Greek astronomers looked for stellar parallax to prove Earth moves around the Sun but found none—not because Earth doesn't move, but because stars are so distant that the shift is immeasurably small to the naked eye."

**[FIGURE 7: Precession—Earth's Wobbling Axis]**
- A spinning top shown wobbling, its axis tracing a cone over time
- Earth shown the same way: the rotation axis not truly fixed but slowly tracing a cone over 26,000 years
- North celestial pole shown moving relative to the background stars
- Three positions marked: current (Polaris), past (Thuban, 5,000 years ago), future (Vega, 14,000 years from now)
- The precession cycle shown with arrows and time labels
- Caption: "Earth's axis precesses—wobbles slowly—due to gravitational tugging from the Sun and Moon on Earth's equatorial bulge. The wobble takes 26,000 years to complete. As the axis wobbles, the point in the sky nearest the north celestial pole changes."

### Section 3: Ptolemy's Circles and the Heliocentric Turn

**[FIGURE 8: Retrograde Motion Observed]**
- A star field showing Mars' path across the sky over several months
- Mars moving eastward (left to right) normally
- A period where Mars loops backward (westward) clearly marked
- Dates or months labeled on the path
- Caption: "Mars sometimes moves backward (retrograde) against the background stars. The Ptolemaic system explained this with epicycles. The heliocentric system explains it as a perspective effect: Earth, moving faster, overtakes Mars in their orbits."

**[FIGURE 9: Ptolemy's Epicycles—The Model]**
- A large circle (deferent) centered on Earth
- A smaller circle (epicycle) whose center moves around the deferent
- A planet marked on the epicycle at several positions
- The planet's apparent path in the sky (shown on the right) tracing a loop
- Label: "Position A—planet moves with epicycle center, appears eastward"
- Label: "Position B—planet moves opposite to epicycle, appears westward"
- Caption: "Ptolemy's model used nested circles. The planet moved on a small epicycle whose center orbited Earth on a larger deferent. By choosing the right speeds and sizes, the model could reproduce observed retrograde motion."

**[FIGURE 10: Copernicus' Heliocentric System]**
- The Sun at the center
- Planetary orbits drawn in the correct order: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn
- Each orbit shown as a circle (Copernicus retained circular orbits)
- Earth with its moon shown in its orbit
- A line from Earth showing the direction to Mars at two different times, illustrating how retrograde motion emerges
- Caption: "Copernicus placed the Sun at the center. Planets orbit the Sun in the correct order. Retrograde motion emerges naturally: when Earth overtakes a slower planet in its orbit, the planet appears to move backward."

**[FIGURE 11: The Phases of Venus]**
- Three panels showing Venus at different points in its orbit around the Sun
- At each point: Venus' appearance from Earth (crescent, half, full, etc.)
- Earth's position shown, with a line of sight to Venus
- The terminator (day-night line) on Venus clearly marked
- Dates or orbital positions labeled
- Caption: "Venus shows all phases if it orbits the Sun. If Venus orbited Earth, it could only show crescent and near-full phases. Galileo observed the full range of phases, proving Venus orbits the Sun and supporting heliocentrism."

**[FIGURE 12: Jupiter's Moons—Evidence Against Geocentrism]**
- Jupiter shown as a disk with the four Galilean moons around it
- Orbital paths of the moons shown
- Moons labeled with their orbital periods (Io: 1.77 days, Europa: 3.54 days, Ganymede: 7.16 days, Callisto: 16.7 days)
- Earth shown separately in its orbit
- Caption: "Galileo discovered four moons orbiting Jupiter. This proved that not all celestial objects orbit Earth. If the moons can orbit Jupiter while Jupiter orbits Earth (or the Sun), then Earth's Moon could orbit Earth while Earth moves through space."

### Supplementary Diagrams

**[FIGURE 13: The Zodiac Belt]**
- The ecliptic shown as a band around the celestial sphere
- The 12 zodiacal constellations labeled
- The ecliptic offset from the celestial equator by 23.5°
- A small note: "The Sun, Moon, and planets remain within this 18° wide belt"

**[FIGURE 14: Hipparchus' Star Catalog (Sample)]**
- A historical-style table with entries: Star name, constellation, magnitude, declination, right ascension
- 10 example entries (Sirius, Polaris, Vega, Rigel, etc.)
- Magnitudes shown in the ancient system (1st magnitude = brightest)
- Caption: "Hipparchus created the first star catalog with 850 entries, assigning precise coordinates (declination and right ascension) to each star. By comparing his measurements to older observations, he discovered precession."

**[FIGURE 15: Light Rays from Distant vs. Near Sources]**
- Multiple light rays emanating from a nearby source (say, position A), diverging as they spread
- Multiple light rays from a farther source (B), diverging less
- Light rays from infinity, arriving as parallel lines
- Mathematical annotation showing angle decreases with distance
- Caption: "The Sun is so distant that light rays from it arrive at Earth nearly parallel. This is why Eratosthenes could assume parallel rays in his calculation—the Sun is far enough away that the angle is too small to measure directly."
